 Part 1 — Survive the New Battlefield 
 Chapter 1 — The Day Easy Edge Died 
 Story Hook: Eight Wins, One Weekend, and a Brutal Lesson 
 Let me tell you about the most expensive “confidence streak” I’ve seen in this market. 
 A trader I’ll call Minho had what most people dream about: eight straight wins on Polymarket. Not tiny wins either. Clean entries, fast exits, disciplined sizing. He looked sharp. He sounded sharp. By the end of week two he had a little audience in a private chat asking, “What are you buying next?” 
 That’s usually the danger signal, by the way. The moment your PnL starts sounding like a personality trait, risk is already leaking into your process. 
 Minho wasn’t reckless at first. He was methodical. He took event markets with obvious momentum, avoided thin overnight books, and mostly traded around known catalysts—debates,  court rulings, economic prints, campaign schedule leaks, all the usual oxygen in prediction markets. 
 Then one weekend happened. 
 He was long a heavy favorite in a political market that had been grinding upward all week. Price had gone from 0.58 to 0.71 with almost no meaningful pullbacks. Every dip got bought. Everyone in his feed was posting probability charts with arrows pointing up and captions like “easy hold.” 
 If you’ve traded long enough, you know that feeling. It’s the “I finally understand this game” feeling. 
 On Saturday afternoon, liquidity looked healthy on the surface, but inside the order book the support was thinner than it appeared. Spread tightened, yes. But depth at best bid was inconsistent. Large clips started appearing and vanishing. The market looked stable because quote updates were fast, not because conviction was deep. 
 Late Saturday night, a conflicting narrative fragment started circulating—nothing confirmed, but credible enough to trigger uncertainty. Not “the event changed,” just “the certainty changed.” 
 Price slipped from 0.71 to 0.66. Then 0.63. 
 Minho did what many smart people do under pressure: he interpreted new information through old confidence. “This is noise. Great chance to add.” He added. 
 By Sunday morning, liquidity got weird. Market makers widened just a bit. Not panic wide, but enough. Bots started  stepping in front of visible orders, then backing off when aggressive flow came through. Slippage increased. He tried to trim; fills were partial. He tried again lower; got clipped. 
 By Sunday evening the contract touched 0.49. 
 Eight wins erased and then some. 
 The painful part wasn’t being wrong on the event. The painful part was realizing he was trading a market that had changed shape while he was still using last year’s playbook. 
 He kept saying the same line in our review call: “I thought edge was edge.” 
 No. Edge is conditional. 
 What worked in 2024—slow consensus formation, loose spreads, obvious mispricings that stayed open long enough for discretionary traders to exploit—got compressed by 2026. Faster information, better bots, and more efficient copy-trading behavior killed lazy edge. Not all edge. Lazy edge. 
 That weekend wasn’t bad luck. It was the new battlefield making itself clear. 
 If this chapter does one thing for you, it should break the illusion that recent wins prove your model is robust. In these markets, recent wins often prove only that current conditions favored your exact style for a brief window. 
 Now let’s dissect why easy edge died—and where disciplined traders still get paid. 
 
 Concept: Why 2024-Style Edge Decayed by 2026 
 Prediction markets don’t die. Their inefficiencies evolve. 
 In early waves, money is made by showing up with basic structure. In mature waves, money is made by precision, speed, and process quality. 
 By 2026, three forces changed the game: 
 Information velocity exploded Spread compression reduced naive EV Bot pressure turned obvious setups into knife fights 
 Let’s break each one down like a trader, not a theorist. 
 1) Information velocity exploded 
 Back then, you could have a 15–60 minute reaction advantage if you tracked niche sources and cross-referenced faster than average participants. 
 Now? The half-life of “new edge” is tiny. 
 A surprising quote, a filing update, an off-script interview moment—these are parsed, clipped, summarized, and distributed in seconds. Retail traders receive the same headline at nearly the same time. Models scrape source feeds and issue auto-adjust signals immediately. 
 That means your raw information edge is weaker unless: - You interpret context better than consensus, - You know resolution mechanics better than consensus, - Or you execute better than consensus. 
 Notice what’s missing: “I saw the news first.” That’s no longer a reliable business model. 
 2) Spread compression reduced naive EV 
 As participants and bots increased, bid-ask spreads tightened in many popular markets. Beginners celebrate this because tighter spreads feel “professional.” But tighter spreads also mean less room for sloppy entries and exits. 
 In wide-spread markets, you could still make money with rough timing if directional thesis was strong. 
 In compressed markets: - You pay fewer explicit costs, - But your edge is easier to arbitrage away, - And getting in at the wrong micro-moment can erase your expected value. 
 A 3–5 cent error on entry used to be survivable. In many conditions now, it is the difference between positive and negative expectancy. 
 3) Bot pressure increased in obvious zones 
 Bots are not magical; they’re just relentless where rules are clear. 
 They dominate: - Fast quote adjustment, - Spread capture, - Obvious mean reversion windows, - Visible large-order response. 
 Humans still dominate: - Narrative regime change interpretation, - Resolution ambiguity analysis, - Cross-market contextual reading, - Strategic patience. 
 The trap is when humans try to beat bots at bot tasks, while ignoring human advantages. 
 If you keep chasing short-term micro-arb in crowded markets, you’re competing against systems that never sleep, never tilt, and never revenge-trade. 
 That’s not “impossible.” It’s just a low-quality game unless you have your own automation. 
 
 The Real Enemy: Fake Confidence Loops 
 Most drawdowns I review are not from low intelligence. They’re from fake confidence loops. 
 A fake confidence loop looks like this: 
 Win streak happens in favorable regime. Trader attributes results to personal skill only. Position size increases before process improves. Regime shifts; trader interprets shift as temporary noise. Losses trigger reactive entries. Risk concentration spikes when liquidity quality drops. 
 The final insult: after the blow-up, trader says, “I was unlucky.” 
 No. You were under-instrumented. 
 You didn’t have objective regime monitors to tell you market conditions had changed. 
 So let’s fix that. 
 
 Code: Monitor Spread Compression and Volume Spikes 
 You don’t need an enterprise data platform. You need a practical script that tells you when your usual game is no longer your game. 
 The script below does three useful things: 
 Tracks rolling spread behavior Flags abnormal volume spikes Produces a “regime warning score” you can check before placing size 
 You can adapt this to your actual Polymarket data source (API dump, CSV export, or your own feed). For demonstration, we assume a CSV with these columns: 
 timestamp  (ISO datetime) market_id best_bid best_ask last_price volume 
 import  pandas  as  pd 
 import  numpy  as  np 
 from  pathlib  import  Path 
 def  load_data(path:  str )  ->  pd.DataFrame: 
     df  =  pd.read_csv(path) 
 df[ 'timestamp' ]  =  pd.to_datetime(df[ 'timestamp' ], utc = True ) 
 df  =  df.sort_values([ 'market_id' ,  'timestamp' ]).reset_index(drop = True ) 
      return  df 
 def  compute_features(df: pd.DataFrame, 
                      spread_window:  int   =   48 , 
 vol_window:  int   =   96 )  ->  pd.DataFrame: 
      """ 
 spread_window, vol_window are row-based windows. 
 If your data is 15-min bars, 48 ~= 12h and 96 ~= 24h. 
 """ 
     out  =  [] 
 for  market_id, g  in  df.groupby( 'market_id' , sort = False ): 
         g  =  g.copy() 
         g[ 'spread' ]  =  g[ 'best_ask' ]  -  g[ 'best_bid' ] 
         g[ 'mid' ]  =  (g[ 'best_ask' ]  +  g[ 'best_bid' ])  /   2 
 g[ 'spread_bps_of_mid' ]  =  np.where(g[ 'mid' ]  >   0 , g[ 'spread' ]  /  g[ 'mid' ]  *   10000 , np.nan) 
 # Spread compression: current spread vs rolling median 
 g[ 'spread_median' ]  =  g[ 'spread' ].rolling(spread_window, min_periods = 10 ).median() 
         g[ 'spread_compression_ratio' ]  =  np.where( 
             g[ 'spread_median' ]  >   0 , 
             g[ 'spread' ]  /  g[ 'spread_median' ], 
             np.nan 
         ) 
          # Volume spike: z-score on rolling mean/std 
 vol_mean  =  g[ 'volume' ].rolling(vol_window, min_periods = 20 ).mean() 
 vol_std  =  g[ 'volume' ].rolling(vol_window, min_periods = 20 ).std(ddof = 0 ) 
 g[ 'volume_z' ]  =  np.where(vol_std  >   0 , (g[ 'volume' ]  -  vol_mean)  /  vol_std,  0 ) 
          # Price acceleration (simple) 
         g[ 'ret_1' ]  =  g[ 'last_price' ].pct_change() 
         g[ 'ret_4' ]  =  g[ 'last_price' ].pct_change( 4 ) 
          # Regime warning score (heuristic) 
 # Higher when spread is unusually tight AND volume spike occurs with fast price move. 
         g[ 'warning_score' ]  =  ( 
             ( 1   -  g[ 'spread_compression_ratio' ].clip(upper = 1.5 )).clip(lower = 0 )  *   30 
              +  g[ 'volume_z' ].clip(lower = 0 , upper = 6 )  *   10 
              +  g[ 'ret_4' ]. abs ().clip(upper = 0.15 )  *   200 
         ) 
         g[ 'warning_level' ]  =  pd.cut( 
             g[ 'warning_score' ], 
              bins = [ - np.inf,  20 ,  40 ,  60 , np.inf], 
 labels = [ 'normal' ,  'watch' ,  'caution' ,  'high_risk' ] 
         ) 
         out.append(g) 
      return  pd.concat(out, ignore_index = True ) 
 def  latest_dashboard(df_feat: pd.DataFrame)  ->  pd.DataFrame: 
     cols  =  [ 
 'timestamp' ,  'market_id' ,  'best_bid' ,  'best_ask' ,  'last_price' ,

     ] 
     latest  =  (df_feat 
               .sort_values( 'timestamp' ) 
               .groupby( 'market_id' , as_index = False ) 
                .tail( 1 )[cols] 
 .sort_values( 'warning_score' , ascending = False )) 
      return  latest

 Micro-Checklist: Pre-Add Rules for Existing Positions 
 Most damage happens when traders add to losers without fresh evidence. Use this gate: 
 Is there new objective information improving expected value? Has liquidity remained stable enough to exit if wrong? Is warning score not elevated? Is this add within pre-defined risk cap? Would I initiate this same size if I had no existing position? 
 If the last answer is “no,” don’t add. 
 
 Field Drill: The 72-Hour Reset After a Regime Shock 
 When you take a hard loss during a structural shift, do not jump straight back into normal size. Run a 72-hour reset protocol. 
 Hour 0–12  - Flatten any non-essential positions. - Capture screenshots of entry/exit conditions. - Export fills and quote history. - Write one paragraph: what changed in market structure? 
 Hour 12–24  - Reconstruct trade path with timestamps. - Mark where thesis changed versus where execution failed. - Estimate slippage paid versus expected. - Recalculate if trade was positive EV at actual fill prices. 
 Hour 24–48  - Replay same market with half-speed decision simulation. - Identify first point where size should have been reduced. - Identify first point where no-add rule should have triggered. - Build one new pre-trade condition to prevent repeat. 
 Hour 48–72  - Trade micro-size only. - Require green status on regime dashboard. - Take max two trades. - Debrief immediately after each trade. 
 This protocol sounds simple. That’s why it works. Complexity is usually ego hiding from accountability.

 Chapter 2 — How Polymarket Really Prices Reality 
 Story Hook: The 70% Favorite That Lost Anyway 
 A trader named Alex sent me a message after a brutal loss: 
 “I bought at 0.70 because it was basically done. Then it resolved NO. How does a 70% market lose?” 
 That question sounds innocent, but it reveals a massive misunderstanding. 
 A 0.70 price does  not  mean “this event will happen.” It means that  right now , in this specific market, under this specific liquidity profile, with this specific crowd and resolution framework, participants are willing to exchange claims around that level. 
 That’s very different. 
 In Alex’s case, the market had spent days between 0.65 and 0.72. Consensus looked stable. News flow seemed supportive. Sentiment was heavily one-sided. 
 Then a procedural interpretation issue surfaced—small at first, then central. Not “did reality change?” but “how will this market be adjudicated based on wording and source criteria?” 
 Price reacted late because most participants were trading headline narrative, not resolution mechanics. 
 By the time confusion was fully priced, there was no graceful exit. 
 Alex wasn’t wrong about the broad storyline. He was wrong about what he was actually buying. 
 He thought he was buying “real-world truth likelihood.” 
 He was buying a contract with: - Time decay pressure, - Liquidity-dependent execution, - Narrative momentum flows, - And legal-style settlement interpretation risk. 
 That difference is where pros get paid and amateurs donate. 
 Let’s build the right pricing model in your head. 
 
 Concept: Price = Probability + Liquidity + Narrative Momentum + Resolution Risk 
 Most books stop at expected value formulas. Useful, but incomplete. 
 On Polymarket, market price behaves like a blended signal: 
 [ f(P_{event}, L, M, R) ] 
 Where: 
 (P_{event}): estimated event probability (L): liquidity / order book quality (M): narrative momentum (crowd emotion + media flow) (R): resolution risk (rules, wording, source hierarchy, timing) 
 Let’s decode each component in trading language. 
 1) Event probability (the part everyone obsesses over) 
 Yes, this matters. Your model of reality is still core. 
 But two traders can agree on 65% probability and take opposite trades if one sees inflated momentum premium and one sees hidden resolution fragility. 
 So “I think it’s likely” is not enough. 
 You need:  likely at what price, under what path, with what exit quality . 
 2) Liquidity (the part everyone underestimates) 
 Liquidity is not “there are numbers on the screen.” 
 Liquidity means: - Can you enter near intended price? - Can you scale without moving market too much? - Can you exit when thesis changes? 
 A contract can look attractive statistically and still be untradable for your size. If entry and exit quality are poor, your modeled edge leaks as slippage. 
 In prediction markets, slippage isn’t a nuisance; it is a silent tax that can consume your advantage completely. 
 3) Narrative momentum (the part everyone pretends they ignore) 
 Narratives are flow engines. 
 When crowd attention locks onto a storyline, price can overshoot fair probability for longer than rational traders expect. If you short pure hype too early, you can be “correct” and still get buried. 
 Momentum in these markets is driven by: - Shareable headlines, - Influencer amplification, - Partisan identity reinforcement, - Timing near catalysts. 
 Momentum is not stupidity. It is path dependence. 
 If you trade as if momentum should disappear quickly, you’re donating to those who understand crowd timing. 
 4) Resolution risk (the pro-level filter) 
 Resolution risk is your edge amplifier. 
 Questions to ask: - Is wording ambiguous? - Are terms operationally defined? - Which source is authoritative? - What happens in delayed reporting scenarios? - Is there room for technical interpretation that diverges from headline meaning? 
 Many “shocking” outcomes are only shocking to traders who never read rule language deeply. 
 You are not trading philosophical truth. You are trading a contract that resolves under explicit terms. 
 Read them like your bankroll depends on them—because it does. 
 
 Why Consensus Can Be Expensive 
 When everyone sees the same thing, price often includes a convenience premium. 
 A heavily discussed favorite can be overpriced because: - Casual buyers pay up for certainty feeling, - Momentum bots reinforce trend, - Contrarians have limited size appetite, - Event path still has fragility. 
 In those cases, the favorite may still win, but buying at that level is poor business. 
 Let’s be blunt:  You don’t get paid for being right. You get paid for being right at mispriced levels. 
 That’s the entire game. 
 
 Code: Build an Implied Probability Tracker with Moving Deltas 
 Here’s a practical script to track market-implied probability changes and identify overreaction windows. 
 Assume CSV columns: -  timestamp  -  market_id  -  price_yes  (0 to 1) -  volume 
 The script computes: - Rolling changes (15m/1h/4h style via row windows) - Volatility-adjusted delta - Potential overreaction flags when delta is large but follow-through quality is weak 
 import  pandas  as  pd 
 import  numpy  as  np 
 from  pathlib  import  Path 
 def  load_prices(path:  str )  ->  pd.DataFrame: 
     df  =  pd.read_csv(path) 
 df[ 'timestamp' ]  =  pd.to_datetime(df[ 'timestamp' ], utc = True ) 
 df  =  df.sort_values([ 'market_id' ,  'timestamp' ]).reset_index(drop = True ) 
      return  df 
 def  add_probability_signals(df: pd.DataFrame, 
                             w_short:  int   =   4 , 
                             w_mid:  int   =   16 , 
 w_long:  int   =   48 )  ->  pd.DataFrame: 
      """ 
 Example if 15-min rows: 
 w_short=4 (1h), w_mid=16 (4h), w_long=48 (12h) 
 """ 
     frames  =  [] 
      for  mkt, g  in  df.groupby( 'market_id' , sort = False ): 
         g  =  g.copy() 
         g[ 'p' ]  =  g[ 'price_yes' ].clip( 0.001 ,  0.999 ) 
          # Simple deltas 
         g[ 'dp_short' ]  =  g[ 'p' ]  -  g[ 'p' ].shift(w_short) 
         g[ 'dp_mid' ]  =  g[ 'p' ]  -  g[ 'p' ].shift(w_mid) 
         g[ 'dp_long' ]  =  g[ 'p' ]  -  g[ 'p' ].shift(w_long) 
          # Realized local volatility proxy 
 g[ 'ret' ]  =  g[ 'p' ].pct_change().replace([np.inf,  - np.inf], np.nan) 
 g[ 'vol_short' ]  =  g[ 'ret' ].rolling(w_short  *   3 , min_periods = 5 ).std(ddof = 0 ) 
          # Vol-adjusted move strength 
          g[ 'move_strength' ]  =  np.where( 
             g[ 'vol_short' ]  >   0 , 
             g[ 'dp_short' ]. abs ()  /  g[ 'vol_short' ], 
             np.nan 
         ) 
          # Volume context 
 vol_mean  =  g[ 'volume' ].rolling(w_mid, min_periods = 5 ).mean() 
 vol_std  =  g[ 'volume' ].rolling(w_mid, min_periods = 5 ).std(ddof = 0 ) 
 g[ 'volume_z' ]  =  np.where(vol_std  >   0 , (g[ 'volume' ]  -  vol_mean)  /  vol_std,  0 ) 
          # Overreaction heuristic: 
 # big short move + high volume + weak continuation in mid window 
 g[ 'continuation_quality' ]  =  np.sign(g[ 'dp_short' ])  *  g[ 'dp_mid' ] 
         g[ 'overreaction_flag' ]  =  ( 
             (g[ 'move_strength' ]  >   2.5 )  & 
             (g[ 'volume_z' ]  >   1.5 )  & 
              (g[ 'continuation_quality' ]  <   0.01 ) 
         ) 
          # Crowd stress index (custom) 
         g[ 'crowd_stress_idx' ]  =  ( 
             g[ 'move_strength' ].fillna( 0 ).clip( 0 ,  10 )  *   5 
              +  g[ 'volume_z' ].clip( - 1 ,  5 )  *   8 
              +  g[ 'dp_short' ]. abs ().clip( 0 ,  0.20 )  *   120 
         ) 
         g[ 'signal_label' ]  =  np.select( 
             [ 
                 g[ 'overreaction_flag' ], 
                 g[ 'crowd_stress_idx' ]  >=   35 , 
                 g[ 'crowd_stress_idx' ]  >=   20 , 
             ], 
 [ 'overreaction_window' ,  'high_emotion' ,  'watch' ], 
             default = 'normal' 
         ) 
          frames.append(g) 
      return  pd.concat(frames, ignore_index = True )

 Tips: Detect Crowd Overreaction Windows Without Guessing 
 Most traders say “this is overdone” based on emotion. You need process. 
 Use this checklist before fading any extreme move. 
 Overreaction Detection Checklist 
 Step 1 — Context first  - [ ] Was there truly new information, or just repackaged commentary? - [ ] Is the move occurring near a known catalyst window? - [ ] Did related markets move similarly (cross-market confirmation)? 
 Step 2 — Market quality check  - [ ] Is depth real or flickering? - [ ] Is spread still tradable for your size? - [ ] Is volume broad-based or one-way impulse? 
 Step 3 — Resolution sanity check  - [ ] Did any rule interpretation issue emerge? - [ ] Are settlement sources  unambiguous? - [ ] Could timing language create unexpected edge cases? 
 Step 4 — Trade design  - [ ] Entry ladder planned (not single all-in print) - [ ] Max invalidation level pre-defined - [ ] Partial exit plan set before first fill - [ ] Position size reduced in high emotion regime 
 If you can’t check at least 10 of 12 boxes, you’re not trading edge—you’re trading adrenaline. 
 
 Mentor Notes: Three Pricing Mistakes That Drain Accounts 
 Confusing likely with cheap  A likely outcome can still be overbought. Ignoring path risk  Even if final outcome favors you, price path can force bad exits. Treating resolution details as admin work  Resolution is the contract. If you skip it, you’re gambling blind.

 Practical Framework: The “Why This Price, Right Now?” Drill 
 Before every meaningful trade, answer these four prompts in  writing: 
 Probability  — What is my independent estimate and why? Liquidity  — Can I enter/exit without self-sabotaging fills? Momentum  — Is crowd flow helping me, hurting me, or neutral? Resolution  — What exact clause could invalidate my casual interpretation?

 Execution Rule: Separate Thesis Price from Action Price 
 Your thesis might say fair value is 0.64. That does not mean buy at 0.64 automatically. 
 Define: -  Thesis price : your fair estimate -  Action price : level where reward/risk is acceptable given path and liquidity 
 For example, if fair value is 0.64 but momentum is overheated and liquidity unstable, action price might be 0.58–0.60 with staged entries. 
 Beginners blur thesis and action into one number. Pros keep them separate.

 Advanced Concept: Narrative Momentum Has Phases 
 Most traders treat momentum as one thing. That’s a mistake. It has phases, and each phase deserves different tactics. 
 Phase 1: Discovery  - New narrative appears. - Early price move is jumpy and uncertain. - Spread can stay wide while direction forms. 
 What works: small probing entries, fast thesis updates, no ego attachment. 
 Phase 2: Amplification  - Influential accounts and channels repeat the story. - Order flow becomes one-directional. - Pullbacks get bought quickly. 
 What works: trend participation with strict size controls. Don’t fight this phase blindly. 
 Phase 3: Saturation  - Everyone already knows the narrative. - New buyers are lower quality and more emotional. - Price sensitivity to contradictory info increases. 
 What works: reduced momentum chasing, preparation for two-way volatility. 
 Phase 4: Fragility  - Price can break hard on small negative updates. - Liquidity providers widen quickly. - Stops cascade, slippage expands. 
 What works: fast risk reduction, no averaging down without new evidence, opportunistic re-entry only after stabilization. 
 If you can identify phases in real time, you stop asking “Is this up or down?” and start asking “What game am I currently in?”

 Concept: Market Taxonomy That Protects Your Attention 
 Not all Polymarket contracts deserve equal focus. Build a taxonomy so your decision energy goes where expected return per unit stress is highest. 
 Here are the key dimensions. 
 1) Event type 
 Examples: - Elections / political outcomes - Policy / legal decisions - Macro-economic releases - Corporate / product launches - Sports / entertainment outcomes 
 Different event types imply different information quality and manipulation risk. Political markets may have rich data but narrative warfare. Niche entertainment markets may have sparse data and thin books. 
 2) Time horizon 
 Short horizon  (hours to days): fast flow, high reflexivity, execution-heavy Medium horizon  (weeks): thesis + timing balance Long horizon  (months): carrying risk, opportunity cost, regime drift 
 Your style should match horizon. If you hate overnight uncertainty, don’t pretend you’re a long-horizon specialist. 
 3) Manipulability profile 
 Ask: - Can a moderate bankroll visibly push price? - Is participant concentration high? - Are liquidity providers thin enough to be bullied? 
 In highly manipulable markets, technical signals degrade because price can be temporarily “performed” rather than discovered. 
 4) Liquidity profile 
 Beyond top-line volume, inspect: - Spread stability across sessions - Depth consistency at top levels - Slippage under stress - Exit viability during catalysts 
 A market that looks liquid only at calm times may become a trap when you need it most. 
 5) Resolution clarity 
 This is non-negotiable. 
 Score clarity by: - Precision of wording, - Explicit source hierarchy, - Handling of delays/revisions, - Existence of historical disputes in similar contracts. 
 If resolution is vague, model uncertainty tax into your required edge—or skip.

 def  minmax(series: pd.Series)  ->  pd.Series: 
     s  =  series.astype( float ) 
     mn, mx  =  s. min (), s. max () 
      if  np.isclose(mx, mn): 
 return  pd.Series(np.full( len (s),  0.5 ), index = s.index) 
      return  (s  -  mn)  /  (mx  -  mn) 
 def  build_scores(df: pd.DataFrame)  ->  pd.DataFrame: 
     x  =  df.copy() 
      # Liquidity proxy from volume, spread, depth 
 x[ 'volume_norm' ]  =  minmax(np.log1p(x[ 'avg_daily_volume' ])) 
 x[ 'spread_norm' ]  =   1   -  minmax(x[ 'median_spread' ])   # tighter spread => better 
     x[ 'depth_norm' ]  =  x[ 'depth_score' ]  /   10 
     x[ 'liquidity_score' ]  =  ( 0.45   *  x[ 'volume_norm' ]  + 
                              0.35   *  x[ 'spread_norm' ]  + 
                              0.20   *  x[ 'depth_norm' ])  *   100 
      # Core dimensions (0-100) 
 x[ 'resolution_score' ]  =  (x[ 'resolution_clarity' ].clip( 0 ,  10 )  /   10 )  *   100 
 x[ 'data_quality_score' ]  =  (x[ 'data_quality' ].clip( 0 ,  10 )  /   10 )  *   100 
 x[ 'catalyst_score' ]  =  (x[ 'catalyst_structure' ].clip( 0 ,  10 )  /   10 )  *   100 
      # Penalty dimensions 
 x[ 'manipulation_penalty' ]  =  (x[ 'manipulation_risk' ].clip( 0 ,  10 )  /   10 )  *   100 
 x[ 'noise_penalty' ]  =  (x[ 'narrative_noise' ].clip( 0 ,  10 )  /   10 )  *   100 
      # Horizon fit bonus (example: preferred 7-45 days) 
     x[ 'horizon_fit' ]  =  np.select( 
         [x[ 'horizon_days' ]  <   3 , 
 x[ 'horizon_days' ].between( 3 ,  7 , inclusive = 'left' ), 
 x[ 'horizon_days' ].between( 7 ,  45 , inclusive = 'both' ), 
 x[ 'horizon_days' ].between( 46 ,  90 , inclusive = 'both' ), 
          x[ 'horizon_days' ]  >   90 ], 
         [ 40 ,  70 ,  100 ,  75 ,  55 ], 
         default = 60 
     ) 
      # Composite score 
     x[ 'market_score' ]  =  ( 
          0.22   *  x[ 'liquidity_score' ]  + 
          0.20   *  x[ 'resolution_score' ]  + 
          0.18   *  x[ 'data_quality_score' ]  + 
          0.15   *  x[ 'catalyst_score' ]  + 
          0.10   *  x[ 'horizon_fit' ]  - 
          0.08   *  x[ 'manipulation_penalty' ]  - 
          0.07   *  x[ 'noise_penalty' ] 
     ) 
      # Grade buckets 
      x[ 'grade' ]  =  pd.cut( 
         x[ 'market_score' ], 
         bins = [ - np.inf,  45 ,  60 ,  75 ,  85 , np.inf], 
 labels = [ 'D_skip' ,  'C_watch_only' ,  'B_trade_small' ,  'A_core_watch' ,  'S_focus' ] 
     ) 
      # Do-not-trade hard filters 
     x[ 'do_not_trade' ]  =  ( 
         (x[ 'resolution_clarity' ]  <   5 )  | 
         (x[ 'avg_daily_volume' ]  <   1000 )  | 
         (x[ 'median_spread' ]  >   0.08 )  | 
         (x[ 'manipulation_risk' ]  >=   8 ) 
     ) 
     x[ 'final_action' ]  =  np.where( 
         x[ 'do_not_trade' ], 
          'SKIP' , 
 np.where(x[ 'grade' ].isin([ 'S_focus' ,  'A_core_watch' ]),  'TRADE_CANDIDATE' ,  'WATCH' ) 
     ) 
 return  x.sort_values([ 'final_action' ,  'market_score' ], ascending = [ True ,  False ])

 SELECT * 
 FROM market_snapshots 
 WHERE price IS NOT NULL 
 """

 def  build_features(df): 
      if  df.empty: 
          return  df 
 df[ "timestamp_utc" ]  =  pd.to_datetime(df[ "timestamp_utc" ], utc = True ) 
 df  =  df.sort_values([ "market_id" ,  "outcome" ,  "timestamp_utc" ]).copy()

     df[ "spread" ]  =  df[ "best_ask" ]  -  df[ "best_bid" ]

         df.groupby([ "market_id" ,  "outcome" ])[ "ret" ] 
         .rolling( 5 ) 
         .std() 
         .reset_index(level = [ 0 ,  1 ], drop = True ) 
     ) 
      # Volume acceleration 
 df[ "vol_prev" ]  =  df.groupby([ "market_id" ,  "outcome" ])[ "volume_24h" ].shift( 1 ) 
 df[ "vol_accel" ]  =  (df[ "volume_24h" ]  -  df[ "vol_prev" ])  /  df[ "vol_prev" ].replace( 0 , pd.NA) 
      # Simple quality tags 
     df[ "liquidity_tier" ]  =  pd.cut( 
         df[ "liquidity" ], 
         bins = [ - 1 ,  10000 ,  100000 ,  500000 ,  10_000_000_000 ], 
          labels = [ "thin" ,  "moderate" ,  "deep" ,  "very_deep" ], 
     )

 Polymarket doesn’t care if you’re smart. It cares if the price you pay is lower than the probability reality delivers. 
 Calibration is what closes that gap. 
 
 Concept: From Prediction Ego to Probability Discipline 
 Calibration means: when you assign a probability, reality agrees over time. 
 If you make 100 forecasts at 0.70 and only 58 happen, you’re not calibrated. 
 In event trading, poor calibration causes three expensive behaviors: 
 Overpaying  — buying “strong” ideas at no edge. Oversizing  — staking as if uncertainty is lower than it is. Overtrading  — entering marginal setups you should skip. 
 Three Core Tools 
 You need three simple tools, not a PhD. 
 Brier Score  — measures forecast error quality. Calibration Buckets  — checks if predicted bins match outcomes. Edge Thresholding  — trade only when expected value clears your costs and uncertainty buffer. 
 Let’s break this down in practical terms. 
 Brier Score (Plain English) 
 For binary outcomes, Brier is: 
 [ (p - y)^2 ] 
 p : your predicted probability (0 to 1) y : actual outcome (0 or 1) 
 Lower is better. 
 If you predict 0.80 and the event happens ( y=1 ): error is (0.80 - 1)^2 = 0.04. If it fails ( y=0 ): error is (0.80 - 0)^2 = 0.64. 
 It punishes confident mistakes hard. Good. 
 Calibration Buckets 
 Group your predictions into bins: 
 0.50–0.55 0.55–0.60 … 0.85–0.90 
 For each bin, compare: 
 mean predicted probability actual win rate 
 If your 0.70–0.75 bin wins 0.61, you’re overconfident in that region. 
 Edge Thresholding 
 A trade is not “I think it happens.” A trade is “my probability minus price exceeds threshold.” 
 Basic long YES logic: 
 Model probability = 0.64 Market price = 0.58 Raw edge = 0.06 
 Then subtract friction buffer: 
 fees + slippage + model uncertainty = say 0.03 
 Net actionable edge = 0.03. 
 If your minimum threshold is 0.04, this is  no trade . 
 Discipline beats excitement. 
 False Precision Is the Silent Killer 
 Most traders pretend their model output is exact. 
 It isn’t. 
 A predicted 0.67 is usually closer to a range like 0.60–0.72 depending on sample quality. 
 Practical fix: 
 Shrink probabilities toward 0.5 when confidence is low. Require larger edge for lower-data regimes. Reduce size when calibration drift worsens. 
 You’re not trying to look precise. You’re trying to not go broke.

 import  numpy  as  np 
 from  pathlib  import  Path 
 TRADES_PATH  =  Path( "./data/clean/trade_history.csv" ) 
 OUT_BUCKETS  =  Path( "./data/clean/calibration_buckets.csv" ) 
 def  load_trades(path): 
     df  =  pd.read_csv(path) 
 required  =  [ "pred_prob" ,  "outcome" ,  "entry_price" ,  "side" ] 
 missing  =  [c  for  c  in  required  if  c  not   in  df.columns] 
      if  missing: 
          raise   ValueError ( f"Missing columns:  { missing } " ) 
 df  =  df.dropna(subset = [ "pred_prob" ,  "outcome" ,  "entry_price" ,  "side" ]).copy() 
     df[ "pred_prob" ]  =  df[ "pred_prob" ].clip( 0 ,  1 ) 
     df[ "outcome" ]  =  df[ "outcome" ].astype( int ) 
      return  df 
 def  brier_score(df): 
 return  np.mean((df[ "pred_prob" ]  -  df[ "outcome" ])  **   2 ) 
 def  make_buckets(df, step = 0.05 ): 
     bins  =  np.arange( 0 ,  1   +  step, step) 
 labels  =  [ f" { bins[i] :.2f} - { bins[i + 1 ] :.2f} "   for  i  in   range ( len (bins) - 1 )] 
     d  =  df.copy() 
 d[ "bucket" ]  =  pd.cut(d[ "pred_prob" ], bins = bins, labels = labels, include_lowest = True ) 
     summary  =  ( 
         d.groupby( "bucket" , observed = False ) 
          .agg( 
              n = ( "outcome" ,  "count" ), 
              mean_pred = ( "pred_prob" ,  "mean" ), 
              win_rate = ( "outcome" ,  "mean" ) 
          ) 
           .reset_index() 
     ) 
 summary[ "calibration_gap" ]  =  summary[ "win_rate" ]  -  summary[ "mean_pred" ] 
      return  summary

 Concept: Entries, Exits, and Kelly-Lite for Event Markets 
 Execution Principle #1: Enter in Ladders, Not Ego Candles 
 A single all-in click is fragile. 
 Better approach: 
 Define desired total size upfront. Split into tranches (e.g., 40% / 35% / 25%). Place first tranche at current acceptable price. Place additional tranches only if price improves or signal confirms. 
 Laddering solves two problems: 
 reduces regret from immediate adverse move prevents overpaying during emotional spikes 
 Execution Principle #2: Exits Are Planned Before Entry 
 You need at least two exit paths before you enter: 
 Thesis-invalidated exit  (information changed) Price-based risk exit  (market moved against you beyond tolerance) 
 And ideally one profit-taking framework: 
 partial at first target partial at second target runner with tightened risk 
 This prevents the “green PnL to red PnL” tragedy. 
 Execution Principle #3: Kelly-Lite, Never Full Kelly 
 Full Kelly assumes stable probabilities and frictionless markets. 
 Event markets are regime-shifting, noisy, and slippage-prone. 
 Use Kelly-Lite: 
 compute Kelly fraction from edge apply heavy haircut (e.g., 0.25x Kelly) cap by hard per-trade risk limits 
 You are optimizing long-term survival + compounding, not maximizing one trade. 
 Execution Principle #4: Liquidity Is a Position Variable 
 Size should depend on liquidity. 
 Same edge, different liquidity = different executable size. 
 If estimated slippage consumes half your expected edge, your true size is too big. 
 Execution Principle #5: Kill-Switch Is Mandatory 
 You need automatic brakes when conditions degrade: 
 consecutive losses threshold intraday drawdown threshold spread blowout threshold API/data failure threshold 
 No kill-switch = you trade emotionally in worst conditions.

 # file: position_sizer.py 
 from  dataclasses  import  dataclass 
 @dataclass 
 class  SizingConfig: 
     bankroll:  float 
     max_risk_per_trade_pct:  float   =   0.02     # 2% hard cap 
 kelly_haircut:  float   =   0.25               # quarter Kelly 
     min_edge_to_trade:  float   =   0.04 
     slippage_buffer:  float   =   0.01 
      fee_buffer:  float   =   0.01 
 def  kelly_fraction_binary(p_win, price): 
      """ 
 Approximation for YES share priced [0,1]. 
 If YES resolves true: payoff per 1 share = 1 - price 
 If false: loss per 1 share = price 
 """ 
     p  =  p_win 
     q  =   1   -  p 
     b  =  ( 1   -  price)  /  price   # net odds equivalent 
      if  b  <=   0 : 
          return   0.0 
      # standard Kelly for odds b: f* = (bp - q)/b 
     f  =  (b  *  p  -  q)  /  b 
      return   max ( 0.0 , f) 
 def  recommended_position(config: SizingConfig, calibrated_prob, market_price): 
     gross_edge  =  calibrated_prob  -  market_price 
 net_edge  =  gross_edge  -  config.slippage_buffer  -  config.fee_buffer 
      if  net_edge  <  config.min_edge_to_trade: 
          return  { 
              "take_trade" :  False , 
              "reason" :  "edge_below_threshold" , 
              "net_edge" :  round (net_edge,  4 ), 
              "notional" :  0.0 , 
         } 
 kelly  =  kelly_fraction_binary(calibrated_prob, market_price) 
     kelly_lite  =  kelly  *  config.kelly_haircut 
     hard_cap  =  config.max_risk_per_trade_pct 
     final_fraction  =   min (kelly_lite, hard_cap) 
     notional  =  config.bankroll  *  final_fraction 
      return  { 
          "take_trade" :  True , 
          "gross_edge" :  round (gross_edge,  4 ), 
          "net_edge" :  round (net_edge,  4 ), 
          "kelly_raw" :  round (kelly,  4 ), 
          "kelly_lite" :  round (kelly_lite,  4 ), 
          "final_fraction" :  round (final_fraction,  4 ), 
          "notional" :  round (notional,  2 ), 
     } 
 if   __name__   ==   "__main__" : 
     cfg  =  SizingConfig(bankroll = 25000 )

      print (result)

 Tips: Slippage, Fills, and Discipline in Thin Books 
 Tip 1: Stop Treating Mid Price as Your Fill Price 
 Mid is a fantasy in thin markets. 
 Track real fill outcomes: 
 expected price at order average filled price realized slippage per share 
 Build your edge thresholds around realized friction, not optimistic assumptions. 
 Tip 2: Time-of-Day Liquidity Matters 
 Some books look tradable only during specific windows. 
 Map your fill quality by time block: 
 Asia hours EU overlap US session pre-resolution rush 
 If your worst slippage cluster is predictable, stop trading that window unless edge is exceptional. 
 Tip 3: Use “If Not Filled, Not Forced” Rules 
 A lot of drawdowns begin with emotional chasing. 
 Set rules: 
 If tranche 1 doesn’t fill within X minutes and spread widens, cancel. If price runs away without you, do not market-chase beyond predefined tolerance. 
 Missed trades are tuition-free. Forced trades are expensive. 
 Tip 4: News Volatility = Wider Safety Margins 
 Around major catalysts: 
 raise min edge threshold reduce tranche size widen invalidation awareness lower total portfolio heat 
 During chaos, precision drops. Risk protocol must tighten. 
 Tip 5: Define Portfolio Heat Cap 
 Even with single-trade limits, correlated exposure kills. 
 Set total active risk cap (example: 8–12% of bankroll at risk across positions). 
 If new trade pushes heat above cap, you must: 
 reduce other exposure, or pass. 
 You are running a portfolio, not a collection of isolated opinions.

 A lot of traders think hedging means fear.

 Concept — Cross-market hedging and pseudo-arb setups 
 Let’s define three tools. 
 1) Correlation trading 
 Two markets move together over time (positive or negative). You exploit dislocations when one runs ahead. 
 Example logic: - Market A (upstream catalyst) moves first. - Market B (downstream consequence) usually follows with lag. -  If A spikes and B barely moves, you may buy B (or reduce A exposure) depending on context. 
 2) Hedge overlays 
 You hold a directional view but cap damage using related contracts. 
 Example: - You’re long outcome X in Market A. - You buy protective exposure in Market C that tends to benefit when X thesis fails. - You accept smaller max upside for far better survival odds. 
 3) Synthetic positions 
 You combine multiple contracts to create a payoff shape that no single market gives. 
 Think like this: - Contract 1 = thesis expression - Contract 2 = timing hedge - Contract 3 = volatility insurance 
 This is where human creativity crushes one-market autopilot. 
 Critical warning: Correlation is a guest, not a spouse 
 Correlation breaks during stress, especially around: - Rule reinterpretations - Source credibility shocks - Platform liquidity fractures - New participant waves (whales, campaigns, media blasts) 
 Never hedge with blind faith. Hedge with ongoing measurement.

 def  rolling_corr(px: pd.DataFrame, a:  str , b:  str , window:  int   =   72 )  ->  pd.Series: 
      # For 5m bars, 72 = 6 hours 
      return  px[a].pct_change().rolling(window).corr(px[b].pct_change()) 
 def  rolling_beta(px: pd.DataFrame, y:  str , x:  str , window:  int   =   72 )  ->  pd.Series: 
     ry  =  px[y].pct_change() 
     rx  =  px[x].pct_change() 
     cov  =  ry.rolling(window).cov(rx) 
     var  =  rx.rolling(window).var() 
     beta  =  cov  /  var.replace( 0 , np.nan) 
      return  beta 
 def  divergence_score(px: pd.DataFrame, leader:  str , lagger:  str , lookback:  int   =   12 )  ->  pd.Series: 
      """ 
 Measures whether lagger underreacted vs leader recently. 
 Positive score => lagger may be behind 
 """ 
     r_lead  =  px[leader].pct_change(lookback) 
     r_lag  =  px[lagger].pct_change(lookback) 
      return  (r_lead  -  r_lag).fillna( 0 ) 
 def  suggest_hedge_notional(primary_notional:  float , beta_val:  float , hedge_confidence:  float   =   0.8 )  ->   float :

 hedge_notional = primary_notional * beta * confidence

      if  np.isnan(beta_val):

 return   max ( 0.0 , primary_notional  *   abs (beta_val)  *  hedge_confidence)

 How to interpret in plain English 
 High corr + stable beta : hedge can reduce variance effectively. High divergence + stable narrative logic : potential lag catch-up trade. Low corr regime : don’t force a hedge that no longer behaves. 
 Tips — Hedge drift and resolution mismatch warnings 
 Most hedge failures are not math failures. They’re  assumption failures . 
 Common hedge mistakes 
 Ignoring resolution mismatch   Two markets can look similar but resolve on different triggers. Over-hedging into dead upside   You flatten risk so much that even when right, payoff is weak. Static hedge in dynamic regime   Beta changes. Liquidity changes. Narrative changes. Hedging correlation, not causality   Temporary co-movement is not a structural relationship. 
 Practical hedge protocol 
 Pre-trade:  Define base thesis market Define hedge instrument and  why  it should offset Define invalidation for hedge itself In-trade:  Recompute rolling corr/beta at fixed intervals Track hedge slippage separately from thesis PnL Adjust only on schedule (avoid emotional over-tuning) Post-trade:  Did hedge reduce drawdown as planned? Did hedge cut too much upside? Keep / modify / delete this pairing from your playbook 
 Synthetic position templates 
 Template A: Conviction + Brake  - 70% notional in core thesis market - 30% in offset market with opposite sensitivity - Use when confidence is high but event path is noisy 
 Template B: Trigger Ladder  - Starter in leading market - Add in lagging market only after confirmation threshold - Reduces premature exposure 
 Template C: Tail Guard  - Core directional trade + small outlier hedge - Helps survive resolution surprises

 Chapter 9 — Risk Ops: Preventing the One Trade That Kills You 
 Story Hook — +40% month erased in one overconfident bet 
 Traders rarely blow up from being dumb every day. 
 They blow up from being undisciplined one day. 
 Dae had a brilliant month. Up 40%. Clean reads. Better execution. Strong confidence. Then came the trade he “couldn’t lose.” 
 He broke three rules in 20 minutes: 
 Doubled planned size after first favorable tick Ignored widening spread because “news is obvious” Refused partial exit because “this runs more” 
 Then liquidity thinned. A contradictory update hit. Book slipped hard. He froze. 
 By session end, most of the month was gone. 
 Here’s the brutal lesson: skill without risk ops is temporary luck. 
 You don’t need a better prediction model first. You need a system that keeps you from self-destruction when confidence spikes. 
 Concept — Drawdown controls, stress testing, liquidity shock protocol 
 Risk ops is not a spreadsheet exercise. It is a  behavioral containment system . 
 The four layers of survival 
 Position-level risk   Max loss per trade Max slippage tolerance Exit plan if liquidity vanishes Day-level risk   Daily loss cap Daily max number of trades Cooldown period after two consecutive impulse trades Week-level risk   Weekly drawdown brake (size reduction) Strategy quality review before re-scaling Portfolio-level risk   Concentration cap by narrative/theme Correlation cluster cap Tail event contingency rules 
 If one layer fails, another catches you. 
 Hard stops vs soft stops 
 Hard stops : non-negotiable, system-enforced  e.g., stop trading after -3R day Soft stops : discretionary warnings  e.g., reduce aggression during unusual spread widening 
 Most people have only soft stops. That’s just a promise to yourself made by your calm brain, broken by your excited brain. 
 Scenario stress framework 
 For any major position, run three scenarios: 
 Base case : expected path Adverse case : moderate contradiction + lower liquidity Shock case : resolution ambiguity + liquidity gap + sentiment reversal 
 If shock case threatens account integrity, your size is too big. Period. 
 Liquidity shock protocol 
 When book quality collapses: 
 Stop adding immediately. Shift from “best price” mindset to “controlled exit” mindset. Slice exits; avoid one panic market order. If market is broken and thesis still valid, de-risk elsewhere (hedge, reduce correlated exposure). 
 You are not paid for being brave in a broken book.

 class  RiskLimits:

 def  fetch_positions()  ->  List[Position]: 
      # Replace endpoint

     r.raise_for_status()

 def  position_unrealized_pnl(p: Position)  ->   float : 
     direction  =   1   if  p.side.lower()  ==   "long"   else   - 1 
 return  direction  *  p.qty  *  (p.mark_price  -  p.entry_price) 
 def  compute_drawdown(equity: pd.Series)  ->  pd.Series: 
     peak  =  equity.cummax() 
     dd  =  (equity  -  peak)  /  peak.replace( 0 , pd.NA) 
      return  dd.fillna( 0 ) 
 def  summarize_theme_exposure(positions: List[Position])  ->  Dict[ str ,  float ]: 
     notionals  =  {} 
      total  =   0.0 
      for  p  in  positions: 
         n  =   abs (p.qty  *  p.mark_price) 
 notionals[p.theme]  =  notionals.get(p.theme,  0.0 )  +  n 
         total  +=  n 
      if  total  ==   0 : 
          return  {k:  0   for  k  in  notionals} 
      return  {k: v  /  total  for  k, v  in  notionals.items()} 
 def  run_risk_checks(positions: List[Position], equity_df: pd.DataFrame, limits: RiskLimits): 
     alerts  =  [] 
 total_unrealized  =   sum (position_unrealized_pnl(p)  for  p  in  positions) 
     dd_series  =  compute_drawdown(equity_df[ "equity" ]) 
 latest_dd  =   float (dd_series.iloc[ - 1 ])  if   not  dd_series.empty  else   0.0 
      theme_exposure  =  summarize_theme_exposure(positions) 
      # Liquidity checks 
 thin_positions  =  [p  for  p  in  positions  if  p.liquidity_score  <  limits.min_liquidity_score] 
      if  thin_positions: 
         alerts.append( 
 f"THIN_LIQUIDITY:  { len (thin_positions) }  positions below liquidity score  { limits . min_liquidity_score } " 
         ) 
      # Concentration checks 
      for  theme, pct  in  theme_exposure.items(): 
          if  pct  >  limits.max_theme_concentration_pct: 
             alerts.append( 
 f"CONCENTRATION: theme= { theme } , exposure= { pct :.1%}  >  { limits . max_theme_concentration_pct :.1%} " 
             ) 
      # Drawdown regime checks 
      if  latest_dd  <=   - limits.drawdown_hard_stop_pct: 
         alerts.append( 
 f"HARD_STOP: drawdown= { latest_dd :.1%}  breached hard stop  { - limits . drawdown_hard_stop_pct :.1%} . Halt trading." 
         ) 
 elif  latest_dd  <=   - limits.drawdown_reduce_threshold_pct: 
         alerts.append( 
 f"REDUCE_SIZE: drawdown= { latest_dd :.1%} . Cut size by 30-50% until recovery." 
         ) 
      return  { 
 "total_unrealized_pnl" :  round (total_unrealized,  2 ), 
          "latest_drawdown" :  round (latest_dd,  4 ), 
 "theme_exposure" : {k:  round (v,  4 )  for  k, v  in  theme_exposure.items()}, 
          "alerts" : alerts, 
     } 
 def  main():

 ap.add_argument( "--max-theme" ,  type = float , default = 0.35 ) 
 ap.add_argument( "--min-liq" ,  type = float , default = 0.25 )

 Concept — Daily checklist, weekly review, monthly optimization

 #!/usr/bin/env python3 
 """ 
 journal_kpi_summary.py 
 Reads trade journal CSV and outputs: 
 - Core KPIs 
 - Setup-level expectancy 
 - Rule-break cost 
 - Weekly trend summary 
 """ 
 import  argparse 
 import  pandas  as  pd 
 def  load_journal(path:  str )  ->  pd.DataFrame: 
     df  =  pd.read_csv(path) 
 df[ "date" ]  =  pd.to_datetime(df[ "date" ], utc = True , errors = "coerce" ) 
 numeric_cols  =  [ "entry" ,  "exit" ,  "qty" ,  "pnl" ,  "fees" ,  "slippage_bps" ,  "hold_minutes" ] 
      for  c  in  numeric_cols: 
          if  c  in  df.columns: 
 df[c]  =  pd.to_numeric(df[c], errors = "coerce" ).fillna( 0 ) 
      if   "rule_break"   in  df.columns: 
          # normalize to bool 
 df[ "rule_break" ]  =  df[ "rule_break" ].astype( str ). str .lower().isin([ "1" ,  "true" ,  "yes" ,  "y" ]) 
      else : 
         df[ "rule_break" ]  =   False 
      if   "setup_type"   not   in  df.columns: 
         df[ "setup_type" ]  =   "unknown" 
      return  df.dropna(subset = [ "date" ]) 
 def  core_kpis(df: pd.DataFrame)  ->   dict : 
     total_trades  =   len (df) 
     gross_pnl  =  df[ "pnl" ]. sum () 
 total_fees  =  df[ "fees" ]. sum ()  if   "fees"   in  df.columns  else   0 
     net_pnl  =  gross_pnl  -  total_fees 
     wins  =  (df[ "pnl" ]  >   0 ). sum () 
      losses  =  (df[ "pnl" ]  <   0 ). sum () 
     win_rate  =  wins  /  total_trades  if  total_trades  else   0 
 avg_win  =  df.loc[df[ "pnl" ]  >   0 ,  "pnl" ].mean()  if  wins  else   0 
 avg_loss  =  df.loc[df[ "pnl" ]  <   0 ,  "pnl" ].mean()  if  losses  else   0 
     expectancy  =  df[ "pnl" ].mean()  if  total_trades  else   0 
 avg_slippage  =  df[ "slippage_bps" ].mean()  if   "slippage_bps"   in  df.columns  else   0 
      return  { 
          "total_trades" :  int (total_trades), 
          "gross_pnl" :  round ( float (gross_pnl),  2 ), 
          "fees" :  round ( float (total_fees),  2 ), 
          "net_pnl" :  round ( float (net_pnl),  2 ), 
          "win_rate" :  round ( float (win_rate),  4 ), 
 "avg_win" :  round ( float (avg_win),  2 )  if  pd.notna(avg_win)  else   0 , 
 "avg_loss" :  round ( float (avg_loss),  2 )  if  pd.notna(avg_loss)  else   0 , 
 "expectancy_per_trade" :  round ( float (expectancy),  2 ), 
 "avg_slippage_bps" :  round ( float (avg_slippage),  2 ), 
     } 
 def  setup_expectancy(df: pd.DataFrame)  ->  pd.DataFrame: 
     g  =  ( 
         df.groupby( "setup_type" , as_index = False ) 
         .agg( 
             trades = ( "pnl" ,  "count" ), 
             total_pnl = ( "pnl" ,  "sum" ), 
             avg_pnl = ( "pnl" ,  "mean" ), 
             win_rate = ( "pnl" ,  lambda  x: (x  >   0 ).mean()), 
             avg_slippage_bps = ( "slippage_bps" ,  "mean" ), 
             rule_breaks = ( "rule_break" ,  "sum" ), 
         ) 
         .sort_values( "avg_pnl" , ascending = False ) 
     ) 
      return  g 
 def  rule_break_cost(df: pd.DataFrame)  ->   dict : 
     rb  =  df[df[ "rule_break" ]  ==   True ] 
     clean  =  df[df[ "rule_break" ]  ==   False ] 
     rb_avg  =  rb[ "pnl" ].mean()  if   len (rb)  else   0 
     clean_avg  =  clean[ "pnl" ].mean()  if   len (clean)  else   0 
     penalty  =  clean_avg  -  rb_avg 
      return  { 
          "rule_break_trades" :  int ( len (rb)), 
          "clean_trades" :  int ( len (clean)), 
 "rule_break_avg_pnl" :  round ( float (rb_avg),  2 )  if  pd.notna(rb_avg)  else   0 , 
 "clean_avg_pnl" :  round ( float (clean_avg),  2 )  if  pd.notna(clean_avg)  else   0 , 
 "estimated_penalty_per_rule_break" :  round ( float (penalty),  2 )  if  pd.notna(penalty)  else   0 , 
     } 
 def  weekly_summary(df: pd.DataFrame)  ->  pd.DataFrame: 
     wk  =  (

 Chapter 7 Drill Pack — Bot-Aware Execution Reps 
 Drill 1: Reaction-speed trap audit (7 days)

 Drill 2: Two-minute no-click rule 
 When a major headline hits, start a two-minute timer. 
 During those two minutes: - no market orders, - no emotional scaling, - no social feed doom-scroll. 
 Instead: 1. Confirm source quality. 2. Check whether market already repriced most of the move. 3. Review your prewritten scenario notes. 4. Decide whether this is still your setup, or already bot territory.

 Chapter 8 Drill Pack — Correlation and Synthetic Position Mastery

 Drill 1: Relationship map (weekly)

 Four Real-World Style Case Studies (Playbook in Action) 
 Below are compact case studies modeled on patterns you will repeatedly face in prediction markets. Use them as rehearsal scripts. 
 Case Study 1: The obvious headline trap 
 Context  - A major account posts a “breaking” update. - Primary  market jumps from 0.54 to 0.67 in under 90 seconds. - Social feeds explode with certainty. 
 Amateur move  - Immediate market buy at 0.68 because “everyone knows now.” - Adds at 0.70 when first retrace is small. - No spread check, no source hierarchy, no invalidation. 
 Professional move  1. Starts two-minute no-click timer. 2. Verifies source chain: - original statement, - secondary confirmation, - procedural relevance to actual resolution. 3. Checks order book health and chase risk label. 4. Finds signal-delay profile implies bots already repriced first burst. 5. Waits for pullback and only enters partial size where risk/reward recovers. 
 Outcome logic  - Headline was directionally correct but partially stale relative to price action. - Market mean-reverted 6 points before grinding higher. - Amateur got poor basis and emotional stress. - Professional got better basis with smaller stress and cleaner add zones. 
 Lessons  - Being right on event does not guarantee good entry. - In headline minutes, price often reflects urgency premium. - Discipline converts “late but correct” into tradable expectancy. 
 Case Study 2: Correlation that works—until it doesn’t 
 Context  - Market A (upstream policy signal) and Market B (downstream enactment likelihood) have moved together for two weeks. - Rolling correlation has been 0.72. - A spikes on a speech; B lags. 
 Trade plan  - Long B as lag-catch setup. - Small hedge in C (uncertainty proxy) in case procedural resistance appears. 
 Mid-trade change  - New procedural detail reduces feasibility of A translating into B. - Correlation drops intraday. - Beta estimate becomes unstable. 
 Amateur response  - “Correlation always catches up.” - Doubles B size. - Treats regime break as temporary annoyance. 
 Professional response  1. Runs scheduled hedge drift check. 2. Sees correlation deterioration and narrative causality crack. 3. Cuts B size, keeps small core, increases caution. 4. Rewrites scenario tree before next action. 
 Outcome logic  - A stays elevated due to sentiment. - B underperforms due to procedural friction. - Blind correlation faith loses. - Causal monitoring preserves capital. 
 Lessons  - Correlation is not a contract. - Regime awareness beats static historical relationships. - Hedge adjustment cadence prevents panic overtrading. 
 Case Study 3: Liquidity shock during conviction trade 
 Context  - Trader holds strong thesis with reasonable entry basis. - Market appears liquid in calm hours. - Unexpected contradictory update hits during low-depth window. 
 What happens next  - Spread widens abruptly. - Top-of-book size disappears. - Price gaps through common stop zones. 
 Amateur response  - Tries to “win back control” by adding near the gap. - Uses market orders in thin book. - Converts manageable drawdown into severe loss. 
 Professional response  1. Stops all adding immediately. 2. Switches objective from “perfect exit” to “damage control.” 3. Scales out in slices across minutes, not one panic print. 4. Reduces correlated exposures to free risk budget. 5. Logs event for future liquidity shock rehearsal. 
 Outcome logic  - Both traders had similar thesis quality. - Difference came from shock protocol, not prediction. 
 Lessons  - Liquidity is part of risk, not background noise. - Stress rehearsals reduce catastrophic improvisation. - Survival behavior is a competitive skill. 
 Case Study 4: The 30-day sprint transformation 
 Starting profile  - Decent intuition, inconsistent execution. - High activity, low process adherence. - Frequent post-hoc rationalizations in journal. 
 Intervention  - Implements daily checklist, weekly KPI review, and monthly one-change rule. - Tags every trade by setup type and rule compliance. - Uses journal parser weekly without fail. 
 Week-by-week progression 
 Week 1  - No major performance jump. - Big discovery: 35% of trades are low-quality impulse entries. 
 Week 2  - Removes worst setup category. - Trade count drops, stress drops, slippage improves. 
 Week 3  - Tightens ladder rules and partial exits. - Win rate barely changes; expectancy improves. 
 Week 4  - Rule-break count cut in half. - Net PnL rises mainly from fewer self-inflicted mistakes. 
 30-day result  - Not a miracle moonshot. - A stable, repeatable operating model with better downside control. 
 Lessons  - You don’t need to predict more. You need to leak less. - Process consistency compounds faster than motivational bursts. - The best month is the month you can replicate. 
 Implementation FAQ (Rapid Fire) 
 Q: How many markets should I watch daily?  A: Usually 3–5 priority markets is enough. More often creates fake productivity and poorer attention quality. 
 Q: Should I fully automate entries?  A: Only after you have a robust discretionary process and evidence by setup type. Start with alert automation, not blind execution automation. 
 Q: What’s a good first hard stop?  A: A daily loss cap you can actually respect (often -2R to -3R). Better a modest hard stop obeyed consistently than an ambitious one ignored. 
 Q: How do I know if I’m over-hedged?  A: If your best thesis days barely pay while complexity rises, hedge likely suppresses too much upside. Re-evaluate hedge notional and trigger logic. 
 Q: What metric should I obsess over first?  A: Rule-break count. It’s the cleanest indicator of whether your process is real.
