 Preface 
 There are a lot of great books about artificial intelligence. Some of them explain in elaborate technical detail how the engineering, statistics, and computer science of AI work. Others focus on one of the many problems that AI might bring about, including algorithms that don’t work as promised, algorithms that discriminate, algorithms that turn against their human masters, and algorithms that automate away human workers. Still others discuss the problematic foundations on which AI is built, from the surveillance of internet users and the exploitation of click workers to the environmental destruction wrought by data centers and mining operations. These are all important issues. 
 What has not been presented is a unified framework for understanding how AI will proceed in a society that is shaped by power and inequality. This book aims to do that. Amid all the breathless debates about technical details, new possibilities, and social problems, I argue that the key issue that unites all the problems of AI is the choice of objectives that AI pursues, and the question of who controls these objectives. Control of these objectives is determined by control over the resources that are required for building AI—data, computational infrastructure, technical expertise, and energy. I call these resources the  means of prediction .

I come from a background in mathematics and statistics, as well as economics, and much of my research concerns questions of methodology. I draw on this background when reviewing the current state of AI in this book. 
 In some of my research, I work to move beyond the technical questions of statistics and machine learning to understand these fields in their social, political, and economic context. In a separate line of research, I work on economic inequality and what policy can do to enable a full and secure life for everyone. I study pilot job-guarantee and basic-income programs. 
 I believe that researchers have an obligation to contribute to a society where we collectively debate and decide our own future, rather than leaving questions of technology and policy to technocrats and experts. It is in this spirit that the present book aims to participate in a broad public debate about the future of AI.

 Introduction 
 Are you scared of artificial intelligence? You should be—if we are to believe some popular stories about the threat of AI and the coming conflict between humans and machines. 
 According to these stories, AI will attain superhuman capabilities and will start to self-improve. It will threaten humans in the name of self-preservation, and it will ultimately become an existential risk to humanity. These stories, told in movies, literature, industry, and academia, touch on our deepest and most fundamental fears. We fear to lose our livelihoods and to descend into poverty. We fear to lose our autonomy and to be controlled by incomprehensible and malign actors indifferent to our fates. We fear to lose our life. And we fear—even worse—that the survival of those we love, and the survival of humanity at large, might be threatened. On top of all that, we fear AI as an inscrutable force that is headed our way. Its arrival is inevitable, and its impact seems beyond anyone’s control. 
 These stories of AI, the stories of the existential conflict between humans and machines, are repeated over and over in Hollywood and in Silicon Valley. But these stories do not help us make good decisions—in technology or in politics. They make it seem like there is only one possible direction for the development of AI, and that society cannot do anything about it. These stories also obfuscate who wins and who loses as AI develops. This obfuscation prevents the public debate from focusing on the real issues at stake, and it prevents  people from doing anything about them. Doing nothing serves the interests of those who benefit from keeping things as they are. 
 This book gives a different perspective on AI and society. Contrary to the popular stories, the progress of AI is not fate but rather a product of human choices. The key conflicts are not between humans and machines but between different people. The answer to these conflicts is shared democratic control of AI and of the objectives that it pursues: Those impacted by algorithmic decisions need to have a say over these decisions. 
 To provide a foundation for such a different perspective, this book first offers an unfettered way of thinking about AI in the way that machine learning experts think about it and understand it. In doing so, it shows the limits of AI, and it shows how AI can be made to work for all people. Doing so requires revising the stories that we tell ourselves about humans and machines.

 The Story of Humans Versus Machines 
 In the classic film  2001: A Space Odyssey , which was released in 1968, a spaceship headed to Jupiter is equipped with an onboard computer named HAL 9000. Over time, this computer becomes a deadly antagonist of the astronauts on the ship. After an apparent computer error, several crew members try to switch HAL off. In the name of safeguarding the secret mission of the spaceship, HAL kills the crew members. Eventually, however, the astronaut Dave Bowman succeeds at deactivating HAL, ignoring the computer’s desperate pleas to stop. 
 In  The Terminator  (1984), the conflict between humans and a self-preserving AI is taken up a notch, and the conflict becomes a question of survival for the entire human species. 
 Many of these same tropes appear in movies such as  The Matrix  (1999),  I, Robot  (2004),  Transcendence  (2014),  Ex Machina  (2015),  M3gan  (2022),  The Creator  (2023), and others. They reflect a particular fear of AI, one amplified by visible figures from the tech industry in this century: that we are headed toward a conflict between humans and machines. Elon Musk argued at the Bletchley Park AI summit that AI is “one of the biggest threats to humanity” and that, for the first time, we are faced “with something that’s going to be far more intelligent than us.” Sam Altman, of OpenAI, has claimed that generative AI could bring about the end of human civilization, and  that AI poses a risk of extinction on a par with nuclear warfare and global pandemics. 
 In academia, this story has also found some resonance. The philosopher Nick Bostrom has written extensively about the existential risks of AI for humanity, and the possibility of an intelligence explosion, where AI keeps improving itself once it has reached human level. The computer scientist Stuart Russell, together with his collaborators at the Center for Human-Compatible Artificial Intelligence at the University of California, Berkeley, has emphasized the so-called  alignment problem —that is, the problem of making machine objectives align with human objectives. 
 Another dystopian story, which is almost equally scary, holds that AI won’t kill us, but it will render human workers obsolete, inevitably leading to mass unemployment and social unrest. A 2023 Goldman Sachs report, for instance, claimed that generative AI might replace three hundred million full-time workers in Europe and the United States. 
 The story told in Hollywood and in Silicon Valley tends to feature a heroic conflict between a man (it is usually a man) and a machine—Dave Bowman and HAL 9000 in  Space Odyssey , Kyle Reese and the Terminator, Nathan and Ava in  Ex Machina , or Sam Altman and the AI-caused extinction of humanity. The academic version of the story, as told by computer scientists, also tends to feature a man and a machine, where there is a value-alignment problem of the machine (that is, a mis-specified objective) or a bias of the machine relative to its objective.

 What the Old Story Misses 
 This book will focus on the key issues that the story of man versus machine misses: Technology is not fate. Just as people make technology, people decide how it is used and what interests it serves. These decisions are made over and over again as AI is developed and deployed. AI is, furthermore, ultimately not that complicated. How AI works can be understood by anyone. The real conflict is not between a human and a machine but between the different members of society. And the answer to the various risks and harms of AI is public control of AI objectives through democratic means. 
 AI is, at its core, automated decision-making using  optimization . That means that AI algorithms are designed to make some measurable objective as large as possible. Such algorithms might, for example, maximize the number of times that someone clicks on an ad. AI therefore requires that somebody picks the objective—the  reward —that is being optimized. Somebody must, quite literally, type into their computer: “This is the measure of reward that we care about.” 
 The important question, then, is who gets to pick the  objectives  of AI systems. We live in a capitalist society, and in such a society the objectives of AI are typically determined by the owners of capital. The owners of capital control the  means of prediction  that are needed for building AI—data, computational infrastructure, technical  expertise, and energy. More generally, the objectives of AI are determined by those with social power, whether that is in the criminal justice system, in education, in medicine, or in the secret police forces of autocratic surveillance states. 
 One domain in which AI is deployed in society is the workplace. AI is used in robotized Amazon warehouses, in the algorithmic management of Uber drivers, and in the screening of job candidates by large companies. AI is also used in consequential domains outside the workplace, including the filtering and selection of Facebook feeds and of Google search results, where the objective is to maximize ad clicks. A third domain is predictive policing and the incarceration of defendants awaiting trial based on the prediction of crimes that they have not committed yet. Perhaps most devastatingly, AI is also deployed in warfare; it was, for instance, used to decide which family homes to bomb in Gaza beginning in 2023. 
 Of course, a good number of researchers and critics have warned of the dangers of using AI in these consequential domains. Joy Buolamwini, a computer scientist at MIT Media Lab, has written extensively on the dangers of inaccurate and racially biased facial recognition systems. Ruha Benjamin, a sociologist at Princeton, has emphasized that AI can replicate and reinforce existing social inequalities in domains such as education, employment, criminal justice, and health care. In a similar vein, Timnit Gebru, a computer scientist writing during her time working at Google, warned of the dangers of large language models acting as stochastic parrots, which repeat language patterns without understanding, and in doing so replicate the biases embedded in their training data. Meredith Whittaker, currently the president of the Signal Foundation, has criticized the political economy of the tech industry, where AI is used by powerful actors in ways that can entrench marginalization. Kate Crawford, professor at the University of Southern California and cofounder of the AI Now Institute, has emphasized the nature of AI as an extractive and exploitative industry. 
 Amid these overlapping critiques, each focused on a different aspect  and pitfall of AI, it is challenging to formulate a systematic way of thinking about AI in society. One possible unifying perspective is provided by computer science. Computer scientists are trained to view most problems as optimization problems. In this context, optimization involves finding the decision that makes a given reward as large as possible, given limited computational resources and limited data. 
 The computer science perspective has informed much of the public discourse around AI safety and AI ethics, especially regarding topics such as fairness or value alignment: “If there is something wrong, then there must be an optimization error.” In this view, the issue is simply that an action was picked that failed to maximize the specified objective. This perspective does not get to the heart of the problem in most cases, however, because it doesn’t engage with the choice of the objective itself. 
 I argue that instead of optimization errors, it is conflicts of interest over the control of AI objectives that are the central issue. When AI causes human harm, the problem is usually not that an algorithm did not perfectly optimize. The problem is that the objective optimized by the algorithm is good for the people who control the means of prediction—people such as Jeff Bezos, founder and former CEO of Amazon, and Mark Zuckerberg, founder and CEO of Meta—but not good for the rest of society. 
 This understanding changes how we should think about possible solutions to the problems of AI. How do we address AI ethics and AI safety if the underlying problems are with the parties that set the objectives for AI? How do we choose these objectives in a way that serves the public rather than just a powerful minority? This book will make the case that the solution for the issues of AI ethics and safety can only be  democratic control . Democratic control is not limited to democratically elected national governments; collective democratic decision-making can exist on many levels, including the workplace, the nation state, and the global level. 
 The challenge, of course, is that democracy is difficult. The democratic  control of a new technology like AI requires public deliberation, and such public deliberation might seem impossible considering the view held by many (and reinforced by the tech industry) that AI is very complicated. 
 But despite all the technical jargon, and despite the breathless chase after the newest innovations, the basic ideas of AI are not that complicated, and not that new, and they can be understood by all of us. No matter who you are, don’t let anyone tell you that you are not the “type” to understand AI. This book will start with a discussion of how AI works, which in turn functions as a foundation for understanding its political stakes and likely path in the future.

 What This Book Does 
 This book provides a concise overview of how AI works—what it does and doesn’t do—as a means toward understanding its capabilities and its political and economic impact. The following is a glimpse at the book’s structure. It begins with the book’s greatest challenge: translating a science that AI companies would like us to believe is impossibly complicated. 
 How AI Works 
 Many of the questions that need to be solved when building AI reflect ancient and fundamental questions about how it is possible to learn from experience and how to act successfully in the world. 
 To get started, we will need to agree on what we are talking about. What is  artificial intelligence ? “Intelligence” is a notoriously loaded term, and public perceptions of AI have oscillated from “an obscure academic niche” to the very broad “everything related to data,” and back to the narrow category of “large language models.” This book will take an intermediate stance, in between these very broad and very narrow definitions. Following the standard technical treatment of AI, this book will define AI as “the construction of systems for  automated decision-making  to maximize some measurable reward.” This definition is more specific than “anything to do with data,”  but at the same time, it includes a lot more than just  large language models . This definition gives us a framework to talk about the many socially consequential settings where AI is used. 
 There are many branches of the field of AI.  Figure 1  provides a taxonomy of some of these branches. 
 Figure 1  A taxonomy of AI 
 Until the end of the 1980s,  expert systems , based on hand-coded human knowledge, were the dominant approach in AI. But most modern AI is based on  machine learning . Machine learning uses data and statistical methods to build automated decision-making systems. To understand AI, we thus need to understand machine learning. One branch of machine learning is  supervised learning , where the objective is to predict some outcome as accurately as possible. Many learning problems are prediction problems: In facial recognition, an individual’s identity is predicted based on an image. In large language models, the next word is predicted based on the preceding words. In the hiring of job candidates, future performance is predicted based on candidate characteristics. In social media feeds, ad clicks are predicted based on user data. 
 Prediction is tricky, because it needs to navigate between two opposite dangers,  overfitting  and  underfitting . When prediction overfits, it develops superstitions: It erroneously extrapolates random occurrences  of the past into the future. If prediction underfits, it is stubborn and refuses to learn: It sticks to beliefs despite contradictory evidence. 
 AI algorithms might try to find the right balance between overfitting and underfitting by checking how well they can predict the data on hand, on which they were trained. The problem with this approach is that hindsight is easy—it is too easy to predict what we already know. This approach would therefore induce the algorithm to overfit the data of the past. Instead, for accurate evaluation, algorithms need to  split the data —that is, check predictions on data the algorithm has not used yet. This approach is called  cross-validation . Supervised learning relies on picking the model that does best, according to this cross-validation criterion. 
 One method for making predictions uses  deep learning , a type of supervised learning that is based on training  neural nets . Neural nets allow for modeling very complicated relationships. They have been extremely successful in recent years for prediction problems where data are abundant, such as image recognition or language modeling. Neural nets, and in particular  transformers  (a special kind of neural net), have also been central for  generative AI —AI that produces text, images, or other media. This includes large language models, where the goal is to predict the most likely word to come next. (Large language models power applications such as ChatGPT.) Generative AI also includes image generation, where images are predicted based on text labels, as well as video generation. 
 Supervised learning is a form of  offline learning , which describes learning based on data that are given. In  online learning , data are collected over time, and what data the algorithm observes might depend on the actions it has previously taken. ( Online  here has nothing to do with being on the internet; it refers to learning over time.) Humans face the same situation: We only see the consequence of an action if we take it. Doctors, for example, can only learn whether a new drug works if they prescribe this drug to some patients. Successful  AI, in online settings, needs to both  explore  new things, by experimenting, and to  exploit  what it has learned, in just the right balance.  Multi-armed bandit  algorithms are designed to do exactly that: They aim to find exactly the right balance between experimenting with different options and doing what seems best based on what has been learned from actions they have previously tried.  Reinforcement learning  goes one step further than multi-armed bandit algorithms. Reinforcement learning builds algorithms that learn to  plan  by learning how likely it is that different states of the world are favorable down the road. 
 The Politics and Economics of AI 
 The technical overview of AI in this book provides the core ingredients that are necessary to fulfill humanity’s ancient dream—to become a Prometheus, a Demiurge, a creator of another intelligence, of an  artificial intelligence . But beware! Is this coveted creation of AI what we hoped for? Or will it instead be a source of new dangers for society? Will we lose control of our Frankensteinian monster, our golem; will we be like the sorcerer’s apprentice in the movie  Fantasia ? 
 To confront this question about the dangers of AI, we need to think about how to evaluate the impact of AI on society, about who gets to steer AI, and whether their direction is desirable for society. This is a wider lens than most conversations about AI take, as it entails moving beyond the standard framework of machine learning and the optimization of a single given objective. In the narrow view of AI, based only on the logic of optimization, all problems can be solved by better engineering, and their solutions are best left to the experts. What this optimization framework misses, however, is the social nature of the problems caused by AI. 
 If an algorithm selecting what you see on social media promotes outrage, thereby maximizing engagement and ad clicks, the problem is not an optimization error: Promoting outrage is good for profits  from ad sales, even if it is bad for society. If another algorithm choosing whom to invite for a job interview systematically rules out candidates who are likely to have family-care responsibilities outside the workplace, that is also not an optimization error: It is good for profits but bad for future parents or those taking care of elderly relatives. If an algorithm setting health insurance rates screens out people who are likely to develop chronic health problems or disabilities, that is not an optimization error either. It is good for profits but bad for people who need health care. 
 Rather than understanding everything as an optimization problem with a single objective, it is critical to recognize that we live in a world where different people have different objectives. We live in a world of inequality, distributional struggles, and conflicting value systems. Accordingly, the most important question for AI must be, Who gets to pick the objective? By asking this question, we transcend the ideological obfuscations that are promoted by the beneficiaries of the status quo. 
 The next part of this book is thus dedicated to thinking about what makes a good society and how to get there. This problem needs to be discussed to address the questions of what objectives AI should maximize and who should choose them. In grappling with this problem, we once again confront ancient and fundamental questions. 
 This book assumes that a society is good if it is good for the people in it. This may sound trivial, but it has important implications for thinking about AI. Assuming that a society should be good for people leads to a set of questions that need to be answered, to evaluate the social impact of AI. First, who are the people whose welfare matters? Second, how do we measure their welfare? And third, how do we consider trade-offs between the welfare of different people that are affected by AI—in other words, how do we assess  social welfare ? AI is beneficial to a society if it maximizes social welfare. 
 The question of how to measure individual welfare is particularly thorny. One might focus on  opportunities  or on  outcomes . One might  focus on objectively measurable standards of well-being or, instead, on the economic concept of subjective  utility —that is, on what individuals would choose if they had a choice. These distinctions imply different ways to evaluate the effects of AI on individuals, and they matter especially when thinking about the use of AI for social good. 
 Constructing AI for a better society requires not only knowing where we want to go but also how to get there. And it requires knowing who will get us there: Who are potential agents of change in a system that appears so inevitable? Potential agents of change are individuals or organizations who can align AI objectives with social welfare. They need to have the interests, values, and capacity to do so. 
 In the field of AI ethics, the focus is often on convincing AI engineers and their managers to be nice. But engineers and managers, independent of their personal qualities, are constrained by the requirement of profit maximization that governs private corporations; their individual agency and accountability are secondary to the fact that they must do a job. If not corporate engineers, who else has the capacity to change the course of AI? The objectives of AI are chosen by those who control the resources to build AI, what this book calls the  means of prediction . These resources include data, computational infrastructure, technical expertise, and energy. Against this backdrop, agents of change need to have strategic leverage over the actors who control the means of prediction to be able to effect change. Leverage can take many forms, from potential strikes and consumer boycotts to bad press and litigation to regulation and legal constraints. The pool of potential agents of change is surprisingly large: unions, consumer advocates, journalists, judges, policymakers, and politicians. 
 Agents of change not only need leverage—they also need values and interests that motivate them to move AI in the right direction. The development of such values might be hindered by ideologies, and effective collective action to change the direction of AI is undermined by ideological obfuscation of the issues at stake. By representing  problems as optimization errors, or as fights between man and machine, attention is diverted from distributional conflicts. By representing problems as technical issues that are best left to experts, rather than as social choices that require collective deliberation, the possibility of democratic governance is denied. By painting the development of AI as inevitable, change is forestalled, and the status quo is preserved. 
 AI in Society 
 Equipped with this background, we will discuss how to regulate algorithms in the next part of the book. We will revisit debates around problem domains including value alignment and AI safety, privacy and data property rights, automation in the workplace, fairness and algorithmic discrimination, and explainability of algorithmic decision. 
 The objectives of AI are determined by those who control the means of prediction, particularly the large datasets needed to train AI. Because data are the basic resource that AI builds on, data privacy and data ownership are core issues. Computer science has developed a systematic framework for discussing privacy,  differential privacy . When an algorithm satisfies differential privacy, whether an individual was included in the underlying data cannot be determined from the algorithm’s output: the individual could be in there, but the algorithm won’t tell. As a legal counterpart to this notion of privacy in computer science, privacy legislation might give individuals actual  property rights  over their data. But the problem with this focus on individual property rights, from an economic perspective, is that this focus misses the point of machine learning: Machine learning is focused on patterns across individuals not individual data. To use the language of economics, learning is all about the  externalities —the implications of data collection for third parties—in the sense that one person’s data can be used to make predictions about another  person. When there are pervasive externalities, individual property rights cannot prevent social harms. Because machine learning is all about externalities, differential privacy can be maintained while machine learning proceeds largely unimpeded, including all its harms and benefits. For this reason, collective governance is the only possible solution to regulate data collection in a way that addresses the harms and benefits of AI. 
 A form of democratic governance is also needed in the AI-augmented workplace. The introduction of new technologies in the workplace typically enables a company to produce more with fewer inputs. But this does not mean that everybody gains. It is quite possible that a new technology increases the  average output  per worker while at the same time decreasing wages for some or all of those workers. The reason is that wages reflect the  additional output  that comes from hiring an additional worker not the average output across all workers. The former might decrease, while the latter increases. Because of this, it is possible to have economic growth without shared prosperity. AI, in particular, might be used in this way,  automating  away a range of occupations, while making its owners richer. Again, this is not destiny. How new technologies are used is a choice. If workers (or workers’ organizations) have a say in the choice of objectives for AI in the workplace, via some form of worker representation in decision-making at the company, then growth with shared prosperity is much more likely. 
 AI drives inequality not only by shifting labor demand but also via  algorithmic discrimination  and  bias . To identify algorithmic bias, both economists and computer scientists often point to a deviation from (profit) maximization. For example, if a man is chosen instead of a woman by a hiring algorithm, this is interpreted as bias only if it would have been more profitable to hire the woman—but not otherwise. This perspective, which only sees a problem if profits are not optimized, again relies on an ideological obfuscation: It purports to reflect the interests of disadvantaged groups while in fact advocating for the maximization of profits. This is not to say that algorithmic  bias isn’t real and insidious. But to identify and quantify it, one must assess who stands to gain or lose from the introduction of an AI system in a given setting, and whose objectives are being maximized, rather than just asking whether there was a deviation from profit maximization. 
 If there is a concern that algorithmic decisions might be biased, one possible response is to require explanations of consequential algorithmic decisions, such as hiring decisions. In this spirit, discussions of  explainability  and  accountability  for automated decisions focus on individual recourse, where individuals might have a right to an explanation why a particular decision was made. To allow for recourse, a common suggestion is to use simple algorithms, which make it possible to explain decisions. But simplicity is a moving target. Instead of just explaining individual decisions to allow for individual recourse, we should focus on explaining AI systems and the objectives they maximize. These are necessary steps toward their democratic governance. 
 Concluding the book, in  part V , I summarize some of the big questions that were discussed throughout—how to learn from observation, how to successfully act in the world, what makes a good society, who might get us there, and what that implies for the ethics and politics of AI. 
 This book makes the case that democratic governance of AI is critical to ensure that its uses are broadly beneficial. But this book does not provide a detailed blueprint for the democratic institutions that are needed to implement such governance. I end the book with some thoughts on different forms that democratic governance might take, going beyond the limitations of electoral and direct democracy, and involving arrangements such as  sortition  and  liquid democracy . This brings us to the end of the book, but it is also only a beginning: Democratic governance of AI needs to be fought for and put into practice. This will be a task for all of us. 
 Without further ado, let us now get started.

 Part II 
 How AI Works 
 When to call a machine  intelligent  is a notoriously vexing question. Attempts to address it tend to open a philosophical can of worms, which leads to difficult debates about terms such as  understanding  and  consciousness . Alan Turing, one of the founders of the field of computer science, proposed a solution that aimed to sidestep these philosophical questions. In a fatefully influential paper published in 1950, he suggested turning the question of machine intelligence into an empirical question by considering an  imitation game : Can a machine, in a natural language conversation, deceive a human conversation partner? Can the machine make the human believe that they are talking to another human, rather than to a machine? If the answer is yes, then the machine is deemed intelligent. This goal of imitating humans has been a guiding light for the field of AI since its inception, for better or worse. 
 Turing’s imitation game is a curious mirror image of earlier attempts to deceive humans into believing they were interacting with a machine, when in fact they were interacting with a human. Most famous of these was the Mechanical Turk, first presented to Maria Theresa, the empress of Austria, in 1770. The Mechanical Turk was supposed to be a chess-playing automaton that competed with human players. It continued to impress the public for over eighty years, defeating opponents such as Napoleon Bonaparte and Benjamin  Franklin. Unbeknownst to the public of the day, the automaton was in fact secretly operated by human expert chess players who were hidden inside the machinery. 
 This historical curiosity gave its name to a modern web platform, Amazon Mechanical Turk (MTurk). On this platform, human workers are hidden behind a digital interface and perform all kinds of more or less repetitive data work, with compensation set at a piece rate. Just as in the case of its historical namesake, behind this platform there are humans disguised as a machine. One important task of workers on this platform, and of click workers more generally, is to produce data that are used to train AI algorithms—so that these algorithms can pretend to be humans. Since the public release of AI tools such as ChatGPT, it now appears that workers on MTurk have in turn started using these AI tools to fulfill their tasks more quickly, while pretending that the tasks are completed by humans. 
 To summarize: Machines are being trained to behave so that they seem like humans, by using data from a platform where humans are presented as machines, but the humans behind this platform have started to substitute themselves with machines pretending to be human.

 What Is Artificial Intelligence? 
 The goal of this chapter is to define  artificial intelligence . The question of what constitutes artificial intelligence is closely tied to the equally loaded question of what makes humans intelligent. A key point to recognize regarding the notion of  intelligence  is that human capacity, like machine capacity, has many dimensions. Most people are quite good at performing some tasks but not so good at performing other tasks. Any attempt to reduce human or machine capacity to a one-dimensional notion of intelligence neglects this central fact. Once we recognize this fact, it does not make sense to say that one person is more intelligent than another, or that a machine is more (or less) intelligent than a human. All humans, and all machines, have their strong suits and their weaknesses. 
 Attempts to reduce intelligence to a one-dimensional hierarchy, where we can say who is more or less intelligent, have a long and problematic history. Such attempts feature centrally in some of the stories that have been told to justify social domination across the last two centuries: colonial domination, social inequalities based on gender or race, and the inequality between so-called skilled and unskilled workers. These stories typically hold that some group or other of people is naturally more intelligent than the rest and therefore naturally destined to rule society. These stories require that intelligence is one-dimensional. If, instead, we were to recognize that you  are better than me at some tasks, but I am better than you at other tasks, then we would have no argument why either of us should rule the other, or why either of us deserves a better life. But even today, and in particular among people working in the tech industry, you do not need to search long to find justifications of inequality in this spirit. These justifications of inequality and arguments against egalitarian policies are based on the presumption that social hierarchies correspond to a natural order of one-dimensional intelligence. Intelligence is in turn equated to merit or deservingness. 
 The assumption that there is a one-dimensional degree of intelligence is also core to stories of an impending AI explosion. According to these stories, AI will be improved by humans until it reaches human intelligence. After that point, AI will continue to improve itself at an exponentially increasing rate. Just like the idea of intelligence-based social hierarchies, the idea of a point in time where AI reaches human levels of intelligence only makes sense if intelligence is one-dimensional. In reality, of course, computers have long been better than humans at many tasks, most notably at computing things, but they are still worse than humans at many other tasks. For AI to iteratively improve itself, AI would need to take over the tasks that AI engineers are currently responsible for. It is not clear whether any of the tasks that computers are good at are related to the tasks of AI engineers. More importantly, it is not clear at all whether better engineering is the limiting factor for further improvements in AI—as opposed to data availability, availability of computational infrastructure, and energy. Arguably, improvements in AI have been due to the increased availability of these three factors, rather than due to the increased intelligence of AI engineers. 
 The rest of this book will avoid the notion of intelligence, but we cannot avoid having a definition of  artificial intelligence . AI is first and foremost a field of research and of engineering. Public understanding of what AI is about has been subject to wild swings. Public understanding has oscillated from AI being an obscure academic niche  field, to it encompassing everything related to data, to it narrowly referring exclusively to large language models. 
 This book will take an intermediate position, following leading textbooks on the subject. According to these textbooks, the goal of AI, as a field of research and of engineering, is to construct  rational agents . A rational agent chooses actions with the objective of maximizing some notion of a measurable  reward  or (this is just a sign flip) minimizing some notion of a measurable  loss . AI algorithms thus solve  decision problems . (Engineers use the term  problem  as in “homework problem”—something to solve, not something unfortunate.) 
 Decision Problems 
 Understanding how AI works, and for whom, thus requires looking at the decision problems that AI is designed to solve. A close look at these decision problems is useful for several reasons. It clarifies the default story of AI optimization errors, because asking AI to solve decision problems means asking AI to optimize something. It also makes explicit the ingredients needed to build AI—that is, the  means of prediction , the ownership of which determines who controls AI. And, finally, it foregrounds the objectives and action spaces of AI that would necessarily be the subject of democratic control if AI were to serve the common good. 
 There are four key ingredients to a decision problem. There is, first, the set of actions from which the algorithm can choose. This set of possible actions varies widely between different applications of AI. A robot might choose which way to turn or how to move its arm. A social media algorithm might choose what ads to display. A large language model might choose what sentence to print next. A hiring algorithm might choose which job candidate to invite for an interview. 
 There is, second, the objective of the AI algorithm—that is, the definition of reward or loss. The reward defines what the goal of an  AI algorithm is: What is it trying to achieve? The reward must be something that can be measured and quantified. The AI algorithm receives a sequence of numbers corresponding to its reward. AI algorithms are designed to make these numbers as large as possible. Again, there are many possibilities for the definition of reward. An industrial robot’s reward might be based on successfully assembling some parts on a conveyor belt. A social media algorithm’s reward might be based on the number of ad clicks achieved, weighted by the price of these ads. A language model’s reward might be based on its success at predicting the next word in a random sentence on the internet. A hiring algorithm’s reward might be based on the share of invited candidates who are ultimately hired. 
 There is, third, any prior knowledge that is built into the algorithm. The robot might have some hard coded knowledge of Newtonian physics. The social media algorithm might have built-in constraints on which ads are appropriate for which demographic groups. A language model might draw on linguistic understanding of syntax, and of grammar more generally. The hiring algorithm might have constraints based on job requirements, such as necessary qualifications. 
 There are, fourth and finally, the data on which the algorithm can draw. The robot might have visual and touch-based sensors. The social media algorithm might have user click histories and ad characteristics. The language model might have large amounts of text scraped from the internet. The hiring algorithm might have data on past applications and hiring decisions. 
 Specifying the ingredients of a decision problem—action space, rewards, prior knowledge, data—is one of the most important tasks in designing AI systems. And any of these ingredients can be a source of social conflict around AI. 
 This concept of a decision problem is shared by several different fields of research. It provides a foundation for both statistics and AI, but it is also central to the field of economics. Economists often assume  that people maximize some notion of  utility —basically, the amount of value or benefit they derive from their actions. (This concept of utility shows up again later in this book, in the discussion of social welfare and of frameworks to evaluate the consequences of AI.) Economic utility is very similar to the idea of a reward in AI, in that both specify what a machine or a human is trying to achieve. A key difference between AI and economics is that AI is about trying to make machines behave optimally, whereas economists often assume that people do behave optimally. Put differently, in AI, the idea of rationality is used in a  prescriptive  manner: What should an algorithm try to achieve? How can the algorithm be made to maximize its reward? 
 Economists assume that people do what they want to do, given the circumstances that they face—that is, people maximize their utility. Put differently, the notion of rationality is used in a  descriptive  way in economics. The assumption that people maximize utility seems strange to most noneconomists. By itself, however, this assumption does not say much. This assumption does not have any implications that we could observe. Assuming that people do what they want to do doesn’t imply anything, since people could want to do anything. 
 In addition to its descriptive use, the notion of utility also has a  normative  meaning, if it is assumed that utility is equal to welfare. This assumption says that whatever people would choose is what is best for them. I return to this assumption when discussing social welfare later. A key point for now is that different people have different objectives in economics, while decision theory in statistics and AI is typically concerned with a single given objective. 
 The common ground between AI and economics is important. It allows us to link our discussion of how AI works, drawing on the insights of computer science, to our discussion of the political and economic effects of AI, drawing on the insights of economics. For computer science, building AI is about maximizing some given notion of reward, or objective. Economists studying inequality and policy  are concerned with conflicting objectives across people. These conflicting objectives might be about distribution: Who gets how much of the pie? They might also be about values: What kind of pie should we have? Different people want different things, and they therefore want AI to pursue different objectives. This, then, raises the critical question of who gets to choose the AI algorithm’s notion of reward. This question is at the heart of social conflict around AI. 
 Machine Learning 
 Different approaches in the field of AI put very different weights on data versus prior knowledge. For several decades from its inception, the field was dominated by approaches that tried to encode human knowledge or expertise into the construction of AI systems. In one version of this approach, so-called  expert systems  would combine their encoded human knowledge and make logical derivations as needed in a given situation. A chess program might, for instance, incorporate rules and guidance from human chess masters about openings, middle games, and end games. This approach to AI was time consuming and limited by the number of available human experts. 
 Since the start of the twenty-first century, however, AI has been almost completely dominated by a different approach, known as  machine learning , which has been remarkably successful. Rather than hard coding human expertise into the system, the idea is to learn as much as possible from data, using statistics. In the chess example, rather than hard coding the recommendations of human players, the algorithm might learn which positions are most likely to lead to a victory in the game. This might be done using databases of existing games of expert players. But, even better, new games could be generated by letting the computer play against itself. This latter approach is only limited by the available computational resources. 
 The switch to machine learning has proved extremely successful. First, it has allowed AI to build on two centuries of insights in  statistical methodology. The problems of machine learning are not always the same as the problems of classical statistics, but they are quite close, which means that the field of AI can build on all the work done by statisticians. Second, and even more importantly, the switch to machine learning allowed AI to scale, almost arbitrarily, in many domains. There is a limit to how many human experts you can find who are available to devise rules of automation for playing chess. But there is almost no limit to how many games you can let the computer play against itself. And, similarly, there is almost no limit to how much text or image or video data can be found on the internet. 
 It is this combination, then, that explains many of the astonishing successes of AI in the twenty-first century: Machine learning could build on the methodology of statistics, on the advance in raw computational capacity that had been progressing for a while, and, most importantly, on the vast amounts of data that were made available by the spread of the internet and by the establishment of online surveillance regimes by the big tech companies. 
 The increased availability of computational power and of data has allowed methods and ideas to scale that had been around for a while. Artificial  neural networks  were studied in a relatively unsuccessful academic niche since the 1960s. This dramatically changed in the early 2000s. Based on scaling of data and computational power, the methods of  deep learning  in neural networks reached a critical point. The performance of these machine learning methods suddenly beat all other approaches to the problems of AI. 
 This development has an important implication. In settings where AI is based on large-scale machine learning, modern AI is synonymous with the world of big data and big computational infrastructure. Control of AI, then, is very much tied to the control of these means of prediction. And the means of prediction, in turn, determine who gets to choose the objectives that are maximized by AI algorithms. This central theme is important for the discussion of the political economy of AI later in this book. 
 These dynamics, where successes in machine learning are driven  by the scaling of data and computational infrastructure, also have implications for future advances in AI. (Attempting to predict the future is generally an unwise exercise, but we can nonetheless speculate on some of its outlines.) It appears likely that computational capacity, while expensive at the scale needed, will not be the limiting factor for AI. In other words, a lack of computational capacity will not prevent the further development of machine-learning-based AI for the foreseeable future. 
 Availability of data, however, might be such a limiting factor, in ways that vary widely across domains. On one extreme, where big successes using deep learning were possible, are games such as chess and go, where almost infinite data can be generated by computer self-play. A close second might be language modeling, which benefits from the vast amounts of digitized text available on the internet (although current approaches might hit a limit, once all available text of a certain quality has been processed). 
 At the opposite extreme, at least in terms of the potential for data collection, are medical diagnoses for rare diseases, where only so many data points will ever be available. That these diseases are rare is, of course, good for those of us who can avoid contracting them. At the same time, it limits the scope for purely statistical extrapolation using machine learning. Also at this extreme is macroeconomic forecasting: There is only one world economy of the 2020s, which again limits the possibility for purely statistical extrapolation. 
 These predictions for the future bring us to our next topic: Prediction, one of the core tasks of machine learning. Prediction in machine learning goes under the name of  supervised learning . There is a wide range of decision problems that can be solved using prediction algorithms. The specific prediction algorithm used often matters much less than the decision problem that it solves.

 Supervised Learning 
 It is an ancient dream of humanity to be able to predict the future. Almost four thousand years ago, Babylonian astronomers were searching the sky for omens and signs, such as lunar eclipses. According to Babylonian beliefs, such omens might spell evil befalling the land. (Interestingly, though, the evil was still deemed preventable.) A millennium later, Pythia, the high priestess of the oracle of Delphi in Greece, started uttering prophecies under possession by the god Apollo, or, according to an alternative interpretation, under the hallucinogenic influence of volcanic fumes. Priestesses and priests of the Brazilian Candomblé religion divine the future by throwing sets of shells on the floor and interpreting the resulting pattern. And then there are, of course, crystal balls, beloved tools of fortune tellers for at least the last fifteen hundred years, from Merovingian France to Victorian England. 
 Prediction is an important task in machine learning and AI—arguably, the most important task. Most of the time, however, machine learning does not rely on celestial constellations, hallucinogenic volcanic fumes, patterns of shells, or crystal balls. Instead, it is based on the analysis of patterns in large datasets, where there are many observations of predictive  features X  (things that are known for each observation) and predicted  outcomes  or  labels Y  (things that the algorithm tries to infer from the known features). The analysis  of such datasets is called  training . Once training is completed, algorithms for supervised learning can be presented with new examples of features  X  and deliver their prediction (best guess) of what  Y  is going to be for these examples. Prediction in this sense is extremely powerful and allows us to solve many different problems. Some examples help to illustrate just how general this formulation is. 
 Probably the single largest application of supervised learning to date is in the sale of ads online—a market of several hundred billion dollars a year, as of the writing of this book.  Figure 2  shows the massive and growing volume of global spending on online advertising. Internet search engines and social media platforms use the data that they harvest, every time you go online, to predict which ads you will be most likely to click on. They then show you the ads that generate the highest expected profit. Expected profit is equal to the probability of you clicking on an ad multiplied by the price of this ad. Relatedly, social media platforms not only choose what ads to show to you but  also what posts of other users you see. They select the posts that they predict to be most likely to keep you on the platform—for instance, doomscrolling through your feed. The longer you stay and the more you come back, the more ads the platform can show you, which increases the platform’s profits.

 In the most socially contested applications of AI, the task of supervised learning is to predict personal outcomes based on observed personal characteristics. In large companies, systems for evaluating job applicants use the résumés of applicants to predict the likelihood that these applicants will be hired after an interview, or that they will be promoted, down the road, if they are hired. In the criminal justice system, algorithms for risk assessment and bail setting used by the courts draw on defendant criminal records and demographics to predict the likelihood that these defendants will have further police encounters if they are released. In banking, credit scoring systems are employed to assess applicants for mortgages, using applicants’ financial histories to predict the likelihood that they will repay a mortgage. In higher education, admissions offices at universities assess undergraduate applicants by predicting the future exam performance of these students. 
 Supervised learning is also applied in warfare, including in the recent war in Gaza. Shortly after the Hamas attacks on Israeli civilians in October 2023, the Netanyahu government launched a bombing campaign against Gaza. AI played a central role in this bombing campaign. A system called Lavender was used to predict, for every resident of Gaza, whether they might be affiliated with Hamas, as a low-level operative or as a higher-ranking functionary. According to reporting by the investigative journalists of  +972 Magazine , those with a high enough probability were selected as targets for killing, with the AI predictions having an accepted error rate of 10 percent false positives. These people were then linked to their family homes. A system called Where’s Daddy? was used to predict when suspected operatives would be at home so that they could be bombed—usually  when their families were present. The number of unaffiliated civilians that might be killed per low-level operative was reportedly set as up to fifteen to twenty; the number of civilians that might be killed per higher-ranking official was sometimes more than one hundred. 
 A less deadly set of applications of supervised learning has led to spectacular successes of AI in language processing, computer vision, and game play in recent years: Large language models can predict the word that is most likely to come next given a sequence of words typed thus far. Algorithms for machine translation predict the translated version of a sentence based on the sentence its original language; they might for instance predict the English translation based on the Mandarin original. Facial recognition algorithms predict the identities of individuals based on video footage. Algorithms for playing go or chess predict the probability of winning the game for any configuration of the game board. 
 I could go on. The key point here is that many tasks in AI can be solved by predicting some outcome  Y  given some features  X , and supervised learning can accomplish this by statistically analyzing many examples of  Y  and  X . Prediction is a special case of automated decision-making, with the decision of the algorithm being its prediction  Y . The objective of the algorithm is to make a prediction close to the target outcome. 
 Often, predictions are followed by an immediate downstream decision, distinct from the predictions themselves: Who gets invited for a job interview, who is incarcerated while awaiting trial, what social media posts get amplified, whose home gets bombed. The ultimate objective of the algorithms in these examples, then, is to hire those candidates who are most similar to past applicants who were promoted, to incarcerate those defendants who are most likely to have further police encounters, to show those social media posts that are most likely to lead to doomscrolling, and to maximize the probability that bombing targets are at home. If these ultimate objectives start to make you nervous, you might be on to something. 
 Different Algorithms, Same Answer 
 There are many different supervised learning algorithms, and it is easy to get lost in their technical details. Ultimately, it does not matter much which of these algorithms is used to solve a given prediction problem—they will all give roughly the same predictions. And most social problems are inherent to the decision problem being solved, rather than to the algorithm that is used for solving it. 
 Consider, again, the example of university undergraduate admissions. Imagine you are an admissions officer, and you want to use a supervised learning algorithm to predict future student exam performance. The students with better predicted grades are preferred for admission. You have students’ high school transcripts, essays, demographics, and standardized test scores, based on which you can make your prediction of exam performance. 
 There are many algorithms you could choose from. You could use a simple OLS (ordinary least squares) regression or a logit regression, maybe with a ridge or a lasso penalty, or, alternatively, with implicit regularization using early stopping during training, you could use a random forest, or a support vector machine, or Bayesian prediction with a Gaussian process prior, or maybe a deep or shallow neural net, maybe with a transformer architecture (for processing student essays); you could train these models using stochastic gradient descent or Adam, you could use  n -fold cross-validation or a simple holdout sample. 
 Confused? You are not alone. 
 Here is a secret, though: In many ways, it doesn’t matter which of these you use. All these methods aim to solve the same decision problem. Because they are solving the same problem, with enough data and enough computing power, and if implemented right, all these methods will end up giving the same answer. The answer given by all these algorithms, if they have enough data, is the true average exam performance for students with these transcripts, standardized  test scores, demographics, and so on. With enough data, the algorithms can figure out what this true average is and thus make the best-possible prediction. 
 This is an instance of a more general idea that is very important in empirical economics,  identification . This idea is very useful for us here, because it simplifies the problem of learning from data. This idea is based on a thought experiment: Imagine you have as many observations of  X  and  Y  as you would possibly like. Assume that you have as much computing power as you care for. Under these two assumptions, what could you possibly learn from the data? 
 For supervised learning, the answer is simple: What the algorithm could possibly learn is how likely different outcomes  Y  are for any value of  X  that the algorithm can observe in the data. This is what all the supervised learning algorithms try to learn, and this is what they will report under our thought experiment, no matter which algorithm we use. 
 It is also important to note what the algorithms cannot learn in this thought experiment. First, they cannot learn the distribution of  Y  for values of  X  that they never observe. Imagine an algorithm trying to learn how fast objects fall if you drop them. If all your data are from earth, the algorithm will never learn how fast objects fall on the moon. Second, supervised algorithms cannot learn the  causal effect  of any of the elements of  X  on  Y . If some element of  X  is related to  Y  in the data, that could be for all kinds of reasons: It might by that  Y  is impacting  X . It could be that some other, unknown factors are impacting both  X  and  Y . It could be that  X  actually impacts  Y . It could be some combination of the above. Supervised learning algorithms have no way of distinguishing between these different reasons. They therefore cannot tell us anything about what would happen to  Y  if we intervened and changed some element of  X . 
 The thought experiment of unlimited data will be important when we start talking about  democratic governance  of AI. This thought experiment allows us to zoom in on the key issues that public debate  should focus on. It tells us that we can ignore a lot of technical details, such as what prediction algorithm was used. Instead, we can focus on what the prediction problem was or, more generally, what decision problem the algorithm solved—we don’t care as much how the algorithm solved this problem. 
 Getting back to the example of university admissions, we can and should have a democratic discussion about whether university admissions should be based on expected test scores. This admissions criterion is only one among many possibilities. An alternative would be to admit the students who are willing to pay the most or whose parents are expected to donate the most to the school—this is indeed common practice among selective universities. Another alternative would be more egalitarian, where admissions try to increase social mobility by giving a preference to students from disadvantaged backgrounds. Still another alternative would be to target a student composition that maximizes ultimate research output; this objective is sometimes used to make a case for diversity in admissions. 
 Each of these objectives can be framed in terms of a prediction problem. These prediction problems could then be solved using all kinds of supervised learning algorithms. The general point here is that public debate can focus on the  objective  of the prediction problem—should we target expected exam scores, future donations, disadvantaged background, or contribution to research output? What algorithm is then used for prediction, to pursue this objective, is secondary. 
 An implication of this argument is that complicated algorithms are not an excuse for leaving important decisions to the “experts”—decisions such as what the objective of university admissions should be. By focusing on the underlying decision problem and on what happens in settings with a lot of data, we can radically simplify the understanding of AI in socially consequential contexts. This simplification opens these contexts for public debate, which might be precluded if we stay on the level of the technical details of machine learning. 
 To talk about identification means to ask what happens when algorithms have, essentially, infinite amounts of data. In real life, unlimited data are, of course, not always available. Supervised learning algorithms, therefore, need to be smart about how they deal with the uncertainty that comes from having limited data. How they do that is the topic of the next chapter. 
 With limited data, algorithms face important trade-offs between overfitting and underfitting. These trade-offs are key to the question of how we can learn from experience—a question that has occupied philosophers and scientists for thousands of years. These trade-offs also delimit the future potential of AI in different application domains. These trade-offs determine the relative role and  complementarity  of data, model complexity, and computational power in enabling improved performance. Studying these trade-offs will shed light on the importance of the different means of prediction (data and computational infrastructure, in particular) and who controls them.

 Overfitting and Underfitting 
 On his long journey home after the conquest of Troy, the hero Odysseus of Homer’s epic poem (the  Odyssey ) must cross the narrow Strait of Messina, which separates Sicily from the Italian mainland. On one side of the strait awaits the sea monster Scylla. Scylla is a former nymph who has been turned into a monster with six dog heads springing from her thighs. These dog heads might devour sailors alive. On the other side of the strait lives the monster Charybdis. Charybdis swallows large amounts of water and then belches them out, thus creating a giant whirlpool that can drag entire ships underwater. If Odysseus sails at a safe distance from Scylla, his entire ship would be drowned by Charybdis. If, however, he sails at a safe distance from Charybdis, his crew would be attacked by Scylla. What to do? 
 Any learning algorithm faces a similar, if usually less deadly, dilemma. The two monsters threatening every learning algorithm are called  overfitting  and  underfitting . Both overfitting and underfitting prevent successful predictions for new data. If an algorithm is overfitting, then it is prone to developing superstitions. An algorithm that is overfitting takes random patterns in the observed data and assumes that they are going to hold in all future instances. If an algorithm is underfitting, then it is stubborn and does not learn. An algorithm that is underfitting does not update its predictions even  in the face of accumulated evidence that it is wrong. To learn successfully, supervised learning algorithms must carefully sail in between these monsters on either side of the narrow strait of learning. 
 Imagine moving to a city in a new country where you don’t know the rules of traffic. (This happened to me not so long ago when I moved to the United Kingdom.) As you walk through your new city, you observe how people are navigating the streets. Are you an underfitter? If so, then you might stubbornly continue to look left when you cross the street, under the presumption that cars should drive on the right. Well, it turns out that here they don’t, and you should update your beliefs and behavior. 
 Or are you an overfitter? In that case, you might observe that, on a few occasions, cars take a left on a red light, and you might conclude that this is a legal maneuver. You would be wrong: It turns out that you just happened to see some drivers who broke the law, and you should not have updated your beliefs so quickly. 
 The problem of overfitting is closely related to the gap between  in-sample  and  out-of-sample  performance in prediction.  In-sample  refers to the data used for training, while  out-of-sample  refers to data that have not been used for training—data that might have been set aside for later use. It is easy to make predictions in-sample for data points that an algorithm has already seen. But these predictions might just pick up coincidental patterns. Such coincidental patterns will not hold out-of-sample, for new data points. 
 Both overfitting and underfitting need to be avoided for predictions to be successful. But doing so in the design of an AI algorithm requires care. One lever that can be used to navigate this trade-off between overfitting and underfitting is  model complexity . 
 Model Complexity and Overfitting 
 One of the most well-known methodological prescriptions is  Occam’s razor . To philosophers, a  razor  is a principle or idea that helps weed out unlikely explanations for something—a way of streamlining how  one thinks about something. Occam’s razor is a broad principle for selecting between different explanations of observed phenomena. This principle is often paraphrased as “the simplest explanation is usually the best one.” 
 William of Ockham (Latin spelling: Occam) was a Franciscan friar and philosopher who lived in the early fourteenth century. Born in England, he studied at Oxford and later moved to Avignon in France. There he got into political trouble with the Catholic Church, because he defended the Franciscan principles that prohibited Franciscan friars from owning private property, either individually or collectively. After fleeing Avignon, William of Ockham found refuge in Bavaria, where he became the leader of a group of Franciscan dissidents. Of William of Ockham’s extensive writings on philosophy, politics, and theology, his most well-known idea today is Occam’s razor. 
 Occam’s razor, which prescribes that we should stick to the simplest possible explanation that is consistent with observed patterns, makes a lot of sense. But does this prescription imply that, in the design of supervised learning algorithms, one should always just consider simple models of the world? Not quite. Simple models allow us to avoid overfitting (extrapolating patterns from a few random observations), but they potentially lead to underfitting (not learning patterns that are consistently present in the data). Sometimes the world is complex, and supervised learning algorithms need to reflect this complexity. 
 Occam’s razor points to a lever by which algorithms can balance overfitting and underfitting. Algorithms can select how simple or complex the prediction functions are that they consider. If an algorithm only allows for simple functions, in line with Occam’s razor, then it will be safeguarded against overfitting—but it might be underfitting. If an algorithm allows for a complex set of possible prediction functions, then one of these functions could be close to describing the true patterns in the world, and the algorithm is safeguarded against underfitting—but it might be overfitting. 
 Figure 3  illustrates this point. Recall that our goal is to minimize  out-of-sample prediction errors, where predictions of the algorithm are different from true outcomes  Y . For low model complexity, in-sample and out-of-sample prediction errors are similar, and both high, because the learning algorithm is underfitting: The models that it considers are too simple to pick up on complex patterns in the data. For high model complexity, the in-sample prediction errors are low, but out-of-sample errors are high, because the algorithm is overfitting: Its models fit random patterns in the training data, which are not present out-of-sample. The best out-of-sample error rate is achieved with intermediate model complexity, where a balance of overfitting and underfitting is maintained. 
 Figure 3  Overfitting and underfitting 
 Choosing model complexity carefully is one lever by which algorithms can navigate between these twin dangers. There are also alternative methods do so, and they go under many different names in machine learning:  Regularization ,  penalization ,  shrinkage , and  early stopping , among others. All these methods, despite their superficial differences, are used because they implement some version of Occam’s razor—to prevent overfitting of the data. 
 How strictly algorithms need to apply Occam’s razor depends on how much data they have at their disposition. The trade-off between overfitting and underfitting shifts as algorithms get more and more data: Overfitting becomes less of a problem as algorithms have more  data at their disposal. Random, coincidental patterns tend to average out over many observations. The risk of underfitting, however, does not change as the algorithm gets to see more data, for fixed model complexity. 
 As more data become available, algorithms should therefore shift toward being more flexible. This increased flexibility, implemented by using more complex models or less regularization, allows the algorithms to reduce the danger of underfitting, and in doing so to fit ever more complicated patterns. 
 It is for this underlying mathematical reason—that overfitting becomes less of an issue with more data, but underfitting remains an issue to the same extent—that trade-offs for machine learning algorithms have shifted in the era of big data. Increasingly complex models, usually leveraging artificial neural nets, are fit on ever-larger datasets. (Examples of this development are the ever-larger large language models, which are discussed in the next chapter, where  figure 6  shows the massive growth of both datasets and model complexity.) 
 But no matter how large the datasets, algorithms still need to make a trade-off between overfitting and underfitting—even if that trade-off shifts with the scale of data. Algorithms still need to decide just how much model complexity to allow for. 
 Tuning and Cross-Validation 
 To find the optimal level of model complexity, algorithms need to evaluate how successful their predictions are. It would be tempting to test a model by checking whether its predictions would have worked well in the past, and to use such a test to find the golden middle between overfitting and underfitting. Unfortunately, this does not work. One should never use the same data to train an algorithm and then to test the predictions of that trained algorithm; hindsight is easy, and such an approach leads to overfitting. 
 Instead, we need to  split  the data collected in the past—learn from one part of the data, and then test our predictions on the remaining part, where this remaining part is called a  holdout sample . Such a test, using sample splitting, correctly simulates the difficulty of foresight. With this kind of test, an algorithm can find the safe passage between the twin dangers of superstition and stubbornness, overfitting and underfitting. Predictive performance on holdout samples also forms the basis of the benchmarks used in AI to compare model performance, for instance on the platform Kaggle, which organizes prediction competitions. 
 As discussed above, there are different methods that algorithms can use to trade off the risks of overfitting and underfitting. All these methods involve some  tuning parameters , which adjust the severity of Occam’s razor—that is, they adjust how much the algorithm errs on the side of overfitting or underfitting, by adjusting model complexity or the amount of regularization. The idea of sample splitting is that we can use the holdout sample to find the optimal value of the tuning parameter. This is basically a matter of trial and error: To start, pick a tuning parameter. Run a learning algorithm on the set of training data. Check your predictions on the holdout sample. Change your tuning parameter, and do the same thing again. Keep doing this until you have found a tuning parameter that seems optimal—that is, a tuning parameter that generates the smallest amount of prediction errors on the holdout sample. 
 A variant of this approach (of using sample splitting for tuning), called  early stopping , is often used in deep learning, which we discuss in the next chapter. In early stopping, during the training of a neural network, predictions are repeatedly updated a little bit, in a direction that improves performance on the training data. After each such small update, the predictions are checked on the holdout sample. The algorithm keeps updating until the predictions start to worsen in the holdout sample, which is the point where the algorithm starts to overfit. This is where the algorithm stops, even if it could improve  its predictions in the training sample further by continuing to update. The early stopping approach is particularly attractive in settings with very large datasets and complicated models, where the cost of computation is a concern. This includes the most successful applications of deep learning. 
 Deep learning forms the basis of almost all the recent spectacular successes of AI. It is also one of the most mystified branches of machine learning, evoking the misleading idea that AI researchers have created artificial brains. Our technical discussion will serve to dispel some of this mystification. But first we should answer the question, What is deep learning, actually? Stay tuned!

 Deep Learning 
 Alchemists have long dreamed of creating artificial life. In the early sixteenth century, the Swiss alchemist and medical pioneer Paracelsus provided a recipe for creating a  homunculus , a small artificial human. His method involved mixing human fluids and horse dung, and then nourishing the transparent being, which was bound to emerge, with human blood. Similar prescriptions can be found in older texts of Muslim alchemy, for instance in the  Jabirian corpus  from the early ninth century. One method for creating artificial animal life described in the Jabirian corpus requires putting animal fluids in an animal-shaped vessel, placing the vessel at the center of a model of the celestial sphere, and applying heat. 
 The alchemist’s dream persists in modern times but in a less physical form. The dream of creating artificial life has been renewed as the dream of creating artificial brains, based on nonbiological hardware, but similar in structure to the biological model. 
 Some researchers in the field of AI have been attempting to build an artificial brain, or at least artificial neural nets, since the 1960s. These researchers were adherents of what used to be known as the  connectionist paradigm , which is now called  deep learning . The connectionist paradigm stood in contrast to the  symbolic paradigm . The latter, which was once dominant in the field of AI, sought to build AI based on higher-level abstractions, instead of reducing intelligence  to the level of neurons. The connectionist paradigm, deep learning, and artificial neural nets were the subject of a somewhat obscure academic niche field within AI for several decades. Now, since shortly after the turn of the millennium, they dominate most branches of AI. John Hopfield and Geoffrey Hinton, two of the pioneers of deep learning from its time of obscurity, received the 2024 Nobel Prize in Physics. 
 Why did this belated success of neural nets occur? Some recent innovations notwithstanding, many of the ideas behind deep learning have been around for quite a while. Rather than involving major conceptual breakthroughs, the delayed success of neural nets can be attributed to the increased availability of data and computational power. Neural nets only came into their own once the scale of both the available data and the available computational power reached a certain critical level.

 How Deep Learning Works 
 Deep learning is a special case of supervised learning, where artificial neural networks are used to make predictions. We have already discussed numerous examples of prediction problems: predicting  image labels from images (as in facial recognition, where individual identities are predicted from images of faces), predicting translated text in one language from text in another language, and so forth. 
 The neural networks used in deep learning define a particular class of prediction functions. A  function  takes a bunch of numbers—pixel values, say, or some numerical encoding of text—and returns another set of numbers. A  prediction function  takes predictive features  X  and returns a predicted value for the outcome  Y . 
 Neural nets build complicated prediction functions out of simpler functions. A neural net starts with some function that takes the predictive features  X  and returns a bunch of numbers. These numbers are then used as the input for a second function, which in turn feeds into a third function, and so on. 
 The numbers returned by the last function in such a chain are the output of the neural network—the prediction. If the network has multiple functions layered on top of each other like this, the network is called  deep . This is the technical meaning of  deep learning : Complicated prediction functions are built from a chain of simpler functions. (The technical meaning aside, calling this approach  deep  was also good marketing. The term  deep learning  clearly resonated with investors and journalists, arguably more than  connectionist paradigm  could.) 
 Why would we call a prediction function built in this way, as a chain of simpler functions, a  neural network ? The neural network metaphor is based on an interpretation of each number (which is returned by each of the functions that were put in a row) as the  activation  of a  neuron . This activation is calculated based on the numbers returned by the previous function—that is, based on the activations of the previous layer of neurons. And the activation of our current neuron, in turn, serves as input for subsequent neurons in the following layer. The whole construction, then, is a network of neurons. 
 Figure 5  illustrates this interpretation and shows a simple artificial neural network. Each layer is a function of the previous layer. The network in  figure 5  calculates predictions by taking the numbers of  the input layer and applying a function to calculate the numbers of hidden layer 1; based on these numbers, it calculates the hidden layer 2 and then calculates the output layer. Artificial neural networks that are used in practice have many more neurons in each layer, and they typically also have more layers. 
 Figure 5  A simple neural network 
 How do artificial neural networks learn? Each of the functions in such a network depends on a set of numbers called  weights . If we change the weights, we get different predictions for the same inputs. The goal of deep learning is to find weights that deliver good predictions. In the neural network interpretation, the weights describe the strength of  synaptic connections  between different neurons. Learning happens via adjustment of these synaptic connections. 
 During learning, every time the network processes a new observation (a new image, a new sentence, etc.), it calculates its prediction (a label for the image, a translated sentence, etc.). This prediction is calculated by applying the first function in our network to the input observation, then the second function to the output of the first function, and so on,  feeding  the information  forward . 
 Once the prediction is calculated by the last layer in the network, this prediction is compared to the true label for the observation at hand, or the true outcome. There will typically be some prediction error. The goal of learning is to make prediction errors as small, or unlikely, as possible. 
 For each observation, we can calculate not only the prediction of the network; we can also calculate how a small change of the weights of the network would have affected the prediction error. And, relatedly, we can calculate in what direction we need to change the weights to reduce the prediction error for our current observation the most; this direction is called the  gradient . Learning algorithms adjust the weights of the network a little bit, moving them in the direction of this gradient, which reduces the error. In doing this adjustment of weights, the algorithms  propagate  information  backward  through the  network: The evaluation that occurs at the level of the last neurons—that is, the prediction and its error—affects how weights change for neurons that come earlier in the network. This backpropagation of information, to reduce prediction errors, is one of the key differences between artificial and biological neural networks. 
 After this adjustment of the network weights, we move on to the next observation and repeat the same process, and then on to the next observation, over and over again. This whole procedure is known as  stochastic gradient descent . 
 As discussed previously, we need to be careful to not overfit the data. The easiest way this can be avoided is by keeping a separate sample of observations that is not used directly for adjusting the network weights, but for which we can also calculate prediction errors at each step. Once the prediction errors in this separate sample stop improving any further during stochastic gradient descent, the learning process is stopped. 
 This whole procedure sounds complicated, especially when you learn about it for the first time. But relative to other methods in machine learning, this procedure is quite simple, in terms of the calculations involved. Long before the recent rise of deep learning, researchers in optimization and statistics had devised a wide range of more complicated and sophisticated ways to learn, improve predictions, and avoid overfitting. 
 It is the relative simplicity of the calculations involved in deep learning (compared to its technical antecedents) that has allowed it to scale up so much. Its simplicity makes it possible to deal with massive amounts of data and massively complex neural nets with billions of weights. And its simplicity is furthered by the type of functions used for the neurons: Almost all the calculations that need to be done for these functions amount to simple multiplications and additions. Specialized computer hardware (e.g., graphics processing units, or  GPUs , and tensor processing units, or  TPUs ) has been built in recent  years to do a large number of these multiplications and additions in parallel, at the same time. Availability of this specialized hardware has contributed to the rise of deep learning. 
 Neural Nets Are Not Artificial Brains 
 It is tempting to think of deep learning and neural nets as artificial brains. Don’t. Despite the original biological inspiration, modern deep learning has little in common with biological neural nets, or with the process of learning in human brains or animal brains. 
 Three differences between biological and artificial neural nets stand out. First, the way deep learning algorithms update the parameters of a neural net propagates information  backward  through the network. This is something that does not happen for biological neurons. Information only travels one way for biological neurons. For biological neurons, there is no mechanism for sending information backward, to adjust weights, if a prediction turns out to be wrong. Second, biological neural nets have cycles and feedback loops. This is something that most artificial neural nets do not have. When making predictions, information in artificial neural nets only flows forward, through the subsequent functions that were layered on top of each other. And third, the functions used in each layer of an artificial neural net are typically chosen to make calculations simple, so that they only involve multiplications and additions that can be done in parallel. These functions are quite different from functions that would model biological neurons more realistically. 
 Instead of thinking of deep learning in terms of artificial brains, it is more useful to think of deep learning as the craft of building complicated functions out of simpler functions—to model complicated relationships, as encountered in domains such as image recognition and natural language processing. 
 There are different ways that the functions making up artificial neural nets can be built. How to best do this depends on the application  domain, and it is the subject matter of a large body of practical knowledge. For computer vision and image recognition,  convolutional neural nets  have turned out to be successful. Such neural nets leverage the fact that an image can be shifted left or right, up or down, and still represent the same object. For natural language processing,  transformer  networks, another variant of neural networks that use  self-attention , form the backbone of recent large language models. Such transformer networks are based on a recognition of the fact that, in human languages, the relevant context for interpreting a word often does not occur in the direct vicinity of the word, but instead it occurs somewhere else in a sentence or paragraph. To illustrate, consider the word “occurs” in the preceding sentence. This word refers to “the relevant context,” which appears much earlier in the sentence. Transformer models learn to pay  attention  to this relevant context. 
 The development of such specialized architectures for neural nets, and of deep learning more generally, is based on a large amount of tinkering and trial and error. It does indeed resemble alchemy at times, in the spirit of Paracelsus or of the Muslim alchemists of the Jabirian corpus. 
 There is something rather unsettling about this state of deep learning—at least for people with a taste for theory and an interest in machine learning, like me: Neural nets work better than they should, according to our theoretical understanding! As shown in  figure 3 , models that are too complex tend to overfit, and deliver poor predictions out-of-sample. How is it possible that the models of deep learning can have many more parameters (weights) than observations and still deliver good predictions, seemingly avoiding the fate of overfitting? And why do they often extrapolate well to settings never encountered in the training data? There is much theoretical interest in these questions, and parts of the answer have started to emerge, but we are still far from a systematic understanding. 
 This state of affairs notwithstanding, a lack of theoretical understanding  regarding how neural networks solve (prediction) problems does not prevent us from having a broad discussion regarding which (prediction) problems they should be solving. In deep learning, as in AI more broadly, such a discussion needs to be the starting point for democratic governance. 
 Self-Supervised Learning and Generative AI 
 As noted earlier, a lot of problems in AI are prediction problems. Supervised learning solves these prediction problems. But there is an issue that we have not discussed yet: Supervised learning needs labeled data, and such labeled data are often expensive or hard to find. Data are one of the key means of prediction, and limits on their availability imply limits on the potential of AI. 
 Take the example of image recognition, which involves predicting labels (“Is this a sloth or a chocolate croissant?”) from the images themselves. Images (say of sloths and chocolate croissants) are abundantly available on the internet, but labels are not. To create such labels, images need to be manually annotated by human workers on platforms such as MTurk. This is expensive, and this approach can only be scaled up to a certain point. 
 As another example, machine translation involves predicting sentences in a target language from sentences in the original language. Creating these translated sentences is even more expensive than labeling images, since it requires qualified human translators. There are certain bodies of translated text that can serve as a basis (for instance, proceedings of the United Nations or the European Union), but there are limits to this approach. 
 So how can machine learning scale further, beyond the limits of human-labeled data? The answer, for certain problem domains, has been found in  self-supervised learning . Behind the evocative name, there is a simple idea: Find prediction problems where no separate manual labeling by humans is needed. In one version of this idea,  the same data alternatingly serves as features and as labels. This approach has been especially important for natural language modeling, where the idea is to predict the next word based on the sequence of preceding words, or to predict a random word in the middle of a sentence based on the rest of the sentence. By changing the prediction goal in this way, data are available at a much larger scale than would be the case if we restricted attention to manually labeled observations. 
 This approach to language modeling brings us back to the goal that the field of AI started out with: Turing’s imitation game. Recall that the imitation game requires a computer to maintain a conversation with a human, such that the human believes they are conversing with another human. Restating this problem, the imitation game requires the computer to generate text. And that is exactly what recent advances in language modeling have succeeded at doing convincingly, most notably in chatbots such as ChatGPT or Claude. Text generation is not the only success story of  generative AI ; the automatic generation of realistic images has also made great advances, notably in algorithms such as Stable Diffusion. 
 Both text generation and image generation build on self-supervised deep learning. The specific model architectures used in these domains are fairly recent inventions. Only time will tell, but one might conjecture that these architectures will be superseded in a few years, while most of the more general principles of AI and machine learning, which this book discusses, are likely to remain relevant in the long run. 
 Today, virtually all large language models use the transformer architecture discussed above. The prime reason for the popularity of the transformer architecture is its practical success; its popularity is not motivated by any well-understood theoretical reasons for its superiority. The scale of the transformer models used in practice is quite mind boggling.  Figure 6  shows the number of training tokens (i.e., the size of training datasets) and the number of parameters by  year of release in different large language models. The vertical axis is scaled so that each step corresponds to a tenfold increase. Some of these models have more than one trillion parameters and were trained on datasets of more than ten trillion tokens. This scale has increased continuously over the last few years. It is this scale that enables transformers to effectively store a compressed copy of the entire internet. (Recent successes of the Chinese AI company DeepSeek suggest that this scale might be reduced somewhat without loss of performance.)

 Language modeling is one success story of deep learning; image generation is another one. The most successful algorithms for image generation today use diffusion models. Diffusion models are based on the following idea: Start with a real image. Add a bit of noise to that image—make it grainy, like on an old TV with bad reception. Then use a neural network to predict the original image from the noisy one. Repeat the process by adding even more noise to the noised-up image, then predict the slightly less noisy one, and so on. How does this allow the algorithm to generate images? Once it has been trained, the algorithm can start with a randomly generated  image that is pure noise. It can then iteratively predict less noisy images until there is a predicted version of a completely noise-free image. This approach turns out to generate realistic, high-quality images. Just as was the case for transformers, the popularity of this approach is due to its practical success. 
 Generative AI, whether for the generation of text or of images, raises important questions of data ownership. Both large language models and image generation models are trained on data that are primarily scraped from the internet, without either the knowledge or the consent of those who produced the original texts and images. One way to think about the resulting algorithms is that they are compressed versions of their training data. When these algorithms generate text or images, they effectively reproduce transformed versions of the data they were trained on. Collecting data by scraping the internet, training generative models on them, and then releasing these generative models for commercial purposes amounts to a massive concentration of control and effective ownership of the most important among the means of prediction—data. This privatization and concentration of a resource that was previously either in the public domain, or the intellectual property of others, will have large ramifications for the concentration of economic power in general, and for the allocation of control over AI more specifically. This is discussed later in the book. 
 Generative AI also raises interesting questions about objective functions and control over AI. Throughout this book, I emphasize that AI maximizes or minimizes some objective function—such as predictive loss (i.e., prediction errors) for the next word on the internet, in the case of large language models. For generative AI, we need to modify this statement slightly: The model minimizes predictive loss for the next word  in response to a prompt  that is chosen by the user of the generative AI tool. Control over objectives is thus effectively split for generative AI. A central entity, such as OpenAI, controls the  foundation model , such as the language model GPT-4.  This foundation model is made accessible via general-purpose interfaces such as ChatGPT. The user of ChatGPT specifies a prompt. The response of the chatbot solves a decision problem that is cospecified between those who control the foundation model (e.g., OpenAI/Microsoft) and the user who chooses the prompt. 
 Neural nets and deep learning have had surprising successes in many domains, one of which is generative AI. But deep learning remains within the framework of supervised learning—that is, of prediction. There is something very important that is missing in the prediction framework. Acquiring new skills for humans often involves learning by doing. We need to try new things to see whether they work. We need to  explore  or experiment. The same is true for algorithms. 
 The next chapter, which concludes our review of how AI works, discusses the trade-off between exploration and exploitation. This trade-off governs how AI can learn to act in the world while collecting data to improve its actions along the way. AI is already used to constantly experiment on humans—for instance, on social media platforms to maximize ad sales. Once again, the choice of objective or reward that is maximized by the algorithms performing the experiments is crucial. This choice of objective is at the heart of potential AI failures, and of social conflicts over AI.

 The Exploration/Exploitation Trade-Off 
 “Outside the comfort zone is where the magic happens.” You might have heard this life advice before: We need to try new things, including things that we are nervous about. This allows us to broaden our horizons and to live a richer, more fulfilled life. But how far should we push this advice? If you already know what is best, why should you subject yourself to discomfort and risk, instead of choosing what you know to be good? 
 Suppose you have had pizza many times in your life, and you know that you like it. But there are a lot of other food options out there that you have not yet tried, such as, for example, Ethiopian food. You might, in fact, like Ethiopian food a lot. As you decide what food to eat, you could play it safe and  exploit  the fact that you know you are going to like pizza. But you could also  explore  instead, by trying Ethiopian food. If you realize that you do like it, you will have improved your food options for the future. And if you don’t like it, you can just go back to pizza next time. 
 This tension between exploring new things and exploiting what you already know to be good is a core principle of AI. It comes up whenever an AI system needs to make repeated decisions while learning from the outcomes of these decisions. Such situations are different from the situations that we have discussed so far; they require making decisions that go beyond mere predictions. In the supervised  learning settings previously discussed, the data were just given. The availability of data did not depend on what the algorithms did. If you are forecasting the weather for tomorrow, for instance, then you are going to observe the actual weather tomorrow, regardless of your forecast today. Therefore, the only thing you need to worry about in weather forecasting is getting the best-possible forecast for tomorrow. You don’t need to think about how your forecast today would affect your ability to make accurate forecasts in the future. Put differently, you only need to exploit; there is no need to explore. 
 But if you made yourself a pizza, then there is no way you are going to find out whether you would have liked Ethiopian food. You might want to try Ethiopian food because there is a chance you will like it, which could affect your future choices. If you never explore, you will always be stuck with pizza. 
 We can only observe how well an option did if we chose it. Situations where this is the case are called  multi-armed bandits  in AI. The name comes from gambling. Imagine you are playing slot machines in a casino (not that you should do that). Slot machines are also known as one-armed bandits. In the casino, you have a choice between multiple slot machines—which explains the anatomically nonstandard bandit in the metaphor. You might try playing different machines until you figure out which has the highest winnings and then keep playing that machine. 
 What is the best way to act in such settings? Either extreme is not optimal: Always exploiting the option you expect to be best (only eating pizza or only playing the machine that happened to pay out initially) means that you never learn anything new. And always exploring new things (trying new cuisines or playing new slot machines) means you never get to leverage the knowledge that you acquired by experimenting. The optimal behavior lies somewhere in the middle. Good algorithms need to find this optimal middle ground, just as they need to find the optimal middle in the trade-off between overfitting and underfitting. 
 The key principle that guides algorithms that trade off exploration and exploitation is called  optimism in the face of uncertainty : If you know for sure that one option is better than another, just go for it—there is nothing to learn by doing the worse thing. But if you are uncertain about an option, and you don’t expect it to be horrible, give it a shot. Apply the benefit of doubt to the unknown. 
 The examples we have used thus far—choosing your dinner, playing slot machines—might seem a little frivolous. But exploration versus exploitation can be a matter of life and death. This is most obvious in the context of choosing between different medical treatments, such as different drugs.  Exploiting  is what you would expect your doctor to do. They should give you the best-possible treatment for your condition. But if we only exploited, medicine would never improve. To  explore  new options, we have clinical trials. In clinical trials, participants are randomly assigned to different treatment options. If at the end of the trial there is enough evidence that a new treatment option is better than the alternatives, then it gets approved and might become recommended medical practice. 
 Is this the best way to trade off exploration and exploitation—to first run clinical trials and then recommend one option as the best one? It is an approximation, but more patient lives could be saved by being smarter about the trade-off. Actual trials have long addressed some of this trade-off, between exploration and exploitation, for ethical reasons: If in a clinical trial it emerges that a new treatment either harms all patients or cures all patients, then the trial must be aborted. Doctors should not knowingly harm patients, nor should they withhold a treatment that they expect can cure their patients. An active area of work in clinical medicine, drawing on these insights from machine learning, is starting to use  adaptive designs , which apply an optimal degree of optimism in the face of uncertainty. Regulatory agencies such as the US Food and Drug Administration have recently released guidelines allowing adaptive designs. 
 By far the most widespread use of multi-armed bandit algorithms, however, is to sell you stuff. The big tech companies that provide  search engines or social media platforms make most of their profits by selling targeted advertisements. They earn money whenever you click on an ad. Their prime objective, therefore, is to maximize ad clicks. Multi-armed bandit algorithms are key tools in doing this. When surfing the internet, you are constantly the subject of little experiments. Algorithms decide what ads are displayed, when, and to whom. Billions of dollars are made by  exploring  what makes you most likely to stay on a site and click on the ads and then  exploiting  this knowledge. This is how Facebook or Google decide what ads to show to you. 
 I noted earlier that something crucial was missing from the supervised learning framework: Supervised learning does not involve experimentation (exploration); it does not take into account how an algorithm’s actions affect its ability to learn and to make better decisions in the future. This is addressed in the multi-armed bandit framework, which formalizes a concern for exploration. But the multi-armed bandit framework is itself still shortsighted in important ways. This framework considers how actions impact the ability to learn, but it does not involve any concern for how actions impact rewards in the future. The multi-armed bandit framework does not involve any notion of planning, which is as crucial in many real-world tasks as it is in playing games. The problem of planning is taken into account in the framework of reinforcement learning. 
 Reinforcement Learning 
 Certain board games have long stood as symbols of intellectual challenge: chess in Europe, go in East Asia, and backgammon in the Middle East. In the past, these games have proven surprisingly hard to master for computers. Professional players could easily beat the best chess, go, and backgammon programs until the 1990s. 
 The main reason why these games are hard for computers is that the set of possible game plays is so vast. Look a few steps into the future  in a game of chess or go, and the number of possible sequences of moves becomes huge—so huge, that it would be impossible for any computer to enumerate all these possible sequences. 
 And yet, computers now defeat the best players in all these games. Famously, the chess computer Deep Blue defeated then–world champion Garry Kasparov in 1997. This highly publicized victory of machine over human was followed nineteen years later by the defeat of go master Lee Sedol in 2016 by the program AlphaGo. AlphaGo’s construction was built on ideas that were previously implemented in the backgammon program TD-Gammon, which was originally released in 1992, and which had reached performance close to the best human players by 1998. 
 The design of Deep Blue was still based on older approaches, drawing on human expertise in chess and on a large database of chess games played by human grandmasters. Compared to Deep Blue, the algorithm of AlphaGo was much simpler. Like TD-Gammon, it used an approach called (deep)  reinforcement learning . Both TD-Gammon and AlphaGo learned to play by playing a vast number of games against themselves. 
 The term  reinforcement learning  comes from behaviorist ideas of how animals learn and how they can be trained. According to these ideas, behavior that is rewarded is  reinforced  and therefore occurs more often over time. Conversely, behavior that is punished occurs less often. If the rewards are chosen appropriately, animals can be trained to solve all kinds of problems. 
 It is arguably ethically less problematic, and more promising, to teach a computer to play backgammon by means of selective rewards than to try the same with your dog or cat. But how does reinforcement learning work with a computer? Your computer has no innate desire for treats, after all. 
 Let us start by again considering the multi-armed bandits. How would they perform in a game of chess or go? A multi-armed bandit algorithm initially makes random moves. If a move is rewarded  (leads to a win), the algorithm will be more likely to make this move again in the future. If a move does not lead to a win, the algorithm will be less likely to make that move. Using this approach directly for chess or go or backgammon would be rather pointless. Most board positions in these games do not lead to a win, no matter what move is made. What is missing from the multi-armed bandit framework for this setting? The key point to note is that the goal of most moves is not to yield an immediate win. Instead, good moves lead to a change of the position of the board that makes it more likely to win in the future. Put differently, there is no direct reward no matter what action is taken. Instead, actions change the state of the environment (the game board) in a way that affects the likelihood of future rewards. 
 This description suggests that we might turn the problem of game play into something like a supervised learning problem: For every configuration of the board, can we predict the likelihood of winning? If we have a good prediction of this likelihood, then we can always pick the move that maximizes the likelihood of winning for the implied configuration of the board. To train an algorithm, we could construct a dataset of board positions and ultimate outcomes of the game (from historical matches or from the computer playing against itself), and then we could run some supervised learning algorithm to predict the outcome of the match for each position. 
 This approach almost works. With infinite data and computation time, it would work. It is however inefficient, because it does not use all the information in the data. Here is a variant that works better: Suppose that you have a preliminary estimate of winning probabilities for each configuration. Now run two predictions for every configuration encountered during play: First, predict whether you will get a win or loss right now. This is exactly the type of prediction a multi-armed bandit would make. Second, predict what the configuration of the board will be after your move, by the next time it is your turn, and for this configuration plug in your preliminary prediction of ultimately winning. Sum those two and you get an estimate of the  probability of winning from the current configuration, which you can use to update your algorithm. 
 An algorithm of this form, with some tweaks, allows AlphaGo and similar programs to learn to play. Instead of attempting to directly predict the ultimate outcome of the game, this approach predicts the prediction (!) for the next configuration. This approach allows learning to advance much faster. In the scheme of things, this is again a simple approach. This is also a very data-hungry approach. This approach can build on the machinery of supervised learning that we discussed before—in particular, on deep learning. Deep learning is indeed what programs like TD-Gammon and AlphaGo used. They trained neural networks to predict the probability of winning, in the recursive manner described above. 
 This all sounds pretty good:  Deep reinforcement learning  can teach itself by exploring the world and learning to plan for the future. Why not use this approach to address all kinds of real-world problems? For example, could we build self-driving cars using this approach? There are at least two big reasons why this is not possible. 
 The first reason is that we don’t usually observe everything about the relevant state of the world. Games like chess or go or backgammon, despite their complexity, are simple in this respect: If you observe the state of the board, then there is nothing else you need to know. There is nothing hidden about the state of the game. A good player can look at the board and propose a move without knowing anything about the past—that is, about the sequence of moves that led to the present configuration of the board. (There are some exceptions to this claim in chess, where you also need to keep track of past movements of the king, rooks, and pawns, but that does not change the basic point here.) 
 This is not how most of the real world works. Consider driving a car, for instance. Suppose you are driving, and you see a child disappear behind a truck that is parked at the side of the street ahead of you. In this scenario, you should remember that there is a child who  is blocked from your view by the truck, and you should infer that the child might jump out at any time—and you should make sure to not hit this child with your car. Since you can no longer see the child because they walked behind the truck, you need to remember what you saw before, you need to draw conclusions about the state of the world, and you need to make predictions about its future state. Any real-world application of reinforcement learning must overcome this issue of  partial observability , which implies that it needs to remember the past to predict the future. 
 The second reason why deep reinforcement learning is not easily applied to real-world problems is that it is a very data-hungry approach. As mentioned above, the way AlphaGo learned was by playing an enormous number of games against itself. This generated a vast trove of data from which the algorithm could learn—much more data than would have been available from recorded games of human masters alone. As an example, AlphaZero, the successor of AlphaGo, played forty-four million games against itself to master the game of chess. 
 This approach works for games, where all actions can take place in a virtual, simulated environment. This approach does not work in real-world settings like driving. For obvious reasons we cannot have millions of self-driving cars driving for millions of hours, racing around and constantly crashing into each other, into other obstacles, and into humans until they figure out how to drive. (Even if tech companies might want to do exactly that.) Actual self-driving cars, to the extent that they exist, thus need to rely on approaches other than pure reinforcement learning. 
 There is a more general lesson here. Current deep-learning-based methods in AI need lots of data. There are some settings where data have become readily available, for instance via self-play in simulated environments or by scraping the internet for enormous databases of text or images. In the contexts where such enormous amounts of data have become available, deep learning has exceeded all expectations.  At the same time, there might be fundamental limits to how much data can be generated in many domains. We can generate a lot more data for the game of go than for road traffic. We might be able to scrape the entire internet for text, but once that has been achieved, further sources of high-quality text data will be hard to find. This might limit the potential for further progress in language modeling, for instance.

 Key Ideas to Remember 
 Let us summarize some of the key ideas that were covered over the course of the preceding chapters. 
 At its core, AI is automated decision-making based on data, where the decisions are chosen to maximize some measurable objective. AI uses data to learn and to act successfully in the world. 
 Much of AI is built to solve prediction problems. Any algorithm that solves a prediction problem needs to avoid the twin dangers of overfitting and underfitting. Data splitting is a general method to find the golden middle between these twin dangers. 
 There are important settings where AI needs to go beyond simple prediction by interacting with the world and recording the consequences of its actions. In these settings, AI trades off exploration (learning new things by experimenting) and exploitation (putting to good use what the algorithm has learned). 
 AI needs to solve problems of planning, where current actions indirectly lead to future rewards. 
 These key ideas, which make AI possible, are also important for understanding the politics and economics of AI. Understanding AI as optimization of some measurable objective means that we can understand the politics and economics of AI as a contest over the choice of this objective. The central roles of data and computational infrastructure for building AI underline the importance of the control of  these means of prediction for the control of AI. Understanding the limits on a further expansion of the means of prediction points to potential limits on future improvements of AI based on machine learning. And understanding that AI is, ultimately, simple and broadly accessible, once we step outside the technical jargon, opens the door for broad, democratic deliberation and control of AI.

 Machine Power 
 All AI is designed to maximize some measurable objective. The key question is: What objective? And also, Whose objective? For an engineer, the answer might be given by their boss. The task of an engineer, narrowly construed, is to achieve the objective given to them as well as possible. 
 In this book, however, we are concerned with how AI impacts society. To that end, we need to ask, What should the objective be? Who gets to decide about the objectives of AI in our current society? What resources determine who exerts control over AI? Who might be able to change the objectives maximized by AI, so that these objectives align better with the objectives that we care about as a society? How can we achieve democratic control of AI? What are the obstacles to such change? What ideological detractions might prevent us from maximizing the objectives that we care about?

 Social Welfare 
 The use of AI has consequences. AI can impact who gets into college, who is hired, who receives a medical treatment, and who is approved for a mortgage. AI impacts what news we see on social media and what sources of information we encounter in search engines. AI thereby impacts the course of public democratic debate, and even our very sense of reality. AI automates the tasks of some workers, so that they become unemployed while others become very rich. AI is a tool of mass surveillance and repression, and AI is playing a prominent role in recent wars. 
 People might disagree whether these consequences of AI are good or bad, because opinions differ about what is good. To evaluate the social consequences of AI, we need to have a framework that describes what is good or bad for society, what is just or unjust, and what increases or decreases welfare. Only once such a framework is established can we talk about whether the consequences of a particular use of AI are desirable or undesirable. And only then can we talk about how we should steer the development and deployment of AI in a different direction. 
 So, what makes a good society? This question has been debated for millennia. This book will not be the one to give the final answer to this question, but I will discuss a framework that allows us to think about it more systematically, which is a start. 
 Let’s agree that a society is good if it is good for the people in it—that we can and should evaluate AI in terms of its consequences for people. This assumption might sound trivial, but it has a lot of bite. This seemingly simple assumption has important implications for AI ethics, which I discuss later in the book. This assumption will lead to different conclusions, relative to the way that the questions of AI ethics are usually framed. 
 This assumption will affect how we think about fairness. It leads us to ask how an algorithm impacts people instead of asking whether the algorithm maximized profits. This assumption will affect how we think about data collection and privacy. It leads us to ask what the downstream consequences of learning are instead of asking whether anybody has an individual incentive to withhold data. This assumption will affect how we think about value alignment. It leads us to ask who wins and who loses because of machine decisions, instead of asking whether there is a conflict between a human and a machine. 
 Writers of many different political persuasions would agree with the assumption that a society is good if it is good for the people in it. Not everyone would agree, however. Nationalists of the extreme Right might put the nation over its people. They might think that something is desirable if it serves the greatness and power of their nation, even if the people of their nation must suffer for it. 
 On different grounds, committed libertarians would also disagree with this assumption. A libertarian might argue that the outcomes produced by private property and unfettered markets are just, by definition. For such a libertarian, it does not matter what the consequences of an arrangement are or how well different people are doing. All that matters is whether the process that got us there is the right one, according to their libertarian standards, which implies that everyone got what they deserved. Nationalists and libertarians might therefore disagree with the discussion in the remainder of this book. 
 The assumption that we should care about how well different people are doing compels us to ask the following three questions:  First, Who are the people who matter—that is, whose well-being should we take into account? Second, What does it mean for people to do better or worse—that is, how should we define the welfare of individuals? And third, How much do we care about different people—that is, how should we trade off the welfare of one person relative to another? In particular, we need to ask how much we care about inequality. Once we have answered these three questions, we can define a coherent notion of  social welfare .  Social welfare  is a term that economists use. If social welfare is higher, then, by definition, a society is better, or more just. If social welfare is lower, a society is worse, or less just. We would like social welfare to be as large as possible. Social welfare is what AI should maximize, in an ideal world. More generally, the notion of social welfare provides a systematic framework for discussing the social impact of AI. 
 There are some important implications of the social welfare framework that hold regardless of how we answer the three questions. The social welfare framework makes explicit that there are multiple conflicting objectives across people. What is good for one person might be bad for another person. Not everyone wants the same thing. Each of us might want a different distribution of the cake, and possibly a larger slice for ourselves. And each of us might want a different kind of cake. This explicit recognition that there are multiple objectives across people, and that there are distributional conflicts, stands in contrast to the frameworks of machine learning that we discussed previously. In machine learning, there is a single objective that the algorithm must maximize. In a framework with a single objective, the worst that can happen is that we fail to optimize the objective; in such a framework, we do not have a language to talk about the conflicting objectives of different people. 
 One implication of the social welfare framework is that we need to take sides in distributional conflicts if we want to make normative statements about AI. Any decision, whether human or automated, will in general lead to gains and losses for different people. AI decisions,  in particular, typically increase the welfare of some and decrease the welfare of others. Making any decision means that we are taking a stance on how to trade off these gains and losses, and that we are choosing who wins and who loses. 
 A second implication of the social welfare framework is that we care about the  consequences  of algorithmic decisions. This, again, contrasts with much of the current discourse around the ethics of AI. Asking how a decision will impact different people is very different from asking if a decision can be justified according to some criteria—for instance, whether it was based on acceptable motives or satisfied certain procedural requirements. 
 Who Matters and How Much? 
 The first question to grapple with when thinking about social welfare is, Who matters—that is, whose welfare should be taken into account at all? One possible response is to say everyone’s welfare matters. This sounds reasonable, but it requires us to specify who is included in “everyone.” Does that mean everyone in a given society, as is often presumed? And, if so, who belongs to a society? Is membership in a society defined by national citizenship? Should the randomness of birth really matter in this way, such that a piece of paper (your passport or birth certificate) determines whether your life counts for something? If we don’t want to restrict attention to citizens at the exclusion of everyone else, how about considering all residents of a country, regardless of what papers they possess? Aren’t all residents of a country part of its society? Then again, why should we stop at the arbitrary geographical boundaries of modern nation states, by excluding nonresidents? Shouldn’t all people of the world matter? Even if we agree on this, tricky questions remain. Should future generations matter in their own right? What about people who are not yet born but might or might not be? What if their future birth depends on decisions that are to be made right now, possibly by an algorithm?  And, furthermore, humans are not the only living beings. Should the welfare of animals also matter? Opinions differ on how to answer these questions; I believe that an inclusive and broad answer to the overarching question—Whose life matters?—is usually the right one. 
 Closely entwined with the question of who matters is the question of how much they matter. How should we trade off welfare across people? This is known as the question of  aggregation . Suppose that we know how to measure individual welfare. How do we go from there to an aggregate notion of social welfare? 
 We might agree that an additional dollar for you is good, and an additional dollar for me is good, all else equal—a larger income increases individual welfare. But which of the two is better, giving the dollar to you or to me? How about giving one dollar to you versus two dollars to me? How do we compare an additional dollar for a rich person versus a poor person, for a sick person versus a healthy person? We need to take a stance on these questions of aggregation if we want to make any normative statements about the desirability of policies or technologies. Almost always, algorithmic decisions generate winners and losers. We need to take a side in distributional conflicts, to trade off these gains and losses caused by algorithmic decisions. 
 The literature on  social choice  has tried to derive answers to the question of aggregation by deriving these answers from some first principles, or  axioms . These axioms might state requirements such as “all individuals should be treated the same,” when calculating social welfare, or “making everyone better off should increase social welfare.” There are, however, limits to how far this axiomatic approach gets us in resolving questions of distributive justice. 
 In modern  public finance  economics, a somewhat different approach dominates. Relative to the axiomatic approach of social choice theory, the approach of public finance makes the normative nature of the question of aggregation more explicit. Aggregation is formalized in terms of  welfare weights . Welfare weights describe how much we care about a small increase of each person’s welfare. In settings  that are about the distribution of money, welfare weights describe how much we care about increasing each person’s income by one dollar, for instance. An egalitarian position assigns a large weight to those who are worst off in a society. The ratio between welfare weights for poor versus rich people measures how egalitarian an aggregation is. Similarly, we might wish to assign larger weights to people who are suffering from health problems, or to those who are disadvantaged in some other ways. 
 The choice of welfare weights is important when we want to evaluate the impact of AI. Often, automated decision-making systems are used to allocate a scarce resource or desirable good based on individual features. These systems are used to decide who is invited to a job interview, who is admitted to university, who gets a medical treatment, and so on. These resources in turn are an important ingredient for the welfare of people who might receive them. But which is better—if you get invited to the job interview or if I do? The answer depends on how the job interview would affect your welfare versus how it would affect my welfare. The answer also depends on how much weight we put on increasing your welfare versus mine. We will get back to this point when we consider the issue of algorithmic fairness later in the book. 
 One can also think about welfare weights in reverse, an exercise that is less commonly done. This works as follows: Given the policy choices made in a society, there is a corresponding set of welfare weights that justifies these policy choices. These weights can be thought of as measuring effective social power—whose interests are represented by the powers that be. In the context of AI, we can similarly ask, Given the objectives that AI is maximizing, what weight does it implicitly put on the welfare of different people? Whose well-being does the objective maximized by AI correspond to? And what determines these welfare weights; that is, who has power over AI? This will be discussed later, when we discuss the means of prediction. 
 Individual Welfare 
 In addition to determining who matters, and how much, the social welfare framework requires that we ask how to measure individual welfare. What does it mean for someone to do better or worse? A lot has been written on this question. Let us first discuss the standard answer of economics to this question, and then briefly describe some alternatives. 
 Economists think of welfare in terms of  utility . There are two main ingredients to defining welfare in this way,  constraints  and  preferences . Constraints define the set of options from which people can choose, and preferences specify how much people like these options. The constraints that people face include everything that impacts their set of options. For typical economic choices, these constraints depend on how much income people have, what prices they face, what taxes and transfers and social insurance payments are in place, and so forth. But this set of constraints also includes the myriad non-monetary circumstances of our life, from social norms about the appropriate jobs for someone with our demographics to the air quality in our home area. All these circumstances impact the effective options at people’s disposition. 
 The second ingredient for defining welfare as utility is people’s preferences. Preferences determine what people would choose, between any set of options, if they had the choice. Some preferences might be similar across people. Most people presumably prefer to avoid starvation, to have a roof over their head, and to enjoy social respect. Other preferences might vary across people: Do you prefer cats or dogs? Is your favorite dessert chocolate or strawberries? 
 In the standard framework of welfare economics, preferences play two roles. They first determine what people would choose, if they had the choice. If you prefer chocolate to strawberries, and if I ask you to pick one of the two, then you are going to pick chocolate. This is the  positive  meaning of preferences. But this does not tell us  anything about welfare yet. The second role of preferences, in this framework, is to determine what gives you higher or lower welfare. If you prefer chocolate to strawberries, and I give you chocolate, then your welfare is higher than it would have been had I given you strawberries. This is the  normative  meaning of preferences. The crucial assumption in welfare economics, then, is that positive and normative preferences coincide. The two are assumed to be the same. What you would choose if you had the choice is by assumption that whichever gives you the highest welfare, according to your  revealed preference . This implies that your welfare can be increased by increasing the set of choices at your disposition: The best option from a larger set is at least as good as the best option from a smaller set of choices. Conversely, restricting the set of options from which you can choose can never increase your welfare. 
 In real life, we of course do not always make the choices that are in our well-considered best interest. Complicated choice problems might lead us to make mistakes, especially when it comes to decisions with consequences that unfold over time or over uncertain events with small probabilities. The possibility of such mistakes is sometimes taken as a justification of policy measures that restrict people’s choices—in practice, most often poor people’s choices—supposedly to overcome their “behavioral biases.” What the utility framework entails, by contrast, is a normative assumption that people should choose for themselves, as long as their choices don’t impact other people. 
 The economic notion of welfare as utility is important for thinking about the use of AI for public policy. It allows us to see problems with naive ways of using AI for social good, and it points to better ways of adapting AI to policy applications. There are, for instance, proposals to use AI for labor-market interventions (where AI might help to target services for the unemployed, including to help them find employment), and in public health, in particular in low-income countries (where AI might be used in matters related to fertility policy). In  both examples, some combination of supervised learning and multi-armed bandits could be used to allocate services and treatments. And since machine learning algorithms are designed to maximize observed rewards, that means that in practice algorithms for labor-market policy might aim to maximize the number of unemployed workers who find employment. Algorithms in reproductive-health policy, meanwhile, might aim to minimize the number of pregnancies among the women who visit health clinics. 
 These are both dangerous objectives. Employment could be maximized by forced labor programs. Pregnancies could be minimized by forced sterilizations. There is plenty of historical precedent for both kinds of policies being administered before the advent of AI; just because they would be administered through technological means now doesn’t mean they amount to progress. And I would argue that they are certainly not part of the AI-governed future that we should aim for. 
 In contrast to the objectives of maximizing employment or minimizing fertility, the objective of maximizing welfare, defined in terms of individuals’ utility, always implies that we should aim to maximize the set of effective choices available to participants—not impose outcomes through coercive means. 
 Reducing the income of unemployed job seekers can never increase their utility, even if it increases their probability of accepting a job. Algorithms that actually improve utility would need to improve the bargaining position of the unemployed in the labor market. Similarly, compelling women to take measures that reduce pregnancies by means of sanctions can never increase these women’s utility, even if such measures reduce fertility. Algorithms that maximize utility need to improve the bodily autonomy of women who visit health clinics. If AI is to be used for public policy in domains such as the labor market or public health, we need to carefully choose the rewards maximized by AI. These rewards need to reflect welfare, properly defined; anything short of that might lead to coercion or worse.  Defining such rewards would require a modification of standard approaches in AI, but it is nonetheless feasible. 
 There is another implication of defining welfare as utility. Utility cannot be meaningfully compared between people. Whether I prefer chocolate to strawberries corresponds to a meaningful choice, which might be observable. By contrast, whether I would prefer to be you, or vice versa, is not a choice that we ever get to make. Observable choices therefore do not allow us to pin down whether one of us is better off. Put differently, to compare welfare across people—which is necessary for any social evaluation of policies or algorithms—we need to draw on additional information, and we need to make normative judgments that cannot be derived from people’s preferences or choices. 
 There are alternatives to the utility-based framework, ones that sidestep any reference to preferences, and that do allow us to make comparisons across people. One of these alternatives is the framework proposed by John Rawls in his famous  A Theory of Justice . In addition to fundamental rights, Rawls discusses what he calls  primary goods . These primary goods are basic resources that are considered unambiguously desirable, because they serve as preconditions for living a full life in society. These primary goods include health, civil and political rights, income and wealth, and education, as well as other resources. These primary goods are, in principle, measurable and, as such, allow for comparisons across people. There is no reference to subjective preferences in the definition of primary goods. 
 Building on John Rawls’s approach, Amartya Sen proposed to focus on  capabilities . Capabilities are the effective options available to us. They are like the set of constraints that we encountered in the utility framework. Relative to the focus on primary goods, the capabilities perspective does not require a definite—and likely incomplete—list of relevant resources that merit consideration. At the same time, the capabilities perspective still allows for a comparison across people. Larger capability sets are better. 
 Spirited philosophical debates have taken place around these and other notions of individual welfare. The good news for the purpose of this book is that all these different notions lead to very similar conclusions regarding the evaluation of AI. All these notions of welfare suggest that we should care about the consequence of AI decision-making for the resources or options available to people, and that increasing option sets or resources or capabilities is good for individuals. 
 Social welfare describes what we should maximize as a society. Social welfare describes what AI should maximize from a normative point of view. But what does AI actually maximize? Who gets to decide what AI maximizes, and why?

 The Means of Prediction 
 Before the Industrial Revolution could take off in Great Britain in the eighteenth century, the  enclosures  took place in the English countryside. English peasants had traditionally cultivated large swaths of land that were held in common. But from the Middle Ages onward, and increasingly in the sixteenth and seventeenth centuries, these lands were enclosed: They were turned into the private property of feudal landholders and converted from cultivated land into sheep pastures for the export of wool. The peasants who used to cultivate these fields lost their livelihoods, reacted with a series of revolts that were violently crushed, and ultimately became the dispossessed workforce of the industrializing cities. 
 A similar and even more violent transformation played out in the process of colonial conquest, enslavement, and extraction of natural resources from colonized countries, turning these colonial resources into private property by means of state violence. Karl Marx called these processes  primitive accumulation . This term describes the accumulation of private capital by precapitalist, violent means as a path toward capitalism. Primitive accumulation served as the necessary basis of capitalist development. 
 There are some striking, if less violent, parallels in the development of modern AI. AI, too, relies on its own forms of primitive accumulation. AI requires several resources; some of these resources  used to be in the public domain (the  commons , where nobody had individual property rights over them) but are becoming “enclosed” through various means. Control over these resources means control over AI, and control over AI means control over the objectives that AI maximizes. 
 The previous chapter asked what objectives AI should maximize, from a social perspective. These social objectives are summarized in the notion of social welfare. The counterpart to this  normative  question is the  descriptive  question: What objectives, and whose objectives, does AI actually maximize? Control of these objectives is determined by control over the resources that are required for building AI. I call these resources the  means of prediction  in reference to Marx’s term  means of production , which designates all the resources (capital, land, etc.) that enter the production process. 
 There are four main types of resources, or means of prediction, that are required for the creation of modern AI. These are data, computational infrastructure, technical expertise, and energy. These means of prediction differ from each other along several dimensions. 
 First, the means of prediction differ in terms of who controls them. To what extent did these resources previously exist in the  public domain ? To what extent can they be  privately appropriated , or  enclosed ? And to what extent can they be controlled by a single  monopolist , or a small number of oligopolists? Enclosure leads to a transfer and concentration of wealth and of control. The ongoing processes of appropriation and enclosure constitute a redistribution of wealth, and of control over the objectives of AI. This redistribution of wealth and control is amplified in cases where monopolies or oligopolies are established, so that only one player or a small number of players exert control over the means of prediction. 
 Second, the means of prediction differ in their  scalability . How much can the supply of these resources be increased? Is their availability limited, or can they be expanded arbitrarily? This matters for the potential for future improvements of AI. If all the required inputs  for AI can be scaled up arbitrarily, then AI can continue to improve using current machine learning approaches at an increased scale. But if some of the required resources are fundamentally limited, then there might be hard limits on future improvements of AI. Relatedly, if some resource is limited, then those who control this limited resource might get very rich, and they might exert a lot of influence over the objectives of AI. If a resource can be arbitrarily scaled, by contrast, then it is much harder to derive concentrated power based on exclusive control of this resource. 
 Third, the means of prediction differ in the extent to which they generate  externalities . Economists speak of externalities when the actions of some economic agent, such as an individual or a corporation, have consequences for others, and when the originators of these actions do not bear the costs or benefits of their actions. Externalities might be positive or negative. Pollution is a negative externality, where others are harmed. The training and deployment of large language models, for example, consumes vast amounts of energy and thereby contributes to climate change. Knowledge production by contrast creates positive externalities, where others benefit. Public funding for foundational AI research, and dissemination of the results, might lead to such shared benefits. In unregulated markets, agents engage too much in actions that create negative externalities, and they engage too little in actions that create positive externalities. 
 In the following, I will discuss how far each of the four means of prediction (data, computational infrastructure, technical expertise, and energy) can be privately appropriated and monopolized, the extent to which they are scalable, and whether they generate externalities. 
 Data 
 Data are the most important of the means of prediction. Modern AI is very data-hungry. Data is often the limiting factor determining the  success of AI systems. The data required for modern AI take many different forms, and they originate from many different sources. 
 The data collected from internet users, the number of which has continuously grown since the 1990s, are one source. These data form the basis for the market for targeted advertisements. The profits of companies such as Google and Facebook derive largely from the companies’ ability to sell targeted advertisements based on surveillance of internet users. Google, Facebook, and others collect detailed data on user demographics, consumption preferences, search behavior, and so on. Other companies, such as Netflix and Amazon, greatly rely on user data as well. They collect data to predict which movies users might watch, or which products users might be willing to buy, and at what price. This centrality of surveillance and targeted advertising for the business model of big tech companies has motivated the Harvard Business School social psychologist Shoshana Zuboff to describe the way that the tech industry operates as a new form of capitalism:  surveillance capitalism . 
 Data in the public domain—created for public benefit by countless writers, researchers, coders, and artists—are a second source. Public domain data are available on platforms such as Wikipedia (the popular online encyclopedia that is based on volunteer contributions), arXiv (a platform for the storage of scientific working papers in the public domain, accessible to anyone), and GitHub (a platform for sharing code publicly, much of it under an  open-source  license). These platforms greatly contribute to the common good. They also serve as one of the prime sources of training data for large language models, and for generative AI more broadly. 
 All the works of art and writing that are available on the internet but that are considered someone’s intellectual property under current laws are a third source of data. This includes much of music, literature, news media, and film, which might belong to big music labels or publishing houses. 
 These sources of data differ in terms of their origins, and thus in their legal ownership. Some were born in the public domain, while  others were not. User data are special in that the information did not belong to anyone before someone commodified it—that is, before these users were subjected to online surveillance. (Privacy laws, such as the General Data Protection Regulation in the European Union, might grant users ownership of their personal data in principle, however.) Data on platforms such as Wikipedia or arXiv are explicitly in the commons. This information belongs to everyone, subject to the restrictions of  creative commons  licenses. Works of art and writing often belong to their authors or the descendants of these authors, or to corporate entities such as music labels and publishers. 
 When AI models are trained on different data sources, control over these data becomes effectively enclosed and falls under the control of those who own the AI models. In a memorable and apt metaphor, the science fiction author Ted Chiang has described large language models, such as the one that underpins ChatGPT, as a blurry compressed image of the internet. The companies that control large language models are effectively scraping and compressing much of the internet, including all its contributions from a myriad of authors and creators, then selling it back to us in repackaged form. 
 Such enclosures do not go uncontested. Regulators attempt to limit the nonconsensual surveillance of internet users, to varying degrees of success. (We will later consider the question of privacy, and the extent to which individual privacy rights can provide protection from the potential harms of AI.) Important legal battles are also fought over the use of media that are protected by intellectual property rights. Larger corporate players, such as news media companies or music labels, are fighting back against the appropriation of their data by AI companies. The outcomes of these battles will have important consequences for the distribution of AI-based wealth, and for control rights over AI. 
 Turning from questions of control to the future potential of AI, the key point to note is that different sources of data afford different degrees of scalability. The potential success of machine learning hinges  on the size of available datasets, relative to the complexity of the underlying learning problems that the algorithm is trying to solve. At one extreme, in terms of scalability, we have data generated via simulation, such as the simulated games that were used to train reinforcement learning algorithms like AlphaGo. In domains where data can be generated by simulation, there are no limits to the machine-learning-based approach, at least in terms of data availability. 
 A close second to simulated data, in terms of scalability, is the unstructured text data available on the internet. The comprehensive scraping of these data has powered much of the recent spectacular advances of large language models. Conceivably, however, limits might soon be reached in terms of the amount of text data of sufficient quality that can be made available. This suggests that further improvements of language models might not come from increased sizes of training datasets. Also available in abundance, relative to the complexity of the underlying learning problem, are data on internet users. The rather mundane task of maximizing ad clicks based on user data is arguably the largest and most profitable application of machine learning to date. In 2023, Meta reportedly received over $131 billion in ad revenues, while Google’s ad revenues totaled $238 billion; see also  figure 2  in  chapter 5 . 
 At the opposite extreme, in terms of scalability, are data for specialized supervised learning problems, like the diagnosis of rare diseases. In domains such as medical diagnostics, there is a fundamental limit to how many instances of a problem can be observed. There are corresponding hard limits to the potential for statistical learning. Another example where the data available for statistical learning are fundamentally limited is macroeconomic forecasting. There is only one instance of the United States in the financial crisis of 2008. As a result, statistical learning might never get very far in predicting similar crises, no matter how sophisticated the statistical models used are. 
 A similar point applies to human genetics, even though this is less  obvious. After the enthusiasm following the first complete sequencing of a human genome in the Human Genome Project in 2003, high expectations were attached to the medical potential of genetics. Gene sequencing has become radically cheaper since then. But there are hard limits on how much genetic data can be generated—there are only so many humans alive. Given the very large dimension of the genome, sequencing eight billion humans might not be enough to draw reliable conclusions on the complex interactions between the many different genes. (Experimental manipulation in the lab, where single genes are edited and the consequences are observed, appears to be more successful.) Yet another domain where approaches based on machine learning have not delivered on their promises thus far is self-driving cars. Immense resources have been dedicated to the collection of data from the vantage point of self-driving cars, but the results have not reached the level of reliability needed for fully autonomous driving in urban traffic. It appears that the complexity of real-life traffic situations is simply too large for this approach. The future will tell if fully autonomous driving can be realized across different environments. 
 Do data generate externalities? Yes, very much so, especially data on individuals. In many ways, the whole point of learning is the externalities, where the data for one person are used to make predictions for another person. Machine learning seeks to recover patterns across observations, not individual observations. In supervised learning, the goal is to recover the relationship between features  X  and outcomes  Y  across people; this goal is very different from the goal of learning individual instances of  X  or  Y . 
 Suppose, for example, that a business collects data on people’s individual features  X , as well as on the price  Y  that individuals are willing to pay for some product. Based on these data, the business learns that certain people, for instance those living in certain neighborhoods, have a high willingness to pay. The business then charges  a correspondingly higher price to people with a high predicted willingness to pay. In this example, people who live in certain neighborhoods are harmed by the collection of data  X  and  Y  for other people in these neighborhoods. The people who shared their data thus created an externality. 
 The centrality of externalities for learning implies that individuals typically do not partake in the harms or benefits from data collection whenever they share their data with third parties. We will discuss this point at length when considering the question of privacy. For now, let’s just note that markets or individual property rights cannot lead to appropriate levels of data collection. Answers to this problem therefore require collective governance. 
 Computational Infrastructure 
 Data alone do not make AI. To process data, we need computers. In application domains of AI where data are abundant, such as game play or large language models, the limiting factor for AI is usually computational capacity. 
 The heart of a typical computer is its central processing unit, or CPU. The CPU is where all the actual calculations take place that let a computer run. Machine learning, and especially deep learning, requires a very large number of calculations during the training of new models. The calculations involved are surprisingly simple, however. For the most part, these calculations reduce to a large number of multiplications and additions. This is especially true for modern deep learning. Specialized processors have been developed in recent years that are able to process a huge number of multiplications and additions in parallel (at the same time). These processors are called graphics processing units (GPUs) and tensor processing units (TPUs). GPUs were originally developed for computer graphics and gaming. The three-dimensional rendering of graphics requires  a great deal of multiplications and additions that are like those needed for training neural nets. GPUs and TPUs are essential for modern deep learning. 
 Even though there are a few companies that can produce these chips, the market for GPUs is almost entirely controlled by one company, NVIDIA, which currently has a 90 percent market share. This might change, and the monopoly might not last forever. There are, however, massive economies of scale in the production of chips. The fixed costs required to develop and produce chips are enormous. Large expenses are needed before the first chip can be produced. 
 Whenever fixed costs are large, monopolies are more likely to arise. Monopolies are able to raise prices over production costs, and thereby to appropriate more of the surplus (profits) of some activity. Correspondingly, it seems likely that a sizable share of profits from AI will be appropriated by NVIDIA, and possibly other chip producers, for the near future. 
 There is also a geopolitical counterpart to this concentration of power in chip production. The United States and China, in particular, are competing for control of chip production, for both economic and military reasons. In 2022, the United States passed the CHIPS and Science Act, which aims to concentrate chip production on US soil. Other measures are taken, as well, to prevent the transfer of knowledge and of the inputs needed for production. 
 Externalities appear less important for computer hardware compared to the other means of prediction. One exception to this is the publicly funded research that enabled the semiconductor revolution and subsequent improvements of processors. Those who conducted this research, and their public funders, did not reap the profits from these innovations—private companies did. Another exception is the mining of rare earth minerals, which are needed in the production of semiconductors and electronics more generally, as well as the environmental and political costs of such mining. Competition over control  of mines is fueling the devastating military conflicts in eastern Congo, in particular. 
 The production of chips delivers the basic hardware needed for modern AI. The deployment of this hardware requires large server farms. These server farms are typically run by the big tech companies (Amazon, Microsoft, Google, and the Chinese company Alibaba) and rented out as part of their cloud computing business. While dominated by a few large players, this sector is more competitive than chip production. Cloud service providers effectively act as intermediaries between hardware producers and energy providers on the one hand and companies training AI models on the other. The size of these providers allows them to scale the required computing power quickly. This was the main asset that Microsoft brought to their union with OpenAI, for example. 
 Technical Expertise 
 Imagine you had the data and the hardware needed to train an AI system. Would you be able to do it? Maybe, but most people would not know where to start. Training AI models requires expertise, in addition to data and hardware. 
 Expertise is a funny thing. All the key ideas that enable machine learning and AI are widely known. The most important of these ideas were discussed earlier in this book. At a more technical and detailed level, too, most knowledge about AI is in the public domain, in the form of research articles that are available on platforms such as arXiv. 
 And yet, expertise on AI is scarce and highly sought after. Expertise in AI also requires practical knowledge, acquired through extensive amounts of tinkering. AI engineers tend to be engaged in a great deal of trial-and-error learning, testing different model architectures and algorithm specifications until some are found that work well.  The situation has some parallels to the alchemist Paracelsus and his recipe for creating a homunculus. Paracelsus described what you need to do, and you can just go to your nearest library and read about it. But that doesn’t mean you or I could actually create a homunculus. (Though, of course, the same might be true of Paracelsus.) Similarly, the basic ideas of AI are well known and widely available. But the details of implementation are a lot more arcane and specific to the various applications. There is much trial and error and tweaking that is required to get large neural nets to work. 
 This brings us back to the history of the commons and their enclosure. Much of the foundational research in AI, as in many other fields, has been done using public funds. This foundational research was an essential precondition for the present boom in AI. But the final implementations and arcane alchemist recipes of the tech companies are usually subject to trade secrecy and patents. 
 Is expertise scalable? In the long run, yes, but not necessarily in the short run. In the short run, there is a limited supply of experts with sufficient training to satisfy the demands of the AI boom. Correspondingly, AI specialists command high wages and some decision-making powers, at least for the moment. One consequence of this limited supply of experts is that it will be hard, for the time being, for public regulators to compete with the wages offered by private tech companies for AI experts. This in turn creates difficulties for democratic governance and oversight. This limited ability of public regulators to compete with the financial resources of the private sector that they are supposed to oversee is a familiar pattern from other industries, such as the financial sector. 
 The supply of expertise and of experts is considerably more elastic in the long run, however. Public attention and high wages will induce more people to enter the field and to become experts on the foundations of AI. The basic research is widely available, as are good educational resources. In the references section of this book, for instance, several freely available machine learning textbooks are  listed. The supply of expertise and experts will adapt to the high demand for such expertise. It is therefore unlikely that machine learning experts will maintain the same degree of leverage over AI, or that they will continue to receive the same share of the generated wealth, in the long run. 
 Just as the benefits of public research are often privately appropriated, knowledge production generates externalities more generally. This includes research on the foundations of machine learning and AI. Research is a typical example of an activity with positive externalities. If someone engages in relevant research and publishes the results, then others can benefit from this, and can build on the generated knowledge, in a way that does not directly benefit the original researcher. Markets therefore typically undersupply foundational research. 
 Energy 
 Modern AI consumes a lot of energy. To train large neural networks, huge server farms need to process data for extended periods of time. And deployment of these networks is increasingly energy-hungry, as well, especially for the recent generation of “reasoning” based language models. Artificial neural nets are much more energy-hungry than the biological models on which they were originally based. The International Energy Agency estimates that in 2022 data centers already made up 2 percent of global energy use, and that this share is likely to double by 2026, to a staggering thousand terawatt-hours. This is almost twice the entire electricity consumption of countries such as France or Germany, based on Ember and Energy Institute data. A large and growing share of this energy consumption is due to AI. 
 This energy hunger has deeper causes, according to the following argument by Geoffrey Hinton, one of the pioneers of deep learning (and winner of the 2024 Nobel Prize in Physics). Deep learning—and  machine learning more generally—uses digital computers. Digital computers consist of many transistors, which are used to store and process binary information. Information processing in digital computers proceeds essentially without errors. The stored information does not randomly degrade. But this absence of errors comes at a cost. We could run transistors at lower energy levels, but doing so would introduce random errors in computing, for physical reasons. These random errors would depend on idiosyncratic properties of the hardware used. With such random errors, anything that is learned during the training of a machine learning model becomes specific to the hardware used. Put differently, we would not be able to copy the estimated model to a different computer and expect it to behave in the same way. Learning would no longer be easily transferable. 
 But this is exactly what happens in our brains! Biological neural nets, and human brains in particular, operate at much lower energy levels than digital computers. Anything else would not have been evolutionarily feasible. It would have required way too much food to maintain more energy-hungry brains. Consequently, our brains are error prone, in the sense that there are random fluctuations in the behavior of neurons. Anything that we learn is stored in a way that is specific to the “wetware” of our brains. We cannot simply transfer knowledge from one brain to another. In fact, the human process of teaching and learning is a rather tedious and inefficient affair, relative to the simplicity of copying a file from one computer to another. Much of our knowledge disappears with our death, because there is no equivalent to the separation of software and hardware that is so important for digital computing. To summarize, the information learned by AI is separable from the hardware used to train its models, it can be copied without error, and thus it is potentially immortal. By contrast, the information learned by our brains is tied to its physical basis, cannot be easily copied, and thus is mortal. But the  immortality of digital information comes at a much higher energy cost, according to Hinton’s argument. 
 Is energy a limiting factor for AI? At a global scale, we have clearly consumed more energy from fossil fuels than the planet can support, and we have produced an unsustainable amount of carbon emissions. To reduce the risks of catastrophic climate change, big reductions of fossil fuel consumption are necessary. To achieve this, the supply of alternative, renewable energy sources needs to be expanded. The AI boom certainly does not help with the goal of reduced energy consumption. 
 From the perspective of corporate actors, energy is an important part of the cost of training and deploying AI models. At the same time, it seems unlikely that these corporations will be unable to procure energy at the scale needed, even if energy consumption must be reduced from the perspective of humanity. It thus seems unlikely that, in the foreseeable future, the scalability of energy use will be a limiting factor for the further development of AI or that control of energy resources implies control of AI. 
 Energy production is a classic example of negative externalities in economics. Polluters and carbon emitters impose large costs for humanity, at a global scale, but the polluters and emitters bear very little of these costs themselves. Markets therefore lead to excessive pollution and excessive carbon emissions. One sensible policy response to this excessive energy consumption by AI companies, but also more broadly, would be  carbon taxation . By making companies bear some of the costs of the pollution that they generate, excessive energy consumption can be reduced. Carbon taxation has proven to be a politically difficult project, however. Complementary policy proposals involve public investments in alternative energy production, energy storage and transmission capacity, and energy efficiency, in the hope of simultaneously creating jobs while reducing the likelihood of catastrophic climate change. 
 Those who control the means of prediction will control the future of AI; their objectives are the objectives that AI will pursue. Their objectives might or might not be aligned with the objectives of society at large—that is, with the maximization of social welfare. If they do not align, what is to be done? And who is to do it? Should we simply appeal to the good intentions of those who control the means of prediction? Or do we need to think more systematically about the set of actors who might possibly align the objectives of AI with the maximization of social welfare?

 Agents of Change 
 The development and deployment of AI raises countless ethical and societal challenges. Any meaningful plan for addressing these challenges requires figuring out who might bring about change. 
 One natural possibility is to appeal to the people who develop and deploy AI. We might ask them to take the ethical challenges of AI into consideration. And, indeed, considerable efforts are being dedicated to raising awareness of ethical questions among engineers, and to developing guidelines and teaching curricula for them. The purpose of these efforts is to train engineers and computer scientists on questions of ethics in general, and of AI ethics in particular. 
 This seems like an honorable and worthwhile endeavor. We might hope that this endeavor is the answer to the ethical and societal challenges posed by AI. But there are reasons to be skeptical. How much can this approach really achieve? Individual corporate engineers and corporate managers are not free to choose the objectives of their work. Private corporations in a capitalist economy are first and foremost profit-maximizing entities. Corporations are legally bound to maximize profits for their owners. And if a corporation does not act in a profit-maximizing manner, then the mechanics of market competition will ultimately lead to its demise and replacement by other corporations—corporations that do maximize profits. In this context, the individual employees of private corporations might at best  have some wiggle room at the margins to act ethically when doing so does not impact profits negatively. 
 No doubt, many corporate employees have the best intentions and genuinely wish to do the right thing with regards to AI and more broadly. But the overarching objective of their corporate employers is the maximization of profits. There is a tension between these two facts, between the desire for ethical behavior and the context that requires profit maximization. Many people who face this tension attempt to resolve it by justifying ethical behavior in terms of its contribution to profit maximization. 
 Consider, for example, the debates about algorithmic bias and discrimination. In these debates, the question whether there is discrimination is often reduced to the question whether profits could be increased by treating disadvantaged groups better: Is there a “business case for diversity”? For instance, could a hiring algorithm increase profits by hiring more women? Another example is the generation of social media feeds. One might be worried about the impact of click-maximizing algorithms on democracy, teenage mental health, and so on. Concerns about these questions are often reduced to the question whether clickbait, which maximizes short-term clicks, hurts profits in the long run by decreasing user engagement. 
 This rather circumscribed version of ethics, where ethical behavior needs to be rationalized by its contribution to profits, ties in well with the technical framework of machine learning as optimization: The circumscribed version of ethics equates ethical problems with a failure to maximize profits, just as machine learning engineers might equate problems of AI with a failure to optimize. If the ultimate goal of algorithmic optimization is to maximize profits, then both perspectives are the same: Problems of AI must be understood as failures to act in a profit-maximizing way. 
 If this diagnosis is correct, that corporate engineers are constrained by the requirements of profit maximization, then it is unlikely that a simple appeal to the ethics of individual corporate engineers will allow us to address the societal challenges posed by AI.  Such an appeal will not allow us to align the objectives of AI with the maximization of social welfare. What is to be done, then? Who can possibly bring us closer to such an alignment of objectives, so that AI is beneficial for society at large? Who can be an agent of change? Potential agents of change need to have the  interests ,  values , and  capacity  to align AI objectives with social welfare. 
 What determines capacity for change? The objectives of AI are chosen by those who control the means of prediction, including data, computational infrastructure, technical expertise, and energy. As long as AI objectives are chosen to maximize profits, aligning AI objectives with social welfare requires aligning profit maximization with social welfare. To have the capacity to effect change, agents of change therefore need to have strategic leverage over the profits of the actors who control the means of prediction. Leverage can take many forms: potential strikes and consumer boycotts, bad press and litigation, and regulation and legal constraints. All these levers can potentially be used to better align profit maximization with social welfare by selectively imposing costs on those who control the means of prediction. 
 The task of aligning AI objectives with social welfare will not be accomplished by one set of agents alone. We need to consider a broad range of potential agents of change, including workers and unions, consumer advocates, journalists and judges, policymakers and politicians. What follows is a tentative survey of these various actors, of their capacity for effecting change, and of their economic interests. This should give an overview of the strategic landscape, and of the potential for short- and medium-term change in the governance of AI. 
 Workers 
 One set of potential agents is  workers . There are many different groups of workers who are affected by AI; they have different interests and different leverage. There are the well-paid workers of the  tech industry, including engineers and AI experts, who are most directly involved in the development of AI. There are the low-paid and mostly invisible click workers who labor in container farms across the Global South, and whose work in labeling and processing data is an essential precondition for supervised learning and AI. There are the gig workers, in taxi companies such as Uber, and on platforms such as MTurk, and the warehouse workers of Amazon, whose workflow is tightly controlled by algorithms. And there are workers throughout the economy, outside the tech industry, whose work will be impacted by the introduction of AI tools in the workplace. As individuals, none of these workers have much leverage. Workers who deviate from the imperatives of profit maximization can be easily replaced. But collectively, these various groups of workers do have potential power. 
 Among these groups of workers, the highly paid engineers of the tech industry appear to be the most powerful—the workers who might have the most leverage over the development of AI. These workers do make consequential decisions about the development of technology in their everyday work. They are well informed about the state of AI, and they are less easily replaced than other workers. As individuals, these engineers nonetheless have limited power, but if they were to act collectively, they would have considerably more leverage. Collective organization of tech workers is, however, hampered by the predominant worldviews and self-conceptions of these workers. While many of them might identify as “liberal,” they tend to be skeptical of organized labor, and are more likely to imagine themselves as the future CEO of a successful startup than as a rank-and-file member of a union. These aspirations, combined with stock options and similar incentive programs, tend to align their perceived interests with the profit maximization of their employers. 
 The aspirations and self-image of tech workers might change because of the concentration and ossification of corporate power in the tech industry. Because of this concentration and ossification, the  “new economy” already looks a lot like the “old economy” in many places. And there are cases of successful organizing of (future) tech workers under the banner of ethical or political goals. In 2019, for example, computer science students at seventeen leading universities pledged to not accept jobs at the data and AI company Palantir, so long as this company worked with the US Immigration and Customs Enforcement (ICE). These students refused to work for a company that helps ICE to detect undocumented migrants, thereby enabling workplace raids, incarceration in camps, and deportation of vulnerable populations. 
 Click workers  are as essential for the development of AI as engineers. Relative to the latter, however, they are much lower paid, almost invisible to public perception, and dispersed across the globe. To understand the importance of click workers, recall that a key ingredient for supervised learning is the outcome labels  Y  attached to the features  X  of training examples. Key breakthroughs in image recognition were based on the well-known ImageNet dataset, which was initially compiled by academics. This dataset contains more than fourteen million images, manually labeled to belong to one of roughly twenty thousand categories. The labeling was done by anonymous click workers on the MTurk platform. Click workers are spread across many countries. They are, for the most part, easily replaced. In richer countries, they might work from their living rooms, juggling care obligations and low-paid online jobs, which means that they never meet other click workers in person. In lower-income countries, they might work in container farms that are quickly built and quickly relocated. These factors make it difficult for click workers to become organized and powerful actors that exert control over the development of AI, at least in the near future. 
 Gig workers , such as Uber drivers, also seem to have relatively little power over tech companies, and the same holds true for  warehouse workers . For the most part, thanks to legal fictions that were carefully constructed by companies such as Uber, the law does not even  recognize gig workers as employees, instead labeling them as self-employed “driver partners” and the like. (There are some promising recent changes, however, including the EU directive on platform work of April 2024 and the US Department of Labor ruling of January 2024, which might increase the likelihood that some gig workers will be classified as employees.) Because of their legal status as being self-employed, gig workers do not even have the traditional protections of labor law on their side, nor do they have the option to (officially) unionize. The type of service work undertaken by gig workers further serves to isolate them, since they do not share a physical workplace, for the most part. Warehouse workers, by contrast, are more likely to know each other and to spend time together given their shared workplace and common work hours. 
 Despite the legal and practical impediments that they face, gig workers and warehouse workers seem more likely to exert organized pressure on tech companies than other groups of workers. The same technologies that serve to disperse and isolate workers might be used by them to connect and organize. Workers on the MTurk platform, for instance, have many online discussion forums where they discuss the employers and jobs that are available on MTurk. Both service work, such as taxi services, and warehouse work are, furthermore, geographically localized and cannot be easily outsourced across the globe. This means that it can be enough to organize the drivers in one city, or the workers in one warehouse, to exert meaningful pressure for improved working conditions. These workers have an interest to change the way that algorithmic, AI-based management governs their working lives. Workers’ efforts to organize for their rights might furthermore enlist the support of local municipalities. Municipal support is again effective in this case because the work in question cannot be easily relocated. 
 Tech workers, click workers, gig workers, and warehouse workers are all directly connected to the tech industry. But AI also has ramifications throughout the entire economy, and workers outside the tech industry are very much impacted by the use of AI tools in the workplace.  These workers do not have direct leverage over the  development  of AI, but they might have leverage over its  deployment . These workers, furthermore, have good reasons to care how AI is deployed. 
 What is the impact of technologies such as AI on the demand for different types of workers, their wages, and their bargaining power more generally? Economic theory suggests that the answer to this question is not preordained. AI might increase or decrease the economic well-being of different workers. This point has been emphasized by MIT economics professor Daron Acemoglu and his coauthors, among others. According to standard economic theory, it is quite possible that productivity grows while general prosperity does not increase. But it is also possible to have broadly shared welfare increases thanks to new technologies. This is discussed in detail in  chapter 16 . 
 Because the impact of AI on worker well-being is underdetermined in this way, and depends on how companies choose to use it, workers have a strong interest to have a say in the deployment of AI, to make sure that it leads to broadly shared welfare increases. Whether workers have the capacity to make this happen depends on the context. Strong and broad-based unions might be able to ensure that AI is deployed in a way that is broadly welfare improving. Relatedly, legally mandated codetermination at the company level, which exists in some countries such as Germany, gives workers additional levers for influencing the deployment of AI. 
 Consumers 
 Another set of agents with potential power over tech companies are  consumers . Consumer demand is essential for profits. This is true when consumers pay directly for goods and services, such as taxi rides, online retail purchases, or movie streaming. But this is also true for search engines or social media platforms, where profits hinge on the exposure of consumers to targeted ads. 
 Historically, people have been less likely to collectively organize  as consumers than they have as workers. One important reason is that work and wages are central for people’s life experience and economic well-being. The same is not true for most consumption goods. Most consumption goods are only a small fraction of our household budget. There are notable exceptions, however, including housing and food. Both housing and food make up an important portion of household budgets, and both have spawned collective political action. 
 Another reason why workers have been more likely to organize than consumers is that work is often a collective and shared experience, with workers interacting at their place of work. Consumption is usually more individualized. Housing is again an exception, where tenants might know their neighbors well. 
 Consumer organizing in the digital realm is hampered by the fact that the services provided by the tech industry are not, typically, a large part of household consumption, and by the fact that consumption of these services is not a collective experience. Social media can sometimes counteract these factors. Social media makes it easier for outrage about the behavior of tech companies to go viral. The relatively small importance of online services for household consumption also makes consumption boycotts less costly for consumers. Because of these two factors, the threat point of being “canceled” due to viral outrage might act as an important check on AI companies, even if sustained and organized action by consumers in the digital realm is less likely. 
 Another factor that favors consumers as agents of change is the law. In important domains, the law privileges the welfare of consumers over the welfare of workers. This is the case for the regulation of monopolies and  market power , in particular. For several decades, market power has been almost exclusively assessed in terms of its impact on consumer welfare, with no consideration to the welfare of workers. Corporate mergers, for instance, are prohibited if they are deemed to make consumers worse off, while no consideration tends to be given to whether the mergers make workers worse off. 
 Both individual and collective action by consumers can be made more difficult by  network effects . Network effects are present whenever a service is more useful if more other people are using the same service. Social media platforms are an obvious example. It is not particularly fun or interesting to be on a social media platform if no one else is there. When there are network effects, it is very hard to switch to a new platform. Everyone on the old platform would need to switch to the same new platform at the same time, which is nearly impossible to coordinate. 
 There is a twist to this argument, however. Network effects are not an unavoidable fact of nature. Network effects are actively created by companies throughout the economy, particularly in the tech sector. This is a point that has been emphasized by the activist and author Cory Doctorow. To illustrate this point, compare the experience of switching mobile phone providers to the experience of switching social media platforms. When you subscribe to a mobile phone provider, you can call telephones on the network of any other provider. This makes it relatively easy to switch providers as an individual consumer, without changing the set of people you might call or who might call you. By contrast, if you switch to a different social media platform, you lose all your friends or followers, and you stop receiving updates from the friends who were in your original network. This makes it hard to switch platforms. Crucially, however, there is no technical reason why it should not be possible to follow users on a platform other than the one that you are using, in the same way that you can call phones on other networks. But following users on another platform is actively prevented by social media companies. These companies use both legal and technical barriers to prevent switching. Their goal is to exert maximum control over their users, with the ultimate purpose of maximizing profits from ad sales. Once a sufficient user base with sufficient network effects has been established, making it hard for users to leave, the quality of services can be reduced, and the focus can be shifted to profit extraction. Cory Doctorow coined the memorable term  enshittification  for this process,  where networks are first artificially closed off and then maximal profit is extracted from users. (This term was chosen by the American Dialect Society as word of the year 2023.) 
 The Media and Public Opinion 
 Workers and consumers can affect corporate decisions, and thus the objectives of AI, because they can withhold their labor or their consumption, which threatens corporate profits. The power of the news media, and of public opinion more broadly, is less direct. It operates by influencing other potential agents of change. Public opinion is nonetheless of great importance to corporate profits. This is because public debate can impact the behavior of workers and of consumers, and because public debate is a precondition of democratic decision-making, in the state and beyond. The shapers of public opinion, particularly the news media, are thus potential agents of change in their own right. 
 Swings in public opinion might affect the supply of workers—as in the Palantir boycott mentioned above. And swings in public opinion can lead to swings in consumer demand, even absent organized boycott campaigns. Tech companies, like other companies, put a lot of resources into the maintenance of their brand image, and into public relations more broadly. Bad press can lead to substantial drops in users and thus in ad sales. 
 Network effects make it hard for consumers to leave a platform or service on their own. These network effects are actively created to reduce consumer power. But there is a flip side to these network effects. They can also amplify collective drops in consumer demand. When, for example, the use of Twitter dropped after Elon Musk’s takeover, this meant that Twitter became less interesting to a lot of other users—even if they did not directly worry about Musk’s changes to the platform, or his political goals. This created an opening for networks such as Mastodon (a nonprofit) and Bluesky (operated  as a benefit corporation). Network effects can thus amplify swings in consumer demand, and thereby increase the importance of public opinion. 
 Both the power and the limitations of consumer demand as a lever for change are reflected in what is called  corporate social responsibility  (CSR). CSR is a blanket term for the consideration of social, environmental, or ethical goals as part of a company’s objectives. For the most part, and quite explicitly, CSR is considered a part of public relations, marketing, and brand management. CSR serves profits insofar as it prevents consumer backlash, boycotts, poor brand image, and the like. The importance of CSR for public relations and marketing shows that the normative concerns of consumers matter—otherwise there would be no reason to manage brand image in this way. But this reason why CSR matters could also point toward shortcuts: If all that matters is image, it might be that an image campaign suffices without substantive changes to corporate behavior. AI companies and their CEOs might, for instance, present themselves in public as gravely concerned about the social impact of AI, the existential risks of AI, and the like, while proceeding with their marketization of AI in just the same way they would absent such concerns. This is arguably what Sam Altman of OpenAI has been doing. 
 The media and public discourse around AI can impact the behavior of people as  workers  and as  consumers , individually and collectively. But public debate can also impact the behavior of people as  citizens —as participants in collective democratic deliberation. Much has been written, since the times of ancient Athens, about the nature of true democracy, and there exists a broad range of views about what makes a democracy. Many writers agree, however, that real democracy cannot be reduced to the casting of an electoral ballot every four years. Instead,  public deliberation  on important issues is a key precondition for a functioning democracy. 
 Applied to AI, this means that the governance of AI cannot be simply delegated to experts and technocrats, even if they were to  be appointed by elected governments. Instead, we need to develop a shared public understanding of what AI is, how it works, and how we want to govern it as a society. We need to come to agreements on what the objectives of AI should be, and who should control the means of prediction. To this end, the news media are necessary as agents of change. It is to this end that this book aims to contribute. 
 The State and the Law 
 Democratic decisions can take place in many spheres, but the most important of these spheres is the state. Within the state, there are many different potential agents of change, including the courts, regulatory agencies, and legislators. These agents in turn have many potential tools at their disposition that can be used to shape the distribution and control of the means of prediction, and the deployment and objectives of AI. 
 Who controls the means of prediction is not a fact of nature. Control rights are shaped by political decisions. Enclosures do not happen in a void. Enclosures are typically backed and moderated by the power of the state. Property rights are guaranteed by the government monopoly of (legitimate) violence. Enclosures might also be enabled by (selective) inaction of the state. Several branches of the law are important for assigning property rights and control over the means of prediction. 
 There is, first,  intellectual property  law, including  copyright ,  patenting , and  trademarks . Important legal contests are under way regarding the interpretation of intellectual property in the context of data collection for the purpose of training AI. The business models of several industries that were built on intellectual property are being jeopardized by AI, especially by generative AI. This includes the news media, the music industry, movies, and television. The collapse of old business models in these industries started with the expansion of the internet, and it is continuing at an accelerated pace with the  development of AI. These industries are in conflict with the tech industry over control of the means of prediction. 
 Another domain of the law that is relevant for the assignment of control over data is  privacy law . While intellectual property is important for the harvesting of news articles, music, or films by AI companies, privacy law is important for the harvesting of user data for purposes such as targeted advertising or individualized pricing. We will discuss later to what extent privacy law can be effective in governing the collection of individual-level data by assigning individual-level control rights. There are strong reasons to think that privacy law cannot ultimately be effective, because of the centrality of  data externalities  for machine learning and AI. Privacy law is nonetheless an important domain where control rights over the means of prediction are being contested. 
 Next, there is  monopoly law , and more generally the regulation of market power. In the late nineteenth century in the United States, monopoly law was devised as a tool against the concentrated economic and political power of corporate trusts. Reining in the power of trusts was considered necessary to guarantee the survival of the American republic. Since the 1970s, this objective of limiting concentrated power has been narrowed, however, to the so-called consumer welfare standard. According to the consumer welfare standard, the concentration of corporate power is legitimate, and even desirable, if it decreases prices or increases supply for consumers, regardless of its impact on workers (weakening their bargaining power), its impact on the process of democracy (outsized corporate influence on government decision-making), or its impact on future legal enforcement (companies becoming “too big to jail”). This limited consumer welfare standard has been put in question recently, notably by some appointees of the Biden administration such as the legal scholar Lina Khan, who embrace a broader interpretation of antimonopoly enforcement, returning to some of the original commitments of antitrust enforcement. 
 Less well-known than antimonopoly laws, but very important for reining in the concentrated corporate power of tech companies, are  standards  and  interoperability  requirements, which might for instance posit that it needs to be possible for users on one platform to follow users on another platform. We mentioned network effects above. Network effects are hard to maintain if interoperability is guaranteed. Interoperability is so ubiquitous in our economy that it is almost invisible. Interoperability is maintained by standards. Think about the fact that you can plug electric devices from arbitrary suppliers into your electric outlets, the fact that you can call phones in networks maintained by different providers, or the fact that you can send emails to computers using different operating systems. Similar standards could be required for tech companies that are in the business of harvesting user data, which would dramatically lower their monopoly power. To give just one example, social media platforms could be required to provide a technical interface (an API, or application programming interface) that allows their users to switch to new platforms while still following the accounts on the old platform and allowing users on the old platform to follow them on the new one. From a technical perspective, such a requirement would be easily implementable. 
 Another legal tool, more established in some European countries than in the English-speaking world, is rules regarding  codetermination . Codetermination, which often applies to larger companies, gives workers a formal voice in the management of these companies, where a certain share of votes in company boards is reserved for union representatives. Additionally, codetermination gives workers representation in establishment-level decisions. Shared control over corporate decisions implies shared control over the objectives of AI, in both AI development and AI deployment. Such shared control might have large implications for the labor-market impact of AI. Shared control might favor uses of AI that augment worker capabilities, rather than automating workers away. 
 The legal tools discussed thus far directly impact the distribution of control over the means of prediction. There are many other legal domains that impose limits on control rather than redistributing control. One example is  antidiscrimination laws . These laws can limit the legality of discriminatory decisions by algorithms. These laws are important for discussions about algorithmic bias and fairness. Another important domain is  labor law , which can impose restrictions on surveillance in the workplace, and on algorithmic management by AI, for instance for gig workers such as those driving for Uber. 
 These are some of the existing levers that courts and regulators can use to rein in concentrated power over the means of prediction, and to allow for more broadly shared control over the objectives of AI. Given these levers, courts and regulators should be addressed as potential agents of change. But we should not restrict our imagination to existing tools and levers. To effectively govern AI, new tools and social arrangements will need to be invented and enacted. 
 Democratic Control 
 We have surveyed a range of actors who might move the objectives of AI in a socially beneficial direction, and the factors that act for or against them becoming agents of change. These actors might move the objectives of AI by strategically threatening profits and redistributing control. Such strategic threats are important in the short term. But over the long term, we might wish to establish a social order where strategic threats to profits are not necessary for ensuring the choice of beneficial objectives. We might wish to instead establish a social order where people have a say over their own fate. Put differently, we might wish to establish a  democratic  social order. 
 Democratic control of AI would be desirable for both instrumental and intrinsic reasons. Instrumentally, democratic control allows us to achieve an alignment between the objectives of AI systems and social welfare. Intrinsically, democratic control is desirable for  its own sake. Democratic control would allow us to realize the ideal of self-governance, or  autonomy . Autonomy is the ability to effectively determine one’s own fate. In a society where we all depend on each other, and don’t just live on our own separate islands, autonomy must be exercised collectively. Collective self-governance means democracy. 
 Democracy is necessary since we need to make decisions that don’t just impact ourselves. Who should have a say in democratic decisions about AI? Arguably, in the first place, those who are impacted by AI decisions. This might be a group of people who interact locally, such as those in a neighborhood, a school, or a workplace. This might be on the national level. This might also be on the global level. Across the world, democracy has become a broadly accepted ideal—but only within the constrained sphere of the political, and at the level of nation states. The ideal of democracy could be applied much more broadly, across society and economy. In practice, rather than being governed by democratic decision-making, society and economy tend to be governed by the rule of those who have the money—that is, those who have property rights. In the workplace, in particular, where most adults spend half their waking hours, you don’t get to elect your manager; instead, you must follow their orders. The promise of democracy, thus, has only been partially fulfilled. We could imagine a world where spheres such as education or the workplace are democratically governed. In such a world, different decisions would be made about AI objectives. 
 Some economists might object that the  price mechanism , using prices and markets to allocate resources, would be more suitable for governing decisions about AI objectives. We might have profit-maximizing companies who determine AI objectives, and consumers who pay for possible benefits from AI, and who are compensated for possible costs. The prices for benefits and costs, so the logic goes, would align profit maximization—and thus AI objectives—with social welfare. 
 The price mechanism, however, does not seem suitable to govern the socially consequential decisions of AI discussed throughout this book. No price governs the selection of news on social media feeds or the impact of that news on the democratic process or on mental health. The admission of students to universities is not primarily governed by a price mechanism, nor should it be. Price mechanisms do not address the distributional consequences of individualized, monopolistic pricing. And no price mechanism mediates algorithmic decisions about which households to bomb in Gaza. Data collection and algorithmic decision-making are rife with externalities and distributional consequences that the logic of the market will not resolve. 
 Collective self-governance in the form of democratic control of AI objectives thus seems ultimately the only solution that can guarantee broadly beneficial uses of AI. In the conclusion of this book, I speculate on how to create preconditions of democratic control, and how democratic control might be institutionalized.

 Ideological Obfuscation 
 Agents of change need the capacity to effect change—they need strategic leverage to have an impact on the objectives that are being maximized by AI. But agents of change also need values and beliefs that make them want to effect change, in a direction that is beneficial for society at large. Once we start to consider values and beliefs, ideology comes into play. Effective collective governance of AI is undermined by ideological obfuscation of the issues at stake. 
 In everyday language, we often use the term  ideology  rather casually to describe the beliefs of people we disagree with. In political theory,  ideology  is a hotly contested term, but it usually means something more specific than suggested by everyday usage. Correspondingly, I use the term to mean a coherent set of beliefs or arguments that ultimately serves certain interests, and that justifies the preservation of an unequal status quo (without making this explicit). Ideology can exist without anybody actively trying to deceive us. For an ideology to persist, it suffices if certain stories that are more convenient for certain social groups are repeated more often, while stories that are less convenient are repeated less often. 
 Ideology, in this sense, often does one or more of the following three things. First, it represents the interests of particular groups as those of society at large. This is often combined with warnings about an external enemy or outsider group. Differences and conflicts of interest  among insiders are ignored, and conflicts with outsiders are played up: “We have to stand together to defend against our common enemies.” 
 Second, ideology represents contingent choices as objective necessities. The very possibility of dissent is thereby forestalled, since it is foolish and dangerous to resist what cannot be otherwise: “There is no alternative” instead of “another world is possible.” 
 And third, ideology represents social relationships as material or technological ones. This happens especially in the context of discussions of the economy. For example, “Wage inequality is due to technical progress, which cannot be stopped.” 
 By making arguments about the general interest, the absence of alternatives, and the technical nature of the issues at hand, ideology has the function of preventing change and preserving the status quo. This benefits those who are currently in power. How does this apply to the old stories about AI discussed at the outset of the book? Can we think about these stories as part of an ideology? 
 Consider the story of  human versus machine . In this story, there is no society. Society is effectively identified with that one human, and all conflicts of interest are effaced. AI becomes the ideal outside enemy, outside humanity at large, which allows humanity to become one homogeneous group. Since there are no conflicts of interest in this story, the interests of humanity at large must be the same as the interests of, say, tech CEOs. 
 In the  existential risk  version of this story, where AI becomes exponentially self-improving, this external enemy is a technical necessity. It is not the product of human choices or social conflicts anymore. It is a self-improving force of nature. 
 Fights of human versus machine (discussed in  chapter 14 ), and intelligence explosions, are favorite stories of science fiction authors and of futurologists. Similar ideological functions can be served by some of the more sober stories of computer science and AI ethics—if all problems of AI are interpreted as  optimization errors . In these optimization  error stories, too, social problems are conceived as technological ones. Discrimination is conceived as a failure to predict accurately or to maximize profits. Value alignment is conceived as a wrongly specified reward. Privacy is conceived as a problem of getting individuals to share their data voluntarily. In each case, technical fixes are proposed for issues that ultimately require social negotiation. 
 A flip side of the ideological argument that the problems of AI require technical fixes is the ideological argument that only  technical experts  can provide these fixes. More broadly, a common story holds that AI is very complicated and hard to understand, and only the experts of the tech industry can possibly address the issues of AI. This story forestalls public debate and democratic decision-making. It concentrates decision-making power in the hands of an interested party, and it prevents the development of alternative stories by preventing debate. As this book aims to show, in contrast to this story, the basic ideas of AI are not that complicated and can be made broadly accessible. 
 A variant of the ideological argument about the technical inevitability of particular choices regarding the course of AI is the ideological argument about the  political inevitability  of these choices. This argument often invokes great-power rivalries. In the United States it might be “If we don’t do it, then China will.” In Europe it might be “If we don’t catch up, the American tech companies will keep dominating.” Functionally, this political argument, just like the technical argument, implies that there is no alternative. 
 A structuring set of ideas—an ideology, if you will, or a story—is certainly necessary to understand and interpret the world. But we do not have to stick to the old structuring ideas and stories that I just discussed. We can instead develop alternative stories. A good alternative story makes explicit that there are conflicts of interest and of values in society. It makes explicit that another world is possible—there are social choices to be made, and technology is not fate. And  it empowers all of us to collectively make these choices that impact us. This book tries to contribute such an alternative story. An alternative story alone will not change the world, but an alternative story might be necessary to change the world. 
 The remainder of this book spells out this alternative story in different domains of the ethics and politics of AI. In each case, I contrast a conventional story with an alternative and better one based on the framework that we discussed in the preceding chapters.

 Part IV 
 Regulating Algorithms 
 AI raises countless ethical and regulatory issues, many of which are hotly contested. It would be a mistake for regulators to focus only on engineering solutions for these issues, restricting attention to the fixing of optimization errors. Instead, democratic control of AI objectives is needed: To achieve  value alignment , we need to align algorithm objectives with democratically determined social objectives. To protect  privacy  and enable socially beneficial levels of data collection, in the context of pervasive externalities in learning, we need collective democratic governance of data collection. To make sure that AI is used to augment human abilities and create shared prosperity, rather than  automation  and unemployment, we need democratic worker voice to determine how to use AI in the workplace. To guarantee  fair  and  equitable  algorithmic decisions, we need to collectively decide how to trade off the consequences, gains, and losses of these decisions for different people. And to  explain  AI, we need to explain the objectives that it maximizes, so that we can have a democratic debate about what objectives AI should maximize.

 Value Alignment 
 The animated movie  Fantasia , released in 1940 by Disney, includes a segment called “The Sorcerer’s Apprentice,” in which Mickey Mouse is tired of doing his chores, which include sweeping the floor and carrying water from a fountain. To make his life easier, Mickey decides to use magic: He turns a broom into his servant, instructing the broom to carry water for him. Everything goes swimmingly until Mickey falls asleep, dreaming of his new powers. The broom keeps carrying water, over and over, until the basement is flooded, and Mickey wakes up to a deluge. He fails in his desperate attempts to stop the flood and is only saved by the appearance of the sorcerer, who puts an end to the mayhem, sending Mickey back to doing his chores as before. 
 An updated version of the sorcerer’s apprentice story, told by the philosopher Nick Bostrom, has become popular in the tech industry. In the updated version of the story, someone creates an AI with the goal of producing as many paper clips as possible. The AI is very good at its job. The problem is that at some point the human creator of the AI has enough paper clips, but the machine continues to produce more. The human creator would like to turn off the machine. Such human intervention stands in the way of the AI achieving its objective, which is to produce as many paper clips as possible. To achieve its objective, the AI thus needs to incapacitate its human creator  to prevent them from switching the AI off. The robot apocalypse ensues, and humanity is annihilated. 
 In the paper-clip story, the AI has an incomplete or misdefined objective. In the telling of AI doomsday scenarios, incomplete or misdefined objectives are often combined with the idea of an intelligence explosion, where an AI self-improves exponentially. In  chapter 1 , we introduced the idea of an intelligence explosion; in  chapter 4 , we then discussed how this idea of exponentially self-improving AI is based on questionable assumptions about intelligence as something that is one-dimensional. In the following, we shall not be concerned with intelligence explosions any further. We focus instead on the problem of mis-specified objectives. This problem is also known as the problem of  value alignment . (“Value” is used by computer scientists to mean the same as “objective,” in this context.) 
 Moving on from Disney movies and doomsday scenarios to a setting closer to home, here is another example of an apparent failure of value alignment. Consider social media platforms, such as Facebook or X, the platform formerly known as Twitter. These platforms select the content that they display by using algorithms. The algorithms used are designed to maximize profits by maximizing ad clicks. To maximize ad clicks, the algorithms need to maximize user engagement—that is, the time users spend on the platform, scrolling through their feed or interacting with posts. 
 One very effective way to maximize engagement is to amplify emotional and outraged posts. The more emotionally activated users are, the more likely they are to stay on the platform, interacting with other users, which gives the platform more opportunities to display ads. A side effect of this amplification of emotionalizing content is, however, the amplification of political outrage, hate, conspiracy theories, and polarization, with potentially dire consequences for the democratic process. 
 Another way that platforms can maximize engagement is to draw on people’s desire for social recognition, transmuting this desire into a hunt for likes, reposts, and replies. For any users of these platforms,  but in particular for teenagers, whose sense of self and social belonging is most in flux, the hunt for online recognition might amplify body image issues and mental health crises. 
 There are at least two different interpretations of problems such as these, where the maximization of ad clicks leads to the fraying of democracy and to harms for teenage mental health. One possibility is to interpret these problems as a question of value alignment between human and machine, in the spirit of the paper-clip story. According to this interpretation, the algorithm’s objective was mis-specified, because it did not explicitly include the impact of algorithmic decisions on domains such as democracy and mental health. A potential solution, according to this interpretation, is to engineer the specification of objectives (algorithm rewards) more carefully. 
 An alternative interpretation recognizes the fact that social media algorithms do exactly what they are designed to do, which is to maximize the profits of the platform companies. The impact of these algorithms on political discourse and body image issues might be regrettable, from the company’s perspective, but that is ultimately someone else’s problem. As shown by “The Facebook Files,” which were leaked by whistleblower Frances Haugen in 2021, management at Facebook was aware of numerous internal studies that had documented adverse effects of its algorithms on democracy and mental health. But, according to Frances Haugen, as quoted in  The Guardian , “The thing I saw at Facebook over and over again was there were conflicts of interest between what was good for the public and what was good for Facebook. And Facebook, over and over again, chose to optimize for its own interests, like making more money.” 
 Before discussing this alternative interpretation of value-alignment problems further, I will first review their standard interpretation. I will discuss how these problems resemble classic problems of incentive design in economics. We will see, based on this analogy, that there are some settings where we need to refrain from using AI to make decisions, because key outcomes are simply not measurable. 
 Reward Design and Incentive Design 
 In the Old Testament, Ecclesiastes 1:9 states, “What has been will be again, what has been done will be done again; there is nothing new under the sun.” And so it is with the concerns of AI ethics and AI safety. Many of these concerns are similar to concerns that have been raised in entirely human contexts. This is true, in particular, for the concern about value alignment, as the following examples illustrate. We will borrow from debates about incentive design and (employment) contracts in economics, to shine light on questions of reward design and value alignment in AI. 
 For several decades, a heated debate has been taking place in the United States regarding the governance of education, and especially of public education. One of the fault lines of this debate has pitted proponents of standardized tests of student achievement against opponents of these tests. Some of the proponents of standardized tests have argued that these tests should be used to evaluate teacher performance. According to this line of reasoning, teachers whose students improve over time, in terms of their performance on standardized tests, should be rewarded. Teachers whose students do not improve should be fired. 
 Opponents of the idea of incentivizing teachers based on test scores argue that such incentives are bad for students. One important argument focuses on the notion of  teaching to the test . There are some student skills that standardized tests might measure well, such as the ability to do routine calculations or the ability to reproduce factual knowledge. But there are other student skills that are harder to pin down and that standardized tests are poorly suited to measure, such as critical thinking or socio-emotional development. Teachers in the classroom must decide what to focus their teaching efforts on, in an environment that is often challenging. Teachers could spend most of their time in class practicing routine calculations, rote memorization, and multiple-choice test-taking skills. Or they could focus  instead on honing their students’ ability to engage in critical discussions and respectful social interactions. If a plan is in place where teachers’ promotion or dismissal depends on the results of their students on a standardized test, then these teachers are compelled to focus their effort on the former—calculations, memorization, and testing-taking skills—at the expense of the latter—critical thinking and respectful interactions. The net result might be a worsened and impoverished school experience for students. This is true even if the skills that are measured by standardized tests are in fact important. The only thing required for this critique to be valid is that there are other skills that are also important, but that are not measured by standardized tests. 
 This kind of scenario is quite common, and it can also be found in many contexts other than education. Such scenarios, where effort has multiple dimensions and only some of these dimensions can be measured, are known as  multitasking  in economic theory. In many employment relationships, the employer would like to incentivize employee performance, whether with promotions and bonuses or with the threat of dismissal. The problem that employers face is that they can only observe some aspects of employee performance, while other aspects are much harder to pin down. If the employer were to attach strong incentives to the observed parts of performance, this would distort employee effort away from all the other unobserved dimensions. Such a distortion of effort could have disastrous consequences. Because of this, high-powered incentives are typically not a good idea from the employer’s perspective. It is likely for this reason that high-powered incentives are quite rarely utilized in private employment relationships. 
 Beyond multitasking, there are also other reasons why high-powered incentives are often a bad idea. Consider the compensation of corporate CEOs. From the 1980s onward, the interests of capital owners came to be increasingly understood in terms of the maximization of shareholder value, as reflected in stock prices. This, in turn,  prompted a movement to pay CEOs according to stock prices. More precisely, executives’ benefits often include  stock options . Stock options give their owner the right to purchase stocks of a company at some predetermined point in the future, and at a price that is fixed in advance. If the future market price of the stock exceeds this fixed price, then the owner of an option makes a profit, based on the difference between the market price of the stock and the price fixed in the option. If the market price of the stock is lower than the price of the option, nothing happens, and the owner of the option gets no payment but also does not lose anything. 
 What will a CEO do if they are paid with stock options? They clearly have an incentive to increase stock prices, in line with shareholder interests but possibly at the expense of workers and customers. But there is also something else going on. If you own a stock option, you profit a lot when the stock value is very high. But you don’t lose anything when the stock value is very low; at worst the option is worth zero. Because of this, it pays for CEOs to increase risk, since they profit from the upside but don’t lose from the downside. The riskier the gambles they take, the higher their expected benefits. This type of risk-seeking behavior has arguably been an important contributor to recent financial crises. In a different context, but for similar reasons, political leaders who are at risk of losing power might engage in risky military escalation. This gives them a chance of remaining in power if things go well. This phenomenon is known as  gambling for resurrection  in political science. This phenomenon might have played an important role in the recent escalation of wars, from Putin’s invasion of Ukraine to Netanyahu’s invasion of Lebanon. 
 In economics, the fields of  contract theory  and  mechanism design  study the design of incentive programs, such as those for teachers or CEOs in the examples discussed above. Incentive programs matter whenever a  principal  (e.g., educational policymaker, shareholder) seeks to align the behavior of some  agent  (e.g., teacher, CEO) with the interests of the principal. Incentives matter in contract theory precisely because not everything is observable by the principal. 
 This is a book about AI, not about educational reform or CEO pay or warmongering political leaders. But the paper-clip-producing AI is like a teacher who is teaching to the test. While the teacher is incentivized to maximize test scores, the AI is rewarded to maximize paper-clip production. Educational policymakers care about the skills measured by standardized tests, but they also care about other skills, such as critical thinking or social competence. Engineers who program paper-clip-producing machines care about producing paper clips, but they also care about other goals, such as not annihilating humanity. Test-based incentive programs for teachers might be doomed to fail because not all important aspects of teaching are measurable by tests. And, critically, reward-maximizing AI algorithms might be doomed to fail because not all relevant objectives of an engineer are measurable in a way that can be encoded in the reward function. 
 There are some important considerations that apply to incentive design for human agents in contract theory but not to reward design for algorithms. First, humans are typically  risk averse . Humans typically do not like large gambles, where they might win a lot, but they might also lose a lot. Because of this, high-powered incentives based on risky outcomes are costly for human agents. By contrast, there is no cost for algorithms to be exposed to uncertain rewards. Second, human agents can typically walk away, at least in societies without forced labor. Human workers are not forced to  participate  in an employment contract with a specific employer, and their  outside option  (their best alternative, if they choose not to work for this specific employer) provides a lower bound on how much they need to be rewarded in an employment relationship. By contrast, for algorithms there is no need to ensure a minimum level of reward to guarantee participation, and providing rewards for algorithms is not costly. 
 These differences do not imply, however, that the insights from contract theory do not apply to algorithms. Consider the multitasking problem. Human agents cannot be rewarded for dimensions of effort that are hard to measure. The reason is that such dimensions  of effort can only be measured with a lot of random noise, and noisy rewards introduce a lot of risk. Risk is costly for human agents. For algorithms, there is no intrinsic cost of noisy rewards. But if an outcome is not measurable at all, then algorithmic rewards cannot be calculated based on this outcome. The underlying problem of multitasking thus equally applies to algorithms as it does to humans. Both algorithms and humans cannot be rewarded based on outcomes that cannot be measured. 
 More generally, there are fundamental agency problems that arise when not everything that we care about is observable. Limits on what can be observed, or measured, imply limits on what can be delegated to human agents—and they most certainly imply limits on what can be delegated to automated AI systems. These limits cannot be transcended by obtaining more of the same kind of data. More of the same kind of data does not solve agency problems. When training social media recommendation algorithms, for example, having more data relating the content of ads to clicks will not teach the recommendation algorithm that it should not undermine the democratic process. When training the paper-clip-making AI, more data on production techniques and paper-clip output won’t teach the AI that it should not eliminate humanity. 
 To summarize: The fundamental limits on delegation in mechanism design (for human agents) and in reward design (for AI agents) are owed to the fact that some variables are not measurable. These problems are unrelated to (and separate from) the problem of not having enough observations—which might independently plague AI, as we discussed earlier for self-driving cars or rare-disease diagnosis. There just are some things AI can’t do well. 
 Learning Reward Functions 
 Researchers in AI are pursuing various approaches to try to solve the problem of value alignment, and to build  provably safe AI —or  so some claim. One of the approaches that has been proposed for addressing the problem of value alignment is known as  inverse reinforcement learning —in effect, sidestepping the problem of explicitly specifying a reward function for AI algorithms. Rather than maximizing an explicitly specified reward function, in inverse reinforcement learning the algorithms are supposed to construct a reward function by inferring human objectives from observed human behavior. The algorithms are meant to learn their own reward function, in addition to learning how to maximize this reward function. This approach builds on the idea of  revealed preferences  that was mentioned in the  chapter 10  discussion on individual welfare: If a human picks an apple over an orange, then the algorithm is supposed to learn that the human prefers apples over oranges. The algorithm thus learns a reward function that assigns a higher reward to apples than to oranges. Similarly, if a human driver brakes when an animal crosses the road, then the self-driving algorithm is supposed to learn that not killing animals is more important than gaining a few seconds of driving time. If a human switches off the paper-clip-making robot once they have enough paper clips, the robot is supposed to learn that humans do not need an infinite number of paper clips. 
 Going a bit further, the algorithms are also supposed to learn the purpose of human actions relative to future rewards. This, then, is truly an inversion of the reinforcement learning problem. If a human waters a basil plant now, he might want to eat basil pesto in a month. If another human trains to increase her endurance, she might want to participate in a marathon later in the year. It is possible for algorithms to learn indirect objectives from observed behavior under some conditions, but it requires both strong assumptions and a lot of data. Economists have been trying to estimate human preferences from human behavior for decades. In doing so, they have learned that estimating preferences is quite difficult and not always feasible, even when maintaining strong assumptions. 
 Inverse reinforcement learning and related approaches may hold  some promise, but there are fundamental limits to what these approaches can achieve. They cannot solve the multitasking problem, and they cannot solve other related agency problems. Recall the problem of teaching to the test. This problem cannot be solved by observing educational policymakers to infer the preferences of these policymakers, which is what the inverse reinforcement learning approach would try to do. Teaching to the test occurs because standardized tests cannot measure certain important dimensions of student development. No amount of reward learning by observing policymakers will solve this problem. Similarly, no engineering solution for an ad-click-maximizing algorithm will solve the problem that the algorithm’s impact on the democratic process is not measurable by recording ad clicks. Nor can this problem be solved by observing Mark Zuckerberg’s behavior to infer his preferences and by deriving from these inferred preferences the correct objective for social media feed selection, which is, again, what inverse reinforcement learning would suggest. There are fundamental limits on the extent to which the value-alignment problem can be solved by reward engineering or by approaches such as inverse reinforcement learning. Because of this, there are settings where important decisions should not be delegated to AI systems. These systems are bound to ignore unmeasured dimensions of human well-being, and the underlying agency problems prevent effective delegation. 
 There is another, and arguably even more important, problem that needs to be solved. Inverse reinforcement learning is supposed to learn human preferences and act accordingly. But which human’s preferences? Whose values should the algorithm align with? 
 Alignment with Whom? 
 There is more than one human in the world. And different humans want different things. Tastes differ across people. Some people like oranges, some like apples. Some people like chocolate, others like  strawberries. These differences in taste seem surmountable, as far as value alignment goes—let the robots give oranges and chocolate to some and apples and strawberries to others. 
 More fundamentally, humans also differ in their values and beliefs. Some people believe in the intrinsic worth of animal life and well-being, others don’t. Some believe in divinely ordained laws and moral prescriptions revealed in religious scriptures, others don’t. Some believe in the equality of the sexes, others don’t. Which of these values should AI be aligned with? 
 Most importantly, humans differ in terms of their position in society. We might all be ultimately quite similar in our basic hopes and desires. We all want physical safety for ourselves and for those we love, we want shelter and food, and we want community, respect, dignity, and love. But we are not all in the same position to obtain these things. We don’t all have the same material resources required to gain access to these things in a capitalist society. We might all want a piece of the cake, but not all of us get a piece the same size, or a piece at all. Whose desire for more cake should AI be aligned with? 
 The most salient value-alignment problems are thus not between a human and a machine. They are between different humans. Consider the algorithms that select what is shown in your Facebook feed. The problem with these algorithms isn’t that they are not aligned with the goals of Mark Zuckerberg, the CEO of Meta; they most likely are. The problem with these algorithms is that the algorithmic decisions that make Mark Zuckerberg rich might also be decisions that undermine the democratic process, or harm teenage mental health. The problem with Amazon’s warehouse management algorithms isn’t that these algorithms are not aligned with the goals of Jeff Bezos, the founder of Amazon. The problem with these algorithms is that algorithmic decisions that make Jeff Bezos richer might also be decisions that harm the health and well-being of warehouse workers. 
 When there are conflicts of values or conflicts of interest, control rights matter. If Facebook users were to decide on the objectives  of the algorithms determining their feed, they would not choose to maximize ad revenues. If Amazon’s warehouse workers were to decide on the objectives of the algorithms controlling their workflow, they would not choose to minimize cost and maximize speed to the detriment of their own health and safety. 
 There are countless settings where algorithmic decisions are contentious—not because the algorithms are not aligned with their human owners, but because the objectives of these owners stand in conflict with the welfare of other people. 
 One example (discussed in more detail in  chapter 16 ) is the deployment of technology in the workplace. There are many choices to be made in the workplace about what technologies to use in the production process and to what end. As emphasized by the Nobel Prize–winning economist Daron Acemoglu and his collaborators, some technological choices that automate tasks that were previously performed by human workers lead to rising profits and declining wages. Technological choices that  augment  human capabilities lead, instead, to rising wages. These things are especially true for the many possible uses of AI. If workers have control over technological choices, they will privilege rising wages and securing employment levels; if representatives of capital have control, they will privilege increasing profits. 
 A second example of conflicts over the objectives of AI is the automatic, individualized setting of prices by companies selling to consumers. Individualized price setting is familiar from domains such as consumer credit, private health insurance, and air travel, especially in the United States. Individualized price setting is likely to become increasingly widespread, absent legal constraints on price discrimination. Once data collection and predictive capabilities have advanced enough, companies will be able to set prices exactly equal to each buyer’s willingness or ability to pay. This will transfer all economic surplus from such transactions to the companies: Profits will increase while consumers will be left just indifferent between buying  and not buying—individualized prices will be equal to the maximum amount that each consumer would possibly be willing to pay. If consumers were to have collective control over the rules of price setting, it would often be in their interest not to allow for such monopolistic price discrimination. There are furthermore not only distributional conflicts between monopolists and consumers; there are also distributional conflicts between different groups of consumers who differ in their willingness or ability to pay. Resolving all these distributional conflicts requires democratic deliberation. 
 An even more consequential example of conflicts over the objectives of AI is in war—for example, the selection of targets to bomb. As discussed previously, in the bombardment of Gaza that began in 2023 after the Hamas attacks on Israel, the algorithmic selection of households as bombing targets used AI systems called Lavender and Where’s Daddy? As reported by the journalists of  +972 Magazine , these algorithms were implemented with accepted death rates of about fifteen to twenty civilians per presumptive low-level Hamas operative killed and up to one hundred civilians per higher-ranking Hamas official. One might safely conjecture that the inhabitants of Gaza would have chosen different objectives. In a hypothetical world where Gazans had equal citizenship rights and democratic voice in Israel, the objectives of these AI systems would presumably have put a higher weight on their lives. 
 This life-and-death example illustrates a problem that equally arises in more everyday applications, including in corporate profit maximization: Aligning values is difficult because there is a conflict between the objectives of those who control AI and the objectives of people who don’t. It raises the question of how AI objectives can be aligned with social welfare, if the control of objectives is based on control of the means of prediction. There is a short-term answer to this question and a long-term answer. 
 In the short term, the objectives of AI will continue to be chosen by profit-maximizing corporations. To align the objectives of AI  with the maximization of social welfare, we thus need to align profit maximization with social welfare. An important set of tools for this purpose in the economist’s toolkit is  Pigou taxes . The idea of Pigou taxes is to make companies (or individuals) pay for damages to other people that are caused by the actions of these companies (or individuals). The motivation for Pigou taxes is not that the companies who cause damage to others  deserve  to pay. Instead, the motivation is to make up for the  negative externalities  that the actions of these companies cause. Economists speak of negative externalities when someone’s actions cause harm to others, where the decision-maker does not bear the cost of these harms themselves. By imposing a tax equal to these externalities, so the logic goes, the externalities become  internalized . If companies must pay for the consequences of their actions, then damages caused to others become part of their profit-maximizing cost-benefit calculations, and are better avoided. 
 Pigou taxes might, in principle, be used to close the gap between profit maximization and the maximization of social welfare, to align the objectives of AI with the objectives of society. That said, in many of the settings where the deployment of AI has consequences for others, it is unfortunately difficult, in practice, to put a monetary value on these consequences. It is also difficult to implement taxes corresponding to these manifold consequences. 
 In a previous chapter, we considered a variety of potential agents of change who can impose costs on companies. The leverage that these potential agents of change have over companies is similar to a Pigou tax. The different groups of workers, consumers, media, courts, and regulators have an ability to threaten actions that reduce corporate profits. By doing so, these agents of change can effectively align corporate profit maximization more closely with social welfare. By incorporating the threat of strikes, consumer boycotts, bad press, or regulatory action into their cost-benefit calculations, the corporations controlling the means of prediction are forced to take into account  the interests and values of these agents of change when choosing AI objectives. 
 This is again true in principle, but in practice using the threat of actions that reduce profits to consistently align AI with social welfare is a daunting task. It requires sustained organization and cooperation among heterogeneous groups of workers—or consumers or regulators—in an era that has seen declining organizational capacity. It also requires that the interests and values of these agents are sufficiently aligned with social welfare in general and with the interests of those impacted by AI systems in particular. For these reasons, threats to corporate profits by external actors are ultimately a somewhat blunt tool. They are the tool available in the short term to align the objectives of AI with society, but they might not be a solution to the alignment problem in the long term. 
 In the long term, we need to establish democratic control. The ideal of autonomy suggests that people should decide their own fate. In an interdependent society, decisions need to be made collectively. This is especially true for AI. To guarantee that the interests of the people affected by AI are reflected in the objectives maximized by AI, these same people need to have effective power over the choice of AI objectives. We need democratic structures to ensure value alignment. And, in so far as objectives are determined by control over the means of prediction, we need democratic control over these means. How could democratic control be organized more concretely? I discuss some historical precedents and some possibilities for the future in the conclusion of this book.

 Privacy 
 Have you ever consumed illegal drugs? Chances are you might not want to answer this question truthfully if a stranger asked you. You might even fear legal consequences if you admitted to consuming illegal substances. Under these circumstances, what can a researcher do if they want to learn about the prevalence of drug consumption? 
 To solve this problem, survey researchers have come up with an ingenious method. This  randomized response method  works as follows: The interviewer requests that you secretly flip a coin. The coin might come up heads or tails. If it comes up heads, you answer the question about drugs truthfully. If it comes up tails, you flip again. If it comes up heads on the second flip, you also answer truthfully, but if the second coin flip comes up tails, you say the opposite of what is true.  Figure 7  illustrates this. 
 Figure 7  Coin-flip method for sensitive surveys 
 How does this method help? For one, it gives you plausible deniability. If you answer yes to the question about illegal drugs, you can always claim later that you got two tails in a row, which is quite possible. You therefore do not incriminate yourself in the eyes of law enforcement by answering yes. 
 But can the researchers conducting the survey still learn something, considering that any given respondent might have lied? The answer, perhaps surprisingly, is yes. The reason is that there is only a 25 percent chance that you would get two tails in a row. There is,  therefore, only a 25 percent chance that you lied but a 75 percent chance that you told the truth. For a given share of yes answers, using this probability of truthful responses, we can back out the underlying share of respondents who truly consumed drugs. 
 Consider the following example: Suppose that we ran a survey using this method, and suppose that 35 percent of respondents answered yes. We want to use this number to figure out the true share  p  of respondents who consumed illegal drugs. If the true share equals  p , where  p  is some number between 0 and 1, we can expect to get a share of yes responses equal to  p  × 0.75 (for those who actually consumed drugs and responded truthfully) plus (1 −  p ) × 0.25 (for those who didn’t consume drugs and lied). Solving the equation ( p  × 0.75) + ((1 −  p ) × 0.25) = 0.35 (the rate of yes answers in the survey) for  p , we recover that in truth only a share of 20 percent of respondents had actually consumed drugs. To verify this, note that if we plug in  p  = 0.2, we get the right result, (0.2 × 0.75) + (0.8 × 0.25) = 0.35. 
 More generally, for any share of respondents who answer that they had consumed illegal drugs, we can back out the true underlying rate, since we know the share of respondents who tell the truth and the share who lie, by construction. This method could be tweaked further to make the probability of a truthful answer even smaller (but bigger than 0.5), to grant an even greater degree of plausible deniability to respondents. 
 This little trick of survey researchers, using secret coin flips to ask sensitive questions, can be made to work much more generally. As it turns out, something like this method is in fact necessary if we wish to protect privacy in a robust manner: We must hide sensitive facts under the cover of random noise. This is one of the key insights of the field of  differential privacy . 
 There are several methods other than differential privacy that have been used in the past to maintain individuals’ privacy in datasets. Unfortunately, most of these methods don’t work consistently. They don’t work because someone with sufficient additional information can typically recover whose records are included in the data and reveal their identities. 
 One method that has been used in the past requires that sensitive or  identifying information  be deleted. Public datasets collected by the state, for example, usually do not contain names, Social Security numbers, or addresses. The problem with this method, where identifying information is deleted, is that your identity can easily be reconstructed from other, seemingly innocent, variables. If you tell me the titles of the last three movies that you watched, for example, or what you had for lunch over the last few days, then there is a pretty good chance that this information would uniquely pin down your identity. 
 Another method that is designed with the intention to maintain privacy prohibits the release of individual data entirely, and it reports only averages or  aggregates  of different variables. This method fails too. One striking example of such a failure is in medical studies of genetic markers that are associated with a certain disease. Some studies had collected data for patients with a certain disease. These studies then analyzed the genetic data of the patients, and they reported the share among the patients who had specific genetic markers for many different genes. No individual data were reported, just these shares across all patients. What the researchers who published these studies did not realize was that you can reconstruct the identity of all patients included in the study, just by knowing the shares for enough different genes. 
 Differential Privacy 
 What can be done if we want to collect data while preserving privacy? Specifically, what can be done to enable machine learning while protecting the identities of the individuals in the data? Is there any hope of collecting useful data if neither the removal of identifying information nor aggregation can preserve privacy? As first shown by the computer scientist Cynthia Dwork and her collaborators, there is a solution, and it requires randomness, as in the survey method described above. Randomness allows us to guarantee  differential privacy . 
 Differential privacy is concerned with mechanisms that reveal information about some underlying data. You can think of a  mechanism  as a sort of  black box . You can pose questions to this black box, and it will give you (approximate) answers. 
 A mechanism for releasing data is considered differentially private if it is essentially impossible to figure out whether someone’s information was included in the data or not based on the output of the mechanism. No matter what questions you pose to the black box, and no matter what additional information you have, a differentially private mechanism does not allow you to learn anything about who is included in the data. For a mechanism to be differentially private, any given output of the mechanism must have (almost) the same probability, regardless of whether my information (or someone else’s) was included in the data—or not. 
 Why would I care whether you can find out if my information is included in the data? Because, if differential privacy holds, I never have an  incentive to withhold  my information. This is the case because it simply makes no difference whether my individual information is included in the data. If you make a decision based on the output of a differentially private mechanism, you would (almost certainly) make the same decision regardless of whether my information was included in the data that was fed into the mechanism. This is true for any possible subsequent decisions, and it is true no matter what  other information is available to you. Withholding individual data does not impact subsequent decisions, and therefore I do not have a reason not to share my data. 
 Differential privacy, as defined above, has several attractive properties. Most importantly, differential privacy is  immune to postprocessing : If a mechanism is differentially private, then you can combine the output of this mechanism with whatever additional data you have, and you can perform whatever computations you like—the result will still be differentially private. The same does not hold for mechanisms that simply remove identifying information, nor for mechanisms that only report aggregates. If you have a dataset, for example, where all names were removed, but where the last movies that participants watched were listed, you might combine this information with another dataset that matches people to their movie list. Combining the two datasets, you would then be able to figure out who was included in the original data, recovering their names and personal details. With a differentially private mechanism, by contrast, this would be impossible. 
 A useful way to understand differentially private mechanisms is in terms of a  privacy budget . Every time a differentially private mechanism reports something about an underlying dataset, a little bit of privacy is lost. The privacy losses add up across reports. If there is an acceptable total loss of privacy, then it would be best to decide beforehand how to spend this privacy budget across different possible reports. 
 An interesting example of this was the 2020 US census, which collected data on all residents of the United States. It had been decided that the data of this census would, for the first time, only be released to researchers in a differentially private manner. There was a total privacy budget that could be spent on releasing information from the census. This limited privacy budget led to heated debates among social scientists. Different social scientists wanted to learn different things from the census. They therefore disagreed over how to spend the allotted privacy budget. 
 How can differentially private mechanisms be constructed? The basic idea is that the mechanisms need to add  random noise  to any reports that they make. The random noise is like the coin that had to be flipped for the survey mechanism described above. The more random noise a mechanism adds, the smaller the privacy loss from a given report. One might wonder: Does this not render the mechanism useless if it just spits out random noise? Not necessarily, since the required amount of noise is often very small. Consider, for example, the US census, and imagine you want to know the share of people in the United States with a college degree. Adding or dropping one person from the census makes almost no difference to this share. If we now add a tiny amount of noise to the reported share of people with a college degree, it becomes indiscernible whether someone’s data were included. 
 Differential privacy is an elegant and coherent framework. It is robust since it protects individuals from any adverse consequences of sharing their data, no matter what other information an adversary has. Does that mean that differential privacy solves the problem of protecting individuals from possible adverse consequences of machine learning and AI, when these are based on individual data? Unfortunately, the answer is no. Before explaining and resolving this apparent contradiction, let’s take a detour to the legal governance of privacy. 
 Data Property Rights 
 Differential privacy captures how computer scientists think about privacy. But what does the law have to say on the topic? The most well-known piece of privacy legislation is the General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) of the European Union. This law went into effect across Europe in 2018 and has since been adopted, with minor modifications, in a range of other countries across Asia, Africa, and South America. 
 The GDPR sets out conditions for the lawful processing of personal  data. The GDPR gives wide-ranging control rights to individuals ( data subjects ) regarding data that concern them. In general, whenever personal data are processed, this requires informed,  affirmative consent  of data subjects, unless the data processing is necessary to fulfill contractual or legal obligations toward these same data subjects. Consent for data processing can be withdrawn at any time, data subjects have a right to know what data is stored about them, and subjects have a right to have their data erased. Violation of these rights can lead to large legal penalties. Data subjects furthermore have a right to receive compensation for resulting damages if their rights are violated. 
 The GDPR has been criticized for several reasons: Compliance with the GDPR is costly and might be easier for large companies, which tilts competitive advantage in favor of the big tech companies. In many cases, compliance might furthermore become a mere box-ticking exercise. Internet users might not be able to effectively use many services if they want to withhold their data (even though the GDPR, in theory, prohibits such behavior by service providers). 
 More abstractly, and most importantly for our discussion, we can understand the GDPR as granting  individual property rights  over data. Individuals can withhold their data, have them deleted, or share them at will in exchange for services or other material benefits. One might believe that these individual property rights will protect individuals from the possible harms of data processing, and in particular from the use of their data for machine learning or AI. This, it turns out, isn’t the case. Individual property rights are not an effective protection against the risks of AI. 
 Data Externalities 
 Differential privacy is generally effective at protecting individual privacy in a large dataset, like the kinds used in machine learning. But that doesn’t mean it is a remedy for  data externalities . Recall that  economists speak of externalities if an individual’s decisions create costs or benefits for other people, where the individual making the decision does not have to bear these costs or does not get to receive these benefits. Whenever there are externalities, markets and individual property rights cannot ensure adequate decisions. Data externalities are a special form of externalities; they are present when individuals impact the well-being of other people by sharing their own data. 
 Now here, then, is the key issue: Externalities are the whole point of machine learning. Machine learning is never about individual data points; it is always about  patterns  across individuals. In supervised learning, in particular, the goal is not to learn individual features  X  and individual outcomes  Y . The goal is to learn the relationship between features  X  and outcomes  Y  across individuals. The patterns detected by machine learning can then be used to inform all kinds of future decisions, for instance by predicting the outcome  Y  for people for whom the true  Y  is not observed. These people’s welfare is impacted by AI. The individuals who originally contributed their data to training the AI do not bear these welfare consequences. This is the reason why there are data externalities. 
 To make things less abstract, consider a private health insurance company. This company would like to enroll people who will have low health-care expenditures in the future, while refusing insurance to anyone with high future expenditures. Absent data, this insurance company cannot distinguish people with future health problems, and therefore it has to offer equal insurance to everyone. 
 Suppose that the insurance company has publicly available features  X  at their disposal for all those who apply for insurance. These features might, for instance, include demographics, employment status, or social media profiles. Health problems  Y  are private information of individuals, however, and are protected by privacy laws. Suppose that the insurance company offers individuals a small reward if they share information about their health outcomes  Y . The  insurance company credibly promises to maintain differential privacy for any health data that these individuals voluntarily share. In this scenario, individuals should be happy to share their health data. Sharing health data has (essentially) no consequence for them, by definition of differential privacy, and in particular the chance of obtaining insurance is not affected for these individuals. If they receive a (small) reward for sharing their data, they might as well do so. 
 The insurance company can now use the data obtained in this way to learn the relationship between publicly available features  X  and private health outcomes  Y . Using supervised learning, the insurance company can learn to predict health outcomes  Y  based on  X  for everyone. Having learned the relationship between  X  and  Y , the insurance company can then refuse insurance to all applicants who are likely to have future health problems according to the predictions of the supervised learning algorithm. The individuals who are refused health insurance are thus harmed by the use of machine learning, while insurance profits are increased. Crucially, all this holds even though individual data property rights were respected, and even though differential privacy was implemented to protect individual data. 
 The principles illustrated by this example hold much more generally: A well-developed field within machine learning theory discusses how to learn predictive relationships while maintaining differential privacy. Strikingly, differential privacy typically imposes only a negligible cost, in terms of predictive accuracy, relative to unrestricted machine learning. This holds true for supervised learning tasks, and it also holds true for multi-armed bandits and related settings. 
 Democratic Data Governance 
 To summarize, data externalities are built into machine learning and AI: The reason anyone bothers with all the work of data collection  and supervised learning is that its predictions can be applied outside the training dataset. Because of these externalities, individual data property rights are an insufficient tool to mitigate the potential harms of AI, and to harness the potential benefits of AI. For the same reason, privacy legislation like the GDPR cannot ensure appropriate levels of data collection and processing. If corporations implement differentially private mechanisms for data collection, individuals have no incentive to withhold their data, even if privacy legislation would entitle them to do so. Machine learning can then proceed almost unimpeded while maintaining differential privacy. Any downstream harms or benefits of machine learning and AI are thus unaffected by the presence of privacy laws and private data collection mechanisms. 
 If individual data property rights cannot protect against the potential harms of AI, what can be done? Any answer to this question must involve some form of collective governance, because individual rights are insufficient. Appropriate levels of data collection and processing can only be ensured collectively. There are different policy tools that collective governance might use. 
 Pigou taxes , mentioned above, are one tool. Pigou taxes, or subsidies, might ensure appropriate levels of data collection if we have good estimates of the welfare impact of externalities. By setting the level of taxes on data collection equal to the amount of the externalities, these externalities become  internalized , thus aligning the incentives of data collectors with social welfare. There are obviously some hurdles to implementing this approach. First, we would need good estimates of the magnitude of externalities, which might vary in complicated ways across different contexts. Second, the state needs to be able to enforce the taxes, by tracking data collection and imposing corresponding tax payments. Given the political will, it might be possible to overcome these hurdles in some settings. 
 Another policy tool is  regulation . Rather than compensating for externalities through tax payments or subsidies, certain types of data  collection could be outright prohibited or required. Regulation might be attractive in settings where it is clear that data collection is socially harmful or socially beneficial. Such regulation might be easier to implement than finely differentiated Pigou taxes. 
 A third policy tool is the direct management of data collection by collective bodies, an idea that has been promoted under the name of  data trusts . The idea is that individuals can contribute their data to some entity, such as a data trust. The entity then makes collective decisions about questions including how to process the data and how to use it for learning tasks on behalf of its data contributors. These data contributors might retain voting rights over the use of the data in the trust. Individuals might, for example, share their health data with a trust to enable medical research or preventive public health measures, while prohibiting the use of the data for the exclusion of individuals from health insurance. Such collective entities for data collection might be managed by the state, or they might be run as independent nonprofit organizations. They might act as intermediaries between individual data subjects and commercial enterprises that want to use their data. Data trusts might, in turn, be subject to Pigou taxes or regulation to align them with the interests of society at large. 
 The above emphasizes the central importance of data externalities in machine learning. There is a complementary issue: the distributional impact of machine learning. In our health insurance example, some people lose because of the use of machine learning, while others benefit. Those with predictable health problems lose by being excluded from insurance, while those without such problems are unaffected, or even see declining costs of insurance. The deeper issue here is that  insurance  (the compensation of  uncertain harms ) becomes  redistribution  (the transfer of resources toward those exposed to  predictable harms ) as predictions get more accurate. With data collection and (machine) learning, uncertainties are reduced, and harms become predictable, so that insurance against uncertain  harms becomes redistribution to those in need. Critically, markets never deliver such redistribution; they can’t, at least not on their own. Collective democratic decision-making is required to enable such redistribution. 
 Insurance is not the only case where data collection and (machine) learning can have large distributional consequences. Another important example of the distributional impact of machine learning is  individual pricing . Those who have a higher willingness to pay for some product, for instance because they urgently need this product, are worse off once data are collected to predict willingness to pay, because their prices go up. Those with lower willingness to pay are better off as a consequence of data collection and machine learning. 
 Both the pervasive presence of data externalities and these distributional questions require collective decision-making to achieve socially beneficial levels of data collection. Individual property rights cannot ever solve these issues by construction. Those impacted by the downstream consequences of data collection need to have democratic control over data. This includes, notably, not only those whose data is collected but also anyone else who is impacted by data collection. Legislators need to create the regulations, taxes, and frameworks for civil society governance that would allow us to govern data collection and processing in the interests of society at large.

 Automation 
 Ned Ludd was an English weaver’s apprentice. In 1779, angered by requests to change his working technique, he smashed two knitting machines—or so the legend goes. Over the decades following this incident, Ludd became an inspiration to a generation of textile artisans who were struggling to survive economically and to maintain decent working conditions amid the squalor of the Industrial Revolution. During these decades, increased automation of textile factories led to declining wages and a de-skilling of work. The artisans and workers, inspired by Ned Ludd, would write letters to industrialists and government officials, demanding the establishment of minimum wages, an end to child labor, and the abolishment of “obnoxious” machines. The letters of these workers would be signed using pen names such as Captain Ludd, General Ludd, or even King Ludd; these workers came to be known as Luddites. They are most famous for their tactic of destroying industrial machinery, or threatening to destroy machinery, as leverage to obtain better working conditions. Called upon by industrialists, the British government eventually dispatched twelve thousand soldiers to suppress the Luddites. These troops crushed the Luddite resistance, and several Luddites were hanged. This put an end to the Luddite movement. 
 Since then, the word  Luddite  has come to be used in a derogatory fashion, to describe anyone resisting the inevitable and beneficial  progress of technology for the betterment of humanity. In line with this judgment, those who subscribe to a  techno-optimist  view can argue that, while the old artisan professions of the textile industry might indeed have largely disappeared, it is still the case that working conditions today, for most people, are considerably better than two hundred years ago. But does that mean that the Luddites were wrong, and that the techno-optimists were (and are) right? Does technological progress really make everyone better off? And, if so, will the same be true for AI? 
 Contra the techno-optimists, public predictions of technological doom in the labor market are also made as reliably as the seasons. Predictions of the final obsolescence of human labor, of mass unemployment, and of social collapse due to workplace automation have been favorite staples of corporate consultants, futurologists, and business journalists for decades. This time is no different, and it is again high season for predictions of technological unemployment due to technological change. One example of this genre is a 2023 report by researchers at Goldman Sachs predicted that generative AI might replace three hundred million full-time workers in the industrialized West. 
 So far, so familiar. But maybe this time is different, after all? Maybe AI is a fundamental leap in our technology. Maybe AI is qualitatively different from everything that came before, in a way that previous waves of innovation were not. 
 Economic history, economic theory, and empirical economics have much to say—and, sometimes, contradicting things to say—about the impact of technical progress on the labor market. A few key points are widely accepted among economists: First, there have been many waves of automation over the last 250 years, and none of them have led to the lasting obsolescence of human work, thus far. Second, the fact that human work hasn’t been eliminated does not mean that everyone has benefited economically from technological progress—far from it. The Luddites, for instance, had a good case  that automation in the textile industry contributed to the immiseration of their communities. Third, as emphasized by the Nobel Prize–winning economists Daron Acemoglu and Simon Johnson, technology is not fate. Technology is human-made; it is not a force of nature, the consequences of which we simply must live with. Technology is the product of a long series of choices made in society. These choices must be made in both the development and the deployment of technology. Who makes these choices is highly consequential; it determines who gains and who loses from technological progress. 
 How Technology Impacts Wages (in Theory) 
 Economists tend to think about technology in terms of  production functions : how much  output  a business can produce for any given combination of  inputs . These inputs can include the number of workers with different kinds of expertise who are employed by the business, the machinery in operation, the amount of energy consumed in the production process, and so on. The managers of a  profit-maximizing company  in a  competitive market  must decide how much of these different inputs to employ, including, in particular, how many workers to hire. In doing so, they need to make a series of cost-benefit calculations. For each worker that they might hire, profit-maximizing managers must ask what wage the company needs to pay this worker and, in turn, how much additional output the worker will produce; the manager wants the latter to be greater than the former. If the additional output is worth more on the market than the wage that the worker would earn, then the manager will decide to hire the worker; otherwise, they will not. 
 The additional output for a newly hired worker is called their  marginal productivity . This additional output from a new worker is in general different from the  average productivity  of workers who have already been hired at the company. The average productivity is defined as the total output of the company divided by the total number  of workers. When a profit-maximizing company makes hiring decisions, what matters for their cost-benefit calculation is the marginal productivity (how much output is increased by hiring the additional new worker), and not the average productivity (how much total output the existing workers produce per worker). 
 According to textbook economics, technological progress matters because it allows companies to produce more with the same inputs of materials and labor. Technological progress never decreases the total output from a given set of inputs; the company can always just stick to the old technologies, and thus make at least the same profit as before, if new technologies don’t increase output. Technological progress therefore unambiguously increases the  average productivity  of workers at any given level of inputs. 
 Here is a crucial point, though: The effect of technological progress on  marginal productivity —on the additional output produced with the addition of another worker—is very much ambiguous. As new technologies are introduced in the workplace, marginal productivity might go up, or it might go down. 
 Consider the industrial knitting machines that the Luddites destroyed. Suppose, just for illustration, that these machines were used at a company that had a fixed amount of wool available for knitting. Before the introduction of the machines, the company needed twenty workers to do the knitting. With the new machines, they only needed ten. The other ten workers became redundant—that is, their marginal productivity went down to zero, since there was no wool left for them to knit. Because the workers’ marginal productivity went down, the profit-maximizing company would not hire them anymore. The remaining workers, furthermore, did not need the training of skilled textile artisans with years of apprenticeship. These artisans could therefore be replaced by lower-paid workers with less training. In this example, profits go up, but workers lose their jobs, or earn lower wages, due to the introduction of a new technology. 
 Figure 8  illustrates an example of a technology that leads to  growth without shared prosperity—where workers are automated away. The production functions, which tell us how much output the company can produce, increase with the number of workers employed—more workers means more output. And with the introduction of a new technology, the company can produce more for any given number of workers. With this new technology, however, the marginal productivity (the amount that output increases when hiring an additional worker) goes down. This means that the slope of the production function, which measures the marginal productivity, becomes flatter. This reduction of marginal productivity leads to a decrease in wages. 
 Figure 8  Technological progress without shared prosperity 
 The opposite case, where marginal productivity increases due to technical progress, is also quite possible. As an example, consider how office workplaces have changed since the advent of personal computers and the internet. Tasks that previously took a long time—such as typing and editing documents or communicating across the globe—became much easier to complete. (I am, for instance, very glad that I do not need to use a mechanical typewriter, where I would need to retype everything to revise the manuscript for the book that you are reading right now.) These technological innovations in office  workplaces have been accompanied by an increase in the demand for knowledge workers, whose productivity has improved, and who have seen rising wages. 
 These two examples, of knitting machines and of office computers, illustrate how new technologies can either automate workers away or augment worker productivity and increase labor demand. The dividing line between automation and augmentation might often be quite thin. Office computers might have increased the marginal productivity of high-wage workers, but they also automated away jobs of certain administrative staff. Knitting machines might have automated away the jobs of highly trained artisans, but they increased demand for workers with less training. 
 How should we evaluate such technological shifts as a society? We should care about marginal productivity, because in a capitalist economy marginal productivity determines who benefits and who loses from technological progress, both in terms of employment prospects and in terms of wages. To understand this, consider first the perspective of a single company. From the perspective of this company, wages are typically fixed. This is true for a company in a competitive labor market but also for one whose wages are set by collective bargaining, as is the case in many European economies. If wages are fixed for a given company, then changes in marginal productivity affect employment at this company: If marginal productivity for certain workers goes up, then the employment prospects of these workers improve; if their marginal productivity goes down, then their employment prospects worsen. 
 Consider now the aggregate economy, rather than just a single company. In the aggregate economy, wages are not fixed; they might respond to changes in employment prospects. If some workers experience high unemployment rates, then their bargaining position is weakened, and they will not be able to command high wages. This is true regardless of whether they bargain individually or collectively.  Conversely, if certain workers have many potential jobs to choose from, then they are in a good position to bargain. They will therefore obtain better wages and better working conditions. 
 The bottom line of all these arguments from economic theory is that technological progress does not automatically lead to a general increase in prosperity. Technological progress does increase average productivity, but it might lead to either decreases or increases of marginal productivity among different groups of workers. Those whose marginal productivity is decreased—because of automation—can see their economic situation worsened, sometimes dramatically. 
 Real labor markets do not always work in this way, where companies simply take wages as given and adjust employment accordingly. There are several reasons for this. One reason is that real labor markets are typically not competitive, as first recognized by the twentieth-century economist Joan Robinson: In a competitive labor market, companies simply must accept and pay a given wage level for workers. They are not able to hire any workers below that wage level, but they can hire as many workers as they wish above that wage level. In a real labor market, by contrast, companies can hire more workers if they raise the wage, but not infinitely more. This is true in particular because all jobs are a bit different, and because it takes time to find appropriate job candidates. As a consequence companies have  market power , which allows them to pay below the marginal productivity of workers. A second reason why companies don’t simply take wages as given is that workers are more motivated and productive if they are paid higher wages. And a third reason is that workers learn employer-specific skills over time, which they cannot simply carry over to another employer. 
 These deviations from competitive labor markets are important when assessing policies such as minimum wage increases, and they imply that wages typically differ from marginal productivity. Nonetheless, marginal productivity remains a central element in a company’s  cost-benefit calculations, even under these more complicated conditions. A company with market power, for instance, will still compare the marginal product of an additional worker to the marginal cost of hiring one more worker, even if this marginal cost is different from the wage paid to that worker. In particular, it remains the case that technological change can either lead to increases or decreases of the economic well-being of different types of workers. 
 Empirical Trends of Wages and Employment 
 Because economic theory is ambiguous about who wins or loses from technological progress, a large empirical (i.e., evidence-based, as opposed to theoretical) literature in economics tries to document and summarize what has actually happened in the labor market in recent decades, in particular in the United States. Perhaps unsurprisingly, the reality is complicated. 
 One influential summary of shifts in the labor market from the late 1980s to the early 2000s became known as the story of  skill-biased technical change . According to this account, popularized by Claudia Goldin (recipient of the 2023 Nobel Memorial Prize in Economic Sciences), Larry Katz, and others, technological change has systematically favored workers with higher levels of education, increasing their marginal productivity, while automating away the jobs of workers with lower levels of education, decreasing their marginal productivity. This has led to a widening gap of wages across education groups, and thus to rising income inequality. This growing gap can only be countered through a general increase in levels of education, so that shifts in labor supply counter the shifts in labor demand. But the share of workers with higher degrees, which had massively increased over the preceding decades, has stalled since the 1980s. Therefore, the technologically induced shifts in labor demand have not been met by corresponding shifts in labor supply, and the gap of  wages and employment prospects has widened since the end of the 1970s.  This story has been criticized for neglecting the role of policy changes and the weakening of organized labor that took place during the same period, attributing rising inequality instead to a seemingly natural and unstoppable development of technology. This story furthermore does not seem consistent with shifts in the labor markets of other rich countries, even though these other countries presumably were exposed to similar technological shifts. Finally, this story does not explain the meteoric rise of the top 1 percent of incomes, which was by far the largest contributor to rising inequality since the 1980s. 
 The accounts of Goldin, Katz, and others also do not seem to fit with the shifts in labor-market opportunities that can be observed since the turn of the millennium. Instead, as noted by economists  David Autor, Melissa Kearney, and others, it appears that since about the year 2000, the middle of the wage distribution has fallen behind, while workers both at the bottom and at the top have experienced wage growth. This pattern of a disappearing middle has been described as a  polarization  of the labor market. This pattern has again been interpreted as reflecting underlying technological shifts of labor demand. According to such an interpretation, technologies like the personal computer or the internet made many routine clerical tasks redundant, thereby eliminating administrative office jobs, while making high-wage workers more productive. The same might be true for the impact of industrial robots, eliminating comparatively well-paid blue-collar jobs. Workers at the bottom of the income distribution, by contrast, who are often engaged in physical care-work or service-sector jobs that require personal interactions with customers, were largely unaffected by these new technologies. 
 It seems unclear whether the same polarization pattern persists in more recent years. Since the COVID-19 pandemic, in particular, it appears that labor-market prospects have started improving for those at the bottom of the income distribution, for the first time in several decades. This brings us to the present day, where AI, and tools based on machine learning more generally, seem destined to have big impacts in the workplace. 
 We can only speculate on the impact that generative AI and related technologies will have. The recent debate has emphasized a few possible directions. For one, the ideal of artificial intelligence as an imitation of human intelligence, as operationalized in Turing’s  imitation game , suggests a general push toward automation (replacing humans) rather than augmentation. Attempts to imitate human capabilities will lead to technologies that replace humans, because this technology will be able to complete similar tasks. This contrasts with a potential focus on technological capabilities that are systematically different from human capabilities, thus augmenting the latter in a way that would increase marginal productivity and labor demand. 
 Recent advances in AI might also allow automation of the tasks of workers that are higher up in the income distributions, relative to earlier waves of technical innovation. One example is routine legal tasks, such as the drafting of contracts. Another example is routine medical diagnostic tasks, such as the use of image recognition to detect embolisms, thereby replacing radiologists. 
 Some recent experimental studies point in a similar direction. Among workers in online customer services who were randomly given access to AI-based assistants, novice workers and those considered less productive benefited most from these assistants, while experienced and productive workers did not benefit. A possible explanation is that generative AI sets a baseline quality of work. Those workers who would otherwise fall below this baseline stand to gain from the new tools, while those workers who would exceed the baseline without AI assistance benefit less. If this pattern generalizes, AI tools might serve as an equalizer of labor demand. 
 Only the future will tell, however, whether this pattern generalizes. And, most importantly, the future is undetermined. How technology develops and how it is used is not a given. Both development and deployment are the products of social choices, large and small. Corresponding to the theoretical ambiguity in how new technologies shift labor demand, there is a practical indeterminacy that means that these technologies might indeed shift labor demand in different ways, depending on our choices. This practical indeterminacy opens the possibility of democratic decision-making. 
 Democratic Control of Design and Deployment 
 In the 1970s and 1980s, engineers and union activists in Norway, Sweden, and Denmark initiated several ambitious experiments. These experiments were the starting point for what came to be known, in the theory and practice of design taught in design schools, as the  Scandinavian participatory design approach , or  collective resources   approach . This approach aimed to promote workplace democracy—to give workers and unions a say in the development and deployment of technology in the workplace. It was pioneered by the Norwegian Iron and Metal Workers Union in a joint project with academic computer scientists. Their work was followed by the DEMOS project, initiated by the Swedish Trade Union Confederation in 1975, the UTOPIA project of the Nordic Graphic Workers’ Union in 1981, and the Florence project, launched in 1984 as a cooperation of nurses and computer scientists. The goal of all these projects was the same: to have direct involvement and control of workers in the design of technology for the workplace, including software development. 
 According to the founders of the collective resources approach, the role of workers was to go beyond mere consultation of management. Instead, they sought to empower workers by helping local unions to develop their own knowledge and expertise, to enlist the active support of local members, and to avoid being overpowered by management in decisions about technology. National unions and academic experts were supposed to assist in the dissemination of expertise, to provide training, and to develop national framework agreements. 
 The Scandinavian participatory design approach benefited from its development in a context of strong unions and established workplace codetermination, where workers have an active role in decision-making. The Scandinavian approach has served as an inspiration for a larger movement toward participatory design, including in the United States. Outside the Scandinavian context of strong unions and codetermination, however, the focus has shifted toward the involvement of users in the development of commercial products, and away from a focus on workplace democracy. 
 The Scandinavian experience of the collective resources approach might serve as a model for a broader shift of control over technological choices, in the workplace and beyond. It also illustrates how any shared control needs to be based on a real redistribution of power.  Power of workers in the workplace can be based on different sources. It can be based on the ability to withhold labor. It can be based on organizational capacity and unity among workers, across the diverse set of occupations in a company or sector. And it can be based on legal rights of workers. German law, for example, mandates worker codetermination at the company level, in companies beyond a certain size, allocating a certain share of board seats to worker representatives, and requiring worker representation in establishment-level decisions. 
 In addition to power, the collective resources approach requires a willingness and ability of local unions to be actively involved in technological decision-making. Local union representatives need to conceive of their own role in broader terms, instead of thinking of themselves only as wages negotiators for a narrowly defined constituency. The collective resources approach requires imagination and a willingness to engage with alternative technological possibilities, and to risk disagreements with management. 
 To make it possible for workers’ representatives to think bigger about the ways new technologies could be used, an understanding of the technological possibilities is required. For AI, that means an understanding of the technology as optimization of some measurable objectives. As discussed before, it is typically only of secondary importance to know how AI solves its decision problems, what neural network architecture it uses, and so forth. What is more important is to know the objective that is being optimized, and over what possible choices. Workers involved in codetermination over AI for automated decision-making thus need to explore and consider possible alternative objectives. 
 To make things more concrete, consider semiautomated warehouses, such as those operated by Amazon. The physical movements of workers in these warehouses are integrated in a tightly scheduled rhythm that is dictated by robots, while humans are in charge  of tasks in packaging and shipping that are, thus far, hard to automate. The current objectives of these systems are presumably dictated by profit maximization, which involves maximizing the speed of shipping, and maximizing the throughput per human operator, with a high degree of worker surveillance. According to recent investigations by the US Department of Labor, these working conditions expose warehouse workers to a high risk of lower back injuries and other musculoskeletal disorders. This is due to the high frequency with which employees must lift packages, the heavy weight of the items, and the required awkward twisting, bending, and extending, as well as long working hours. The medium-term goal of Amazon might furthermore be to eliminate human workers in these warehouses as far as possible. 
 Imagine what would happen if the objectives of the AI systems in these warehouses were instead chosen by workers. Rather than maximizing the speed of deliveries and maximizing throughput per worker, these workers would presumably choose to assign a higher importance to their own safety, to a reduced risk of injury and burnout, and to a preservation of human agency, among the AI objectives to be maximized. 
 Another example of how workers might choose different objectives for AI is in the workplaces of nurses and health-care workers. Several companies, in particular in Japan and the United States, are developing robots that are intended to replace human nurses. These robots might engage in tasks such as lifting patients or turning them in bed, and they might engage in “conversations” with lonely elderly people. It stands to reason that the replacement of human interactions by interactions with robots, for isolated and lonely elderly patients, is not necessarily improving the welfare of these patients, nor is it in the interest of health-care workers to be replaced by robots. One might imagine different AI-use cases and objectives that augment human nurse capabilities, for instance by helping nurses with  diagnosing conditions, predicting injury risks for patients, or facilitating access to relevant information about medical treatments. It seems likely that human nurses would rather choose such augmentation objectives. 
 These are speculative examples, and it is hard to predict (especially for an outsider to these occupations, like me) what technological possibilities the workers in these domains will face. These examples, however, serve to illustrate our general points: Technical innovations have wide-ranging consequences for the labor market, improving the fortunes of some and worsening the prospects of others. There is no theoretical reason that technological advances will lead to broadly shared prosperity, and in history there have typically been winners and losers of such advances. AI will be no different, but which way these shifts go is a matter of social choices rather than of inevitable fate. We need broad democratic participation in technological choices to get a fair distribution of the benefits of AI. But what does it even mean for the distribution to be fair?

 Fairness 
 Fairness is often understood to be the opposite of discrimination. Different people, however, have very different ideas what  discrimination  means. 
 In 1957, the economist Gary Becker at the University of Chicago published his book  The Economics of Discrimination . In the introduction to this book, Becker described his goal of developing a general theory of discrimination: 
 This theory can be applied to “discrimination” and “nepotism” in all their diverse forms, . . . since they have in common the use of non-monetary considerations in deciding whether to hire, work with, or buy from an individual or group. 
 A few years later, in 1963, Martin Luther King Jr. gave his famous “I Have a Dream” speech on the steps of the Lincoln Memorial in Washington, DC. At the outset of the speech, King stated: 
 But 100 years later, the Negro still is not free. One hundred years later, the life of the Negro is still sadly crippled by the manacles of segregation and the chains of discrimination. One hundred years later, the Negro lives on a lonely island of poverty in the midst of a vast ocean of material prosperity. . . . 
 Now is the time to make real the promises of democracy. Now is the time to rise from the dark and desolate valley of segregation to the sunlit path of racial justice. Now is the time to lift our nation from the quick sands of racial injustice to the solid rock of brotherhood. Now is the time to make justice a reality for all of God’s children. 
 In Becker’s book, discrimination is the opposite of profit maximization. According to Becker, when people and businesses act according to purely monetary motives, they don’t discriminate. King takes a very different perspective. In King’s speech, segregation, discrimination, and poverty are the opposite of democracy, racial justice, and brotherhood. For Becker, discrimination would be defeated if profits were maximized—no matter what levels of inequality result, between or within racial groups. For King, discrimination would be defeated if integration, democracy, and brotherhood were achieved. 
 Becker’s vision, and not King’s, has served as a reference point for most work on discrimination in economics in subsequent decades. And it is Becker’s vision that has been renewed in the more recent debates on  algorithmic fairness , and its opposites,  algorithmic bias  and  algorithmic discrimination . 
 One way to interpret Becker’s notion of discrimination is that people have non-monetary motivations, and we can study the economic consequences of these non-monetary motivations. Many economists would emphasize this (descriptive) interpretation, which seems fairly uncontroversial. But there is also a social and moral (normative) interpretation, which says that discrimination is bad, and therefore its opposite—according to Becker, unconstrained profit maximization—is good. If we apply this moral interpretation to the question of algorithmic fairness and bias, we have to conclude that algorithmic bias is bad and unconstrained maximization of AI objectives, which were chosen by the corporate owners of the means of prediction, is good. 
 The two visions reflected in the quotes from Becker and King suggest two different paradigms for thinking about discrimination, whether by humans or by algorithms. Without any pretension of doing justice to the actual views of either Becker or King, these two paradigms can be summarized as follows. 
 The first paradigm, in line with Becker’s quote, is the  fairness paradigm ; the second one, in line with King’s quote, is the  welfare paradigm . Fairness is about deservingness: Did everyone get what they deserve based on their merit? Welfare is about how well people are doing: Is everyone free from deprivation? Fairness is about justifying the causes of unequal treatment: Can different wages across groups be rationalized by differences in productivity? Welfare, meanwhile, is about evaluating the consequences of unequal treatment: Who wins and who loses from the introduction of some hiring algorithm? Fairness sets its benchmark at unbridled profit maximization—that is, the absence of non-monetary motives. Welfare’s benchmark is the just allocation of resources to those who need them the most. 
 Becker’s book on discrimination was written long before the age of AI and machine learning, but its concepts of discrimination are reproduced by approaches that are in use today in machine learning. As we have discussed, AI and machine learning are concerned with optimization; failures of AI are understood as failures to optimize. Becker understood discrimination as a failure to maximize profits. AI algorithms that are controlled by corporations are designed to maximize profits. Failures of AI algorithms to maximize their objective, then, are failures to maximize profits, and therefore can be interpreted as algorithmic discrimination, or unfairness. 
 The welfare paradigm tells us something different about the evaluation of possible failures of AI. Based on the welfare paradigm, we should worry less about optimization failures and more about the question of what it is that is being optimized—that is, whose welfare matters and who is choosing the objectives. 
 Taste-Based Discrimination and Algorithmic Bias 
 Let us now take a closer look at  taste-based discrimination , as defined by Becker, and at  algorithmic bias , as defined by computer scientists. 
 Consider a profit-maximizing company choosing a person to hire. As discussed earlier, the company must compare the cost of hiring an additional worker (i.e., the wage in a competitive labor market) to the benefit of hiring this additional worker. The benefit is determined by the worker’s marginal product (i.e., how much this worker would increase output, and thus profits, if hired). For a profit-maximizing company, nothing else matters. No non-monetary considerations affect a profit-maximizing company’s hiring decisions. 
 But suppose now instead that other considerations do enter this company’s hiring decisions. Suppose that this company discriminates against women. According to Gary Becker’s definition, the presence of taste-based discrimination implies that the company is doing something other than maximizing profits. In particular, if the company discriminates against women, this means by definition that the company could increase profits by changing its hiring practices, by hiring more women and fewer men than it currently does. Put differently, the company is foregoing profits by hiring men who have lower marginal productivity (or higher wages), instead of hiring women. 
 If there are no possible changes of hiring practices that would increase profits—if everything is maximized already—then everything is fine; no taste-based discrimination is present. Profits are as good as they get, which means there is no discrimination. 
 This is where the limitations of Becker’s approach become apparent. Notably, the absence of taste-based discrimination is consistent with the presence of arbitrary levels of inequality in wages or job prospects between men and women. For example, expectations about future care obligations of job candidates—obligations that still fall disproportionally on women—might be part of the profit-maximizing  cost-benefit calculations for hiring; if a company takes these things into account, it does not constitute taste-based discrimination, as defined by Becker. 
 There is a striking ideological sleight of hand that happens when discussions of inequality are reduced to matters of taste-based discrimination. Debates about discrimination set out to name and address injustices, in the labor market and elsewhere. They set out to speak for the interests of disadvantaged groups—in the above example, women job candidates. But rather than considering the welfare of these disadvantaged groups, as King did, the notion of taste-based discrimination considers the welfare of capital owners. It asks whether profits have been maximized. If profits have been maximized, no injustice is present, according to this notion. 
 This is a book about AI, not about the potential bigotry of hiring managers. But the very same considerations that apply to hiring managers also apply to algorithms. Consider algorithms that recommend to hiring managers whom to hire. Such algorithms can be based on supervised learning—trained on data for previously hired workers, where these data link the marginal productivity  Y  of hired workers (or, more likely, some observable proxies for marginal productivity) to features  X  of their job applications, such as their résumés and letters of recommendation. Supervised learning yields predictions of marginal productivity  Y  given applicant features  X . For a new job applicant, then, these predictions of marginal productivity  Y  are used to make a hiring recommendation: If the predicted marginal productivity  Y  exceeds the wage that the job candidate would earn, and if the goal is to maximize profits, the candidate should be hired. If predicted marginal productivity  Y  is less than the wage, then the candidate should not be hired. 
 A hiring algorithm thus takes the features  X  of a job candidate as its input and produces a recommendation as its output, recommending whether to hire this candidate or not. What would it mean for such an algorithm to be discriminating against some demographic  group, say women? If we adapt the notion of taste-based discrimination to algorithms, then algorithmic bias would be present if there were a way of tweaking the hiring algorithm that would yield higher profits. If the algorithm is discriminating against women, then this might be because its predictions are biased: The algorithmic predictions of productivity  Y  might be too low for women, or too high for men. By adjusting these biased predictions, more women would be hired, and profits would be increased. Discrimination in this sense, then, is a failure to succeed at the goal of supervised learning, which is to deliver accurate predictions of  Y  given  X . A failure to minimize predictive errors translates into a failure to maximize profits. 
 Concerns over the social impact of algorithmic decision-making have led to an outpouring of research that aims to define, measure, and address algorithmic bias. There is more than one notion of algorithmic bias in the literature. In fact, researchers have come up with hundreds of such definitions. Animated debates have taken place about which of these definitions of fairness and bias is most appropriate. Some well-known articles have argued that some of these different definitions contradict each other. But for the most part, these different definitions are all very closely related in spirit. 
 These definitions in the literature on algorithmic bias and fairness have technical names such as  balance for the positive class ,  balance for the negative class ,  equality of false positives ,  equality of false negatives ,  equality of opportunity ,  conditional statistical parity , and  individual fairness . These definitions might compare whether average productivity among those who were hired is the same for men and women, or average productivity among those not hired, or both. They might compare whether hiring probabilities are the same among men and women of high productivity, or whether hiring probabilities are the same for men and women of low productivity. They might ask whether hiring probabilities are the same among men and women who look the same in terms of other features  X  that are predictive of productivity. They might ask whether more generally hiring probabilities  are the same across any individuals who are the same in terms of relevant characteristics  X . 
 Suppose that it is possible to perfectly predict productivity  Y , based on the features  X , and suppose that hiring decisions are based just on predicted productivity. In this case, all the definitions of fairness that we just enumerated agree. In this case, all these definitions of fairness conclude that there is no bias once profits are perfectly maximized. 
 In our discussion thus far, we have emphasized the context of hiring and discrimination based on gender. But similar considerations apply across many domains, and across different axes of discrimination. Debates about algorithmic fairness were in fact jump-started in 2016 by a controversy around risk-assessment tools that are used in US courts. These tools are based on predictions of  recidivism  (future police encounters) of defendants in court. Judges decide whether to jail defendants who await trial based on predicted recidivism. Investigative reporters from ProPublica raised concerns that some of these tools are biased against Black defendants, which led to lively debates. Other domains where similar concerns about algorithmic fairness apply include the admission of students to universities, the allocation of home mortgages to families, online individualized pricing, and the allocation of medical treatments, to name but a few. 
 The Effect of Algorithmic Decisions on Welfare 
 Starting with the quotes of Gary Becker and Martin Luther King Jr., we contrasted the fairness paradigm to the welfare paradigm. We then discussed the notion of fairness in detail. Let us now turn to the notion of welfare. 
 When asking about the impact of an algorithm on welfare, we are asking about the  consequences  of the fact that different people are treated differently by the algorithm. We are not asking what the  causes  of these differing treatments are, which would be the concern  of fairness. As an example, consider a public employment agency’s allocation of a training program to unemployed workers. Algorithms are increasingly used to decide which workers are offered training programs. How should we assess the impact of these algorithms on worker welfare? 
 To answer this question, we must first consider the  causal effect  of the training program on workers’ job prospects. Training is typically not an end in itself. Instead, one might hope that an unemployed worker’s labor-market prospects are improved if they attend the training program, and that their improved labor-market prospects in turn improve their welfare. To assess the algorithm and the training program, we therefore must ask how much each worker’s prospects are improved by attending the training program. 
 The distinction between treatment (e.g., training programs) and welfare (e.g., job prospects) is important. Equal treatment typically implies unequal outcomes. The reason is that people start from unequal places. This important point is often missed in discussions around fairness. Most notions of fairness aim to equalize treatment, conditional on merit, rather than equalizing welfare. 
 The following example illustrates the point: Consider the impact of eyeglasses on effective eyesight. It clearly makes no sense to treat everyone the same by giving everyone the same eyeglasses. Instead, we want eyeglasses that compensate for preexisting inequalities. Different glasses are needed for people with myopia and for people with farsightedness. No glasses are needed for those blessed with naturally good vision. As this example illustrates, unequal treatment can lead to equal welfare, in terms of effective vision. Unequal treatment can compensate for preexisting inequalities and can, at the same time, make everyone better off. Inequalities in welfare (effective vision) can be eliminated—even if baseline inequality of vision is fully genetically determined. By contrast, equal treatment of everyone, with the same type of glasses, would not lead to an equalization, or to a general improvement, of welfare. 
 This example shows that, when we consider the effect of an algorithm on the welfare of those impacted by the algorithm, we need to consider that people are different to begin with. This matters in two important ways. First, the effect of treatment on welfare varies across people. This is known as  heterogeneity of causal effects . The same training program might be useful for one worker but a waste of time for another worker. The same eyeglasses might improve vision for one person but make vision worse for another person. Second, there is  baseline inequality  in welfare. Some workers will do fine even without the training program. They will find a meaningful and well-compensated job either way. Other workers will have a much harder time. They will struggle to find any acceptable employment. Some people have naturally good vision, other people don’t. 
 To assess an allocation algorithm, we need to consider its impact on social welfare. Social welfare, as discussed in  chapter 10 , is an aggregation of individual welfare. A useful way to think of this aggregation is in terms of welfare weights. Welfare weights determine how much we care about improving the welfare of different people, from a social perspective. Typically, we care more about improving the welfare of those who are worst off, relative to those who are doing fine either way. We care more about improving the labor-market prospects of those who have a hard time finding employment, relative to those who can be expected to find well-paid employment anyway. This suggests that good algorithms should offer the training program to those with the worst prospects. Additionally, it matters that the training is effective in terms of improving job prospects. This suggests that good algorithms should offer the training program to those who would benefit the most from attending. Both forms of inequality across people matter when we assess the impact of the algorithm—inequality in baseline welfare and heterogeneity of causal effects. Good algorithms, from the perspective of social welfare, target those who need the program the most and those who benefit the most. 
 Let us now again take a closer look at how we define individual  welfare. We have already emphasized that training attendance is different from job prospects, so that treatment is not the same as welfare. But are job prospects equal to welfare? We need to be careful. If we wanted to maximize the probability that people find jobs, we could institute forced labor programs. This certainly would not maximize the welfare of workers. The same is true for slightly less draconian measures, such as cutting unemployment benefits or instituting regimes of surveillance and control of unemployed workers. If we think of welfare as utility, as economists tend to do, then these measures would reduce worker welfare. Using algorithms to maximize job-finding probabilities might quickly lead to a version of the paper-clip-maximizing AI, discussed in  chapter 14 , where maximization of a seemingly sensible goal (production of paper clips, or jobs for the unemployed) can lead to disastrous consequences. To increase welfare, we need to increase the set of options available to workers. Training programs seem less dangerous: They do plausibly increase options, or at least do not decrease them, assuming that participation is voluntary. 
 To conclude our discussion of fairness and welfare, let’s return to the example of a hiring algorithm used by a company to select interviewees from a pool of job applicants. Let us consider different ways of tweaking such an algorithm, and how these tweaks would be assessed under either the fairness paradigm or the welfare paradigm. These examples illustrate how the fairness paradigm and the welfare paradigm can lead to very different assessments of whether an algorithm or a policy change is good or bad. 
 Consider, first, what happens if the algorithm gets better at predicting productivity. This might be due to increased data collection, or due to better machine learning methods. Improved predictions imply that hiring decisions are more closely aligned with actual productivity. This, in turn, implies that most fairness metrics improve—because profits improve. In the extreme, if productivity becomes perfectly predictable, all fairness metrics will conclude that there  is no bias in hiring decisions. But what happens to welfare? Some people win and some people lose as predictability improves. Typically, those with better economic prospects (whose productivity is higher) win, as predicted productivity gets closer to their true productivity. Those with worse economic prospects lose. Their probability of finding a job decreases. Improved predictions thus tend to be good for fairness but bad for the welfare of those who are worse off, and thus bad for equality of welfare. 
 Consider now instead what happens if some form of affirmative action is instituted, for instance to compensate for the current gender imbalance among hired workers: The algorithm is tweaked to give women a higher chance of being invited for job interviews and therefore being hired. Such affirmative action is unambiguously bad for fairness, according to standard definitions, because tweaking the algorithm in this way is bad for profits. In Becker’s terminology, this affirmative action involves non-monetary considerations, which is his definition of discrimination. What about welfare? Women can be expected to gain from this tweak to the algorithm, while men lose. If, at baseline, the hiring chances of men were higher than those of women, then this decreases inequality of welfare. And if social welfare furthermore assigns a higher weight to those who are worse off, then social welfare is improved. 
 Fairness and welfare are two normative paradigms: They describe what should happen in an ideal world, from different vantage points. How do these normative paradigms relate to what happens in practice? 
 What actually happens is determined by those who control the means of prediction. If algorithms are controlled by capital owners, algorithm objectives will be chosen to maximize profits. The maximization of profits requires the avoidance of non-monetary considerations. Control by capital owners thus translates into a desire to avoid taste-based discrimination, and in particular algorithmic bias. 
 What if, instead, the objectives of an algorithm were subject to  democratic control? Then those who exert democratic control can be expected to choose algorithm objectives that maximize their welfare. There remains the question of how the welfare of different people is traded off by the algorithm. How this trade-off is resolved must be decided by the democratic process. One problem that the democratic process will encounter is that those who have a pivotal role in democratic decisions can be expected to receive larger weights. 
 Democratic decisions require democratic deliberations. To start deliberating, we need  explanations  of what AI systems are doing. Similarly, to know whether you have been treated unfairly and have been discriminated against, you need an  explanation  of how some algorithmic decision was obtained. But what would it mean to explain an algorithm, or to explain an algorithmic decision?

 Explainability 
 Josef K. is a bank clerk and the protagonist of Franz Kafka’s novel  Der Prozess  ( The Trial ), written in 1914–15. On the morning of his thirtieth birthday, Josef K. is arrested. He is told to appear at court the coming Sunday, without being informed where the court is located or what time to appear. He ultimately finds the court in the attic of a working-class apartment building. Josef K. tries to defend himself, but he does not learn what crime he is accused of. The trial takes place over the course of a year, increasingly interfering with Josef K.’s life. The proceedings of the court bureaucracy remain mysterious, and only the lowest echelons of this bureaucracy ever become visible. Josef K. never learns the nature of the crime he is accused of nor the verdict that the court has reached. On the eve of his thirty-first birthday, Josef K. is executed in a quarry. 
 Kafka wrote  The Trial  more than a century ago, but just as Josef K. was subject to the decisions of the courts, people today are subject to the decisions of opaque algorithms. Algorithmic decision-making systems are everywhere: Predictions of future exam success determine who will receive a university education. Human resource algorithms determine who will be invited for a job interview and who will remain unemployed. Credit scores determine who can buy a home. Risk-assessment tools determine who will go to jail while awaiting trial. Military algorithms determine whose houses get bombed. Why  did you not get a degree? Why did you remain unemployed? Why could you not buy a home? Why did you end up in jail? Why did you have a bomb dropped on your house? Because the algorithm said so. Trust the process. 
 Our emerging AI overlords bear more than a faint resemblance to the inscrutable systems of Kafka’s novel. Some machine learning algorithms might be simpler than others, but when it comes to the deep neural networks of modern AI, nobody really knows what is going on inside the  black box —even when we understand what the black box is designed to do. The classifications, predictions, or decisions of these networks are determined by millions or billions of weights. Even knowing all these weights would not tell us much about the underlying reasons for a decision that was made by such a network. 
 What is to be done? Should we simply accept algorithmic decisions, having faith that all will be for the best? Or should we demand explanations? But what would it mean to  explain  algorithmic decisions? This latter question has attracted a lot of recent attention in computer science. (Computer science sometimes distinguishes between  interpretability  and  explainability , but I use these terms interchangeably.) 
 Below I outline three kinds of explainability, each of which might be useful for different groups, to interrogate and understand algorithms. The first kind aims to  explain decision functions : How are inputs  X  mapped into decisions by an algorithm? Explanations of decision functions might be useful for AI engineers. The second kind of explainability aims to  explain decisions : Why was a particular decision made in a particular instance? Explanations of decisions might be useful for those who are adversely affected by an AI system. The third kind of explainability aims to  explain decision problems : What is the AI system trying to achieve and based on what information? Explanations of decision problems are necessary for democratic control. We need to understand the objectives of an AI system to be able to align these objectives with social welfare maximization. These  different kinds of explainability correspond to the goals of different stakeholders—debugging for AI engineers, individual recourse for those affected by an AI system, and democratic governance for the public at large. 
 Explaining Decision Functions 
 It is not obvious that an AI engineer should care about explainability of decision functions. As emphasized throughout this book, AI solves decision problems. The criterion of success for an AI system is given by the objective of the corresponding decision problem. For supervised learning, for example, the criterion of success is whether the trained algorithm can successfully predict outcomes  Y  for new observations of features  X . Why is this not the end of the story—why should we probe algorithms further, and try to explain them, rather than just evaluating this criterion of success? 
 There is a range of reasons that AI researchers have given to motivate their interest in explainability. A common theme across these different reasons is that the specification of decision problems is often  incomplete . We have already encountered a version of this problem in  chapter 14 —recall the incomplete objective of the paper-clip-maximizing machine. Algorithms for supervised learning, more generally, maximize predictive performance for out-of-sample observations. But in addition to predictive performance, other concerns matter. 
 One such concern is  transferability : Will the algorithm continue to deliver good predictions on new problem instances? Will it do so for problem instances that look different from the training data? We might be more inclined to trust that transferability holds if the model’s predictions or decisions are driven by sensible variation in the data, as opposed to random artifacts. Related to transferability is the question of  robustness , including robustness to  adversarial attacks : Can minor perturbations of the inputs, which might be imperceptible  to humans, lead to radically different decisions? Another variant of this concern is the desire to discover  causal  relationships: Predictive algorithms learn correlational patterns in the data. But will these correlational patterns continue to hold if someone intervenes in a way that changes some of the features  X ? Stability of the patterns under interventions will only hold if the patterns are indeed causal. There might, finally, be  legal  or  ethical  concerns that matter, in addition to the primary goal of predictive performance. One example is a worry about potential algorithmic discrimination and fairness. 
 These concerns motivate attempts to explain decision functions. The hope is that explanations of decision functions will allow AI engineers to address these concerns. Unfortunately, it is hard to formalize what we mean by an  explanation . The need for explanations arises because the specification of the objective of a decision problem is incomplete. This specification is typically incomplete precisely because it is hard to formalize the additional concerns that are missing from the problem specification. And because these concerns are hard to formalize, it is also hard to formalize a solution that addresses them. It is hard to formalize explainability of decision functions, in particular. 
 Here is a quick overview of some possible solutions to the problem of explainability that have been proposed in the literature. One possible solution is to use  simple models . What a simple model is depends, of course, on the eyes of the beholder. Models that are often described as  simple  include  linear models  (if they don’t use too many variables) and  decision trees  (if they don’t have too many nodes). Linear models and decision trees are considered simple because a human could conceivably calculate their predictions or decisions with a pen and paper, in a reasonable amount of time. Linear models take the input features, multiply them by some weights, and sum these weighted features to calculate a prediction or decision. Decision trees generate decisions based on a series of yes–no questions. Based  on the answers to these yes–no questions, the observation is put in a category, and the prediction, or decision, is based on this category. 
 A second, related, solution to the problem of explaining decision functions is to use  simple surrogate models . The idea is to start with a complicated machine learning model, which is typically hard to interpret but is successful at predicting out-of-sample. This complicated model can then, in a second step, be approximated by a simple model, such as a linear model. This simple model is called the surrogate model. The interpretation of the simple surrogate model is used to understand what is happening in the more complicated original model. The approximation can happen  globally , across all the possible inputs of the model, or  locally , for feature values in the neighborhood of a point of interest. 
 A third solution to the problem of explaining decision functions is to summarize local  feature importance . AI engineers often want to know how much different features contribute to the predictions of an algorithm. Attributing importance to different features is tricky, because typically it is the  interaction  of different features that leads to a certain prediction, not one feature alone—unless the model is already linear. Methods such as the so-called  Shapley values  aim to apportion importance across features, including in settings where the interaction of features matters. 
 All these approaches—using simple models, global or local surrogate models, and feature importance—tend to suffer from some related problems. One of these problems is that the simple models that are selected, or the corresponding surrogate models, tend to be very sensitive to minor variations of the data—even when the predictions that are generated by these models are not sensitive. The reason is that models that look very different to a human eye, and that would be interpreted very differently, can yield very similar predictions. This is especially true if there are multiple variables that measure roughly the same thing. 
 Explaining Decisions 
 Explanations of complicated decision functions, based on simple approximations to these decision functions, are of interest to AI researchers and engineers. That is because AI researchers and engineers want to understand the behavior of their algorithms, they want to detect possible sources of error, and they want to probe the robustness of their AI systems. Simple approximations can help with these goals. 
 But these goals of AI engineers might not be the most pertinent goals for people who are exposed to the decisions of AI systems. For such people, simple approximations of decision functions might not be all that useful. Instead, people who are subject to automated decisions might want explanations of the decisions, instead of explanations of the decision functions. This is true especially if the decisions are negative. 
 Explanations of decisions might allow individual-level (legal) recourse by allowing people who are negatively impacted to challenge decisions as illegitimate. To illustrate, think of Josef K. Josef K. would have good reason to challenge the decisions of the court, which however remain incomprehensible to him. The lack of explanation makes it impossible to challenge these decisions. Explanations of decisions might also allow people to change their behavior to obtain different decisions: If they knew what actions they will be accused of, they might be able to avoid these actions. 
 Anyone who is subject to automated decisions might thus wish to ask  why  a decision was made, to allow them to contest the decision, and to adapt their behavior to obtain a different decision. It is, however, surprisingly difficult to specify what we mean when we ask  why  questions. To explain why that is (pun intended), we need to take a little detour. We need to first talk about causality, as it is understood in economics and in the social and life sciences more broadly, before returning to why questions and to explanations of decisions.  This will allow us to talk about the causes of a decision in response to  why  questions. 
 Perhaps surprisingly, economists and other social and life scientists rarely ask  why  some outcome occurred. Instead, they ask “what if” questions. A doctor might ask what would happen to a patient’s health if they prescribed a different medical treatment. A policymaker might ask what would happen if a tax rate was raised. And we might ask what decision an AI system would have made had the inputs for this system been different. “What if” questions are closely related to  interventions . The answers to such “what if” questions are particularly useful when we need to choose interventions. Should the doctor prescribe a specific medication? Should the government raise taxes? 
 We cannot directly observe the counterfactual outcomes corresponding to “what if” scenarios. To make “what if” questions empirically meaningful, we need to introduce the idea of  controlled experiments . In a controlled experiment, multiple similar units are treated in different ways. Treatment is assigned randomly, or according to some other controlled design. By comparing average outcomes between units that were treated in different ways, we can estimate the  average causal effect  of treatment. 
 Experiments allow us to  explore  the effect of decisions. We can then later  exploit  this knowledge to make better decisions. This should sound familiar. We have already talked about causal effects of decisions earlier in this book, even if we did not use the language of causality. Multi-armed bandit algorithms are constantly running experiments, to learn about the causal effect of the different arms that the algorithms might choose. 
 Let us now come back to the problem of explaining algorithmic decisions. For algorithmic decisions, the “what if” questions of causal inference are easy to answer. For algorithmic decisions, we can simply  simulate  decisions for different input values. This is true even for black-box algorithms, for which we have no idea what is going  inside. We can repeatedly prompt an algorithm with different counterfactual input values, and we can get the corresponding potential decisions. A doctor cannot do the same. A doctor cannot both treat a patient with one medication and not treat the same patient with this medication, and then observe the outcome in both cases at the same time. The doctor has no “patient simulator.” 
 Being able to answer “what if” questions for algorithms does not immediately allow us to answer  why  questions, however. Even if we know what the algorithm’s decisions would have been if the inputs had been different, we cannot directly answer why the decision was what it was for a given set of inputs. The underlying problem was vividly articulated by the Russian author Leo Tolstoy in his novel  War and Peace . Reflecting on the Napoleonic Wars, and on explanations of historical events more broadly, Tolstoy made the point as follows: “When an apple has ripened and falls, why does it fall? Because of its attraction to the earth, because its stalk withers, because it is dried by the sun, because it grows heavier, because the wind shakes it, or because the boy standing below wants to eat it?” 
 To put Tolstoy’s point more abstractly, there is an endless list of possible answers to the  why  question. All these answers correspond to conditions that needed to be met so that the apple would fall. Does this mean that the endeavor to explain events, or algorithmic decisions, is in vain? Does this mean that we should never ask  why  a specific decision was made by an algorithm? Not quite. We can make sense of this question by specifying it further. One possible specification is given by the notion of a  counterfactual explanation . For any given set of input values for an algorithmic decision, we can ask: What is the  smallest possible change  of these input values that would have delivered some desired, different outcome? 
 This idea is illustrated in  figure 10 . Consider the example of an algorithm mapping job applications  X  into hiring decisions  Y . The gray blob in  figure 10  represents the set of possible input values  X  (job applications) for which the algorithm would have decided not to hire.  For input values outside this set, the algorithm would have decided to hire. The figure also shows the input value for a specific job applicant who was not hired. The arrow indicates the smallest possible change of this applicant’s input values that would have resulted in a different decision. 
 Figure 10  Counterfactual explanation 
 To make this more concrete, imagine that you apply for a job for which you are qualified. You submit your résumé, as well as some recommendation letters. Shortly thereafter, you receive an automated message saying that you will not be invited for an interview. An AI-based human resource system scanned your application materials and decided that you were not a suitable candidate. Understandably upset, you want to know what went wrong. Why did you not get the job that you were clearly well suited for? Why did the algorithm make this decision? 
 Suppose now that the AI system is required to give you a counterfactual explanation of its decision. This explanation might look as follows: 
 You did not get invited to an interview based on the content of your résumé and recommendation letters. You would have been invited for an interview if you had two more years of work experience,  holding all else equal in your application. You would also have been invited if you had indicated proficiency in Python coding, or if you had been a man instead of a woman. 
 Such explanations can be calculated regardless of the (machine learning) approach that was used to produce the decision function of the AI system. The three explanations in this example point toward the different purposes that explanations might serve. The first explanation says that two more years of experience would have gotten you an interview. Work experience might be considered a legitimate criterion in hiring, and there is not much you can do about this. This first explanation might nonetheless give you useful context to understand the decision. The second explanation states that indicating proficiency in Python coding would have gotten you an interview (Python is a popular programming language). This, now, is actionable information. Assuming that you are keen on the job, and that there is the opportunity to apply again in the future, you might consider improving your coding knowledge. Alternatively, you might already be a proficient Python coder, but you did not consider this to be relevant information for your résumé. In this case, you might want to appeal the decision, pointing out that it was based on incomplete information. The third explanation, finally, might suggest that something illegal is happening here. If a male job candidate would have been invited for an otherwise identical application, then this suggests that there might be illegal discrimination by the algorithm’s decision function. In this case, you might consider appealing the legality of the rejection of your application. 
 Counterfactual explanations are similar to a related idea from machine learning known as  adversarial perturbations . Computer scientists are often worried about the  robustness  of their systems, and about vulnerabilities to  adversarial attacks . In the context of image classification, for example, deep learning algorithms might be very successful at identifying different kinds of animals in photos. In the test and training data, they might always correctly distinguish  whether there is a dog or an alligator in the photo. But with only minuscule perturbations of the pixel colors in the photo, which no human eye can detect, it is easy to fool the algorithm. It is easy to make the algorithm confuse dogs with alligators every time. It is possible to find such adversarial perturbations because there are many perturbations that make the photos different from any photos that the algorithm has seen before. The technical term for this is that the perturbed images are  outside the support  of the training data. When features are outside the support, then the algorithm needs to  extrapolate . The fact that it is easy to get nonsensical extrapolations appears to indicate that the image-recognition algorithms do not rely on robust criteria to identify dogs and alligators in the way that humans do. 
 Both counterfactual explanations and adversarial perturbations are based on the idea of taking input features  X  that deliver a certain decision or prediction  Y , and then finding a  small change  of  X  that yields a very different  Y . It is fairly straightforward to define what a different  Y  is—you would have been invited to a job interview, the photo would have been identified as depicting an alligator, and so on. But what is a  small change ? There are many different directions in which we could change the high-dimensional  X  that are typical for machine learning. 
 Because of this difficulty of defining what a small change is, both counterfactual explanations and adversarial perturbations suffer from the same weakness. Both require a notion of what it means to be  close  to an initial set of inputs. This problem has plagued analytical philosophers for a while, when they attempted to formalize the concept of  why  by considering counterfactual universes with different outcomes. What does it mean for a counterfactual universe to be as close as possible to the one we live in? What does it mean for a job application to be similar to the one you submitted that got rejected? What does it mean for an image to be similar to the one that was classified as a dog? 
 For explaining decisions, two criteria are particularly relevant to decide when potential input values are close, because they lead to explanations  that are of consequence for the likes of Josef K. First, suppose that a feature, such as reported coding skills, is easily changed by the affected persons. If this feature is changed, then our measure of distance (or  metric ) should say that we remain close to the starting point. Second, suppose that a feature, such as gender, should not matter for decisions, according to notions of fairness or to legal constraints. If the feature is changed, then, again, the metric should say that we remain close to the starting point. If a feature is hard to change and might legitimately enter the decision, by contrast, then changes to this feature should imply that we move far away from the starting point. These considerations illustrate that there are many judgment calls to be made in practice when trying to answer the question  why  a specific decision was made. 
 Explaining Decision Problems 
 We have discussed explanations of decision functions and explanations of decisions. Explanations of decision functions are useful for AI engineers who want to understand how their algorithms work and how their algorithms might fail. Explanations of decisions are useful for those affected, who might want to contest decisions or might change their own behavior to get different decisions. 
 For democratic governance of AI, however, we need to understand decision problems, rather than decision functions or individual decisions. We need to understand what it is that AI systems are maximizing, and based on what information. This is necessary so we can know whether the objectives of the AI system align with those of society at large. 
 Recall the concept of  identification , which was discussed in  chapter 5 . There I argued that it often does not matter what machine algorithm is used to solve some decision problem. The answers given by different algorithms mostly agree if the algorithms have been trained on enough data. For supervised learning, for instance, there is a best-possible prediction of the outcome  Y  given features  X . All the different  machine learning methods should eventually learn to make this best-possible prediction, after sufficient training. 
 For democratic control of an AI system, we need to understand what this AI system is doing. Different machine learning methods will tend to do the same thing; they will give the same solution for a decision problem. We therefore often do not need to understand the technical details of which machine learning method was used or the details of how it solves the decision problem. What we do need to understand instead is the decision problem itself. What is the objective that the algorithm tries to maximize? Based on what data? What are the options from which the algorithm chooses when making decisions? 
 This is good news for democratic control. The technical details of machine learning methods are often hard to understand. The theoretical understanding, even of AI researchers, regarding why and how deep neural nets and transformers work is still limited. Some of the best mathematicians and theoretical physicists are currently trying to make progress on these questions. If a theoretical understanding of how deep learning works were necessary for democratic control, our prospects would be bleak. But such a theoretical understanding is not necessary. What is necessary is a broadly shared understanding and debate about what it is that the AI algorithms are maximizing, and over what set of possible decisions. 
 An algorithm informing student admissions for public universities might aim to admit the students with the highest predicted exam performance. Is this the right objective? Should the algorithm instead aim to increase social mobility across generations? Should it aim to increase demographic diversity among admitted students? Should it select on criteria of merit other than test performance? And is selecting a fixed number of students to be admitted the right set of policies to consider? Rather than reallocating a limited number of admissions slots, should it increase the total number of students admitted? 
 An algorithm selecting posts to display in a social media feed  might aim to maximize the time that users spend scrolling through their feed, to maximize the number of ads that these users are exposed to. The most effective way to maximize user engagement might be to promote polarizing and emotional posts and clickbait. Is maximizing ad clicks the right objective? Should the algorithm instead select informative posts that promote democratic debate? Should it discourage the chase of likes and retweets, which comes at the expense of deeper interactions? And who should decide what is informative, what promotes democratic debate, or what promotes deeper social interactions? 
 An algorithm setting individualized prices for an online retailer might set prices to maximize seller profits. It might do so by predicting the value of each consumer’s willingness and ability to pay and then setting the price equal to this value. Should the algorithm instead set prices to maximize consumer welfare? 
 A chatbot algorithm based on a large language model might give answers corresponding to the most likely sequence of words that might follow a given question. The algorithm might do so by predicting responses in online discussion forums on the internet. Is this the right prediction target? Does this end up reproducing the bigotry of online forums? What should be the right prediction target? And who gets to decide that? 
 In all these examples, there is room for reasonable disagreement. To have reasoned disagreements about what it is that the algorithms should maximize, we first need to have explanations of what they are currently maximizing. How do corporations and other actors define the reward that is maximized by their algorithms? These explanations, then, allow for public deliberation and processes of democratic decision-making. Even if explanations of machine learning methods, such as deep neural nets, are complicated, explanations of decision problems are not. The choice of AI objectives does not need to be left to experts and technocrats. Informed democratic governance is possible.

 Old Problems, New Challenges 
 Public discussions of AI can often seem quite breathless. New innovations are announced daily, a constant stream of research papers introduces new ideas, corporate events publicize breakthroughs for new benchmarks, regulators scramble to keep up, and new legal initiatives move forward. And yet, many of the questions regarding how to build AI, and how to govern it, are the same questions that humanity has grappled with for millennia: How can we learn? How should we act in the world? What makes a good society? 
 In addition to the hype and excitement around the breakthroughs of the day, these ancient questions are what make AI and its governance such a fascinating and multifaceted topic. I hope that this book has conveyed that these ancient questions matter for AI, and that AI can in turn shed new light on these questions. Given the pace of developments in AI, some of what has been said in this book will inevitably appear outdated in the not-so-distant future. But I am certain that these fundamental questions will stay with us, and I hope that the same is true for many of the ideas discussed here. What follows is brief recap of the fundamental ideas in this book, followed by some discussion regarding the possible future of democratic governance of AI.

 The Ancient Questions Behind AI 
 All of AI is about solving decision problems. To solve a decision problem, one needs to maximize a measurable objective by choosing actions based on whatever information is available. This information can take the form of knowledge that is taken to be true a priori. This information can also take the form of experience, or data. By learning from experience, we can hope to make better decisions in the future. 
 The leading approach to AI of the last two decades has been machine learning. Machine learning algorithms are trained on data and learn to make decisions based on statistical inference. Because the goal of AI is to solve decision problems, the notion of learning in AI is a fundamentally  pragmatic  one: The goal is not to recover some deeper  truth  about the world but instead to  act  successfully in it. 
 To do so, one needs to make accurate predictions about the world. This is the old human dream of divining the future. It is also one of the goals of science. To achieve this goal, one might look for patterns in past data and then make predictions, assuming that the same patterns continue to hold in the future. 
 Accurate predictions are also the goal of supervised learning in AI. Supervised learning involves an automated search for patterns in data. In the search for such patterns, we face the twin dangers of overfitting and underfitting. Algorithms that are overfitting superstitiously  extrapolate random patterns from the data into the future. Algorithms that are underfitting refuse to recognize patterns that are in fact consistently present. These same problems are encountered by humans when they learn, from little children coming to terms with the principle of gravity by dropping food to scientists in modern academia. 
 The ground truth for successful learning is the ability to predict new observations. Sample splitting can be used to check predictive success on a holdout sample that is not used during training. Machine learning algorithms can successfully navigate the trade-off between overfitting and underfitting by fine-tuning themselves to maximize their success at predicting new observations in such holdout samples. 
 To act successfully in the world, one also needs to experiment, to learn about the effect of actions that one might take in the future. We should not always do what we believe to be best. We need to occasionally instead try new options of uncertain payoff, to be able to make better decisions in the future. This, again, is equally true for babies who drop food from the dinner table as much as it is true for experimental physicists who smash together elementary particles in a particle accelerator at extremely high energies. 
 The need to experiment also applies to machine learning algorithms. Like humans, algorithms face a trade-off between exploration and exploitation. Good machine learning algorithms need to carefully balance how much they explore, by experimenting, and how much they exploit the knowledge that they have acquired from past experimentation. 
 To act successfully in the world, by solving decision problems, one needs to optimize measurable objectives. If problems appear, it is thus natural to worry whether the system failed to optimize its objective. Engineers in general, and computer scientists in particular, are very good at optimization, and at detecting optimization failures. The concerns of social scientists, on the other hand, typically  revolve around the fact that we live in a society. In a society, there is more than one person and more than one objective that needs to be maximized. This leads to fundamental normative and positive questions: What is the objective that should be maximized from a social perspective? Who chooses this objective in practice? How can we change this objective, if the objectives of those in control do not agree with the objectives of society at large? What role does control of resources play in determining the objective, and what role do ideas and ideologies play? 
 What makes a good and well-ordered society is again a question that has been debated since antiquity. Modern versions of this question ask about the welfare of the people living in a society, and about social welfare. To talk about social welfare, we need to answer several questions: Who are the people who matter? How do we trade off welfare across different people? How do we define the welfare of these people? Typical answers to the first question have become more encompassing over time: The set of people whose lives matter has increased. Regarding the trade-off across people, many would agree that we should care more about improving the well-being of those who are worse off, relative to those who are already doing well. As for the third question, economists typically define the welfare of individuals based on individual choices: Whatever an individual would choose, between any pair of options, is what gives them higher welfare. Once again, these considerations all apply to AI. To ask whether some application of AI is good, or whether some regulation of AI is beneficial, we need to ask about the consequences for social welfare of the application or regulation. 
 Social welfare might be harmed if AI algorithms fail to successfully optimize their objectives. Social welfare might also be harmed if the objective maximized by AI algorithms is distinct from social welfare. There is a range of reasons why algorithmic objectives and social welfare might diverge. Many of these reasons are familiar from more traditional economic settings. 
 There is, next, the issue of  agency problems  and measurability. In many economic and social settings, we depend on the actions of others, from teachers, who oversee education, to CEOs, who run companies. To align goals, contracts for teachers or CEOs might specify incentives, such as performance pay. Such incentive programs often backfire, however, because key aspects of performance are not measurable. Similar issues are encountered in AI. The design of rewards to guide algorithms is often hampered by the fact that not all relevant aspects of performance are measurable and quantifiable. 
 There is the issue of unequal distribution of control within society, and of conflicting interests. The objectives of AI are controlled by those who are in control of the means of prediction, which include data, computational infrastructure, technical expertise, and energy. If the objectives of those who are in control conflict with the objectives of the rest of society, then there is a problem. This problem can only by addressed by redistributing effective control. 
 Control over AI is acquired by acquiring control over the means of prediction, especially data. Often, the data concern individual data subjects. These individuals might be able to decide whether to share their personal data. The decision whether to share data generates pervasive externalities. The reason for these externalities is that machine learning is all about patterns across individuals, rather than about individual observations. Individual property rights over data, and differentially private data collection, cannot prevent the concentration of control over the means of prediction. Collective governance mechanisms are needed. 
 One important domain where choices over the objectives of AI matter is the workplace, which in turn has ramifications for the labor market. New technologies in general, and AI in particular, can be used in different ways. AI can be used to augment human capabilities, thereby increasing marginal productivity, the demand for labor, and general prosperity. AI can also be used to automate away human  labor, leading to rising corporate profits accompanied by a fall in wages and growth without shared prosperity. Which of these scenarios happens depends on who exerts effective control over technological decisions. 
 To evaluate the impact of automated decisions, we need to take a stance on questions of fairness and inequality. Standard definitions of algorithmic fairness or bias define bias as an optimization error, or failure to maximize profit. An alternative and better approach requires us to inquire after the impact of AI on welfare and on inequality across people. 
 Democratic control of AI requires public deliberation. Public deliberation requires a shared understanding of how AI works. This raises the issue of explainability. Explainability is a precondition for democratic control. Fortunately, key aspects of AI can be explained without explaining the technical details of implementation. The reason is that, with enough data, different approaches to solving a decision problem will converge to the same solution. What really matters is the decision problem itself—what the objective is, what the actions an algorithm chooses from are, and what information they are based on. These aspects of a decision problem need to be the focus of public deliberation over AI. 
 The preceding brief review illustrates how many of the problems and ideas concerning AI are, in fact, ancient problems and ideas. These problems and ideas have long been debated by humanity, in some cases for millennia: How to learn and act in the world, how to navigate the tension between superstition and stubbornness, and how to navigate the tension between doing what seems best and exploring new options. How to evaluate what makes a good society, how societal decisions are determined by control over resources and by ideologies, and how to redistribute control. How agency problems arise from limited observability, how conflicts of interest matter for collective decision-making, how externalities undermine decentralized  decision-making, how technology impacts inequality, and how democratic decisions need to be based on the ability to publicly deliberate. 
 This review also drives home that the key problem in the governance of AI is how to align the objectives maximized by AI systems with the objectives of society at large—that is, social welfare.

 Toward Democratic Control of the Means of Prediction 
 How can we align the objectives of AI with the objectives of society? We could try to ask those who build AI to be nice, and to encourage ethical behavior of AI engineers. In the context of profit-maximizing corporations, however, the leeway of engineers in determining AI objectives is quite limited. An alternative would be to redistribute control. We could try to give those who are affected by AI a say in the choice of objectives for AI. This alternative leads us to the idea of democratic self-rule, where decisions over AI are collectively made by those who have a stake in these decisions. 
 It will be difficult to organize this kind of democratic self-rule. There is a long road from the normative ideal of democratic self-governance to an actual implementation of a working institutional framework, where AI is indeed democratically governed. This book will not be able to solve this problem. But we can nonetheless conclude with some thoughts that might point the way. 
 When prompted to think about democracy, most of us will think of familiar institutions. Many will first think of representative democracy at the level of nation states, where professional politicians are elected every few years, and where governance is mediated by these politicians, and by further layers of bureaucracy or technocrats. We might also think of direct democracy, where ballot propositions are put to a general vote, and where all adult citizens get to  make a yes–no decision on a set of policy options. Direct democracy is often exercised at the level of nation states, or at the level of component states (e.g., California in the United States). 
 Both representative democracy and direct democracy are limited in important ways. They are centered around the nation state and its citizens. They are restricted to a sphere that is considered political, as opposed to the spheres of the economy or society. They are based on a separation between politicians and voters. They focus on voting rather than deliberation. 
 There are alternatives to these forms of democracy. We could consider democracy at geographical levels below the nation state or above, at institutional levels such as workplaces or schools, at the level of groups of interest that are not geographically defined. We could go beyond the sphere of the political, narrowly defined, and expand democratic decision-making to spheres now dominated by plutocratic decision-making, such as companies. We could consider institutional arrangements where politicians are not a separate class of people that is distinct from voters. We could emphasize institutions where deliberation takes place, rather than focusing only on systems for voting. 
 Representative democracy and direct democracy are not the only games in town. There are many other possibilities for how collective democratic decision-making can be organized. Some of these possibilities have a long history. One of these other possibilities has been called  open democracy  in a recent book by the political scientist Hélène Landemore. A key element of open democracy is known as  sortition . Sortition involves the random selection of a set of representatives from a population. The selection procedure might be designed to guarantee that this random set is demographically similar to the overall population. Such selection procedures are known in statistics as  stratified sampling . The randomly selected set of representatives is then asked to take time off from their regular daily life to deliberate on an important question and to ultimately reach a joint  decision on this question. These random representatives might be compensated for the time and effort required. 
 This procedure might sound familiar. The principle of sortition is alive in the idea of a  jury of peers  in the US criminal justice system. But sortition has a much longer history, going back all the way to political decision-making in ancient Athens. There are many attractive features of such a system. Sortition avoids the need for a class of professional politicians who compete for votes, with all the familiar problems of representative democracy that this brings. Sortition also avoids the high demands on time and knowledge of all citizens that direct democracy entails, if voters must decide on many issues that they are not necessarily well informed about. And sortition allows for the possibility for careful deliberation among the randomly selected representatives, and for the possibility of achieving consensus, since it is not simply a system for voting. 
 Another alternative to conventional representative democracy is known as  liquid democracy . Liquid democracy combines elements of direct democracy and representative democracy in a flexible way. In liquid democracy, as in direct democracy, everyone has an equal vote on all policy questions. In contrast to direct democracy, however, liquid democracy allows everyone to delegate their vote to any other person. Delegation of votes can be revoked at any time, and delegation can be specific to a policy or policy area. The delegated vote can in turn be passed on further to a third person, and so on. In liquid democracy, everyone is both a voter and a potential delegate, voting on other people’s behalf, but possibly only in some domains for which they have expertise. The barriers to becoming a delegate are much lower than in typical representative democracy. No minimum number of votes is required to become a delegate. Delegation is also not subject to the election cycles familiar from representative democracy, since votes may be delegated or revoked at any time. A version of liquid democracy was first devised in the nineteenth century by Charles Dodgson, who is better known as Lewis Carroll,  the author of  Alice’s Adventures in Wonderland . This system became feasible in practice with the spread of the internet. It can draw on tools such as the free software LiquidFeedback, which also incorporates collectively moderated forums for deliberation. Such tools have been adopted by pirate parties in various European countries (parties that are focused on issues around information freedom and digital rights), among others. 
 One key issue that plagues democracy in practice is that democratic deliberation and decision-making requires time, resources, and effort, especially when we move beyond the standard model of representative democracy. We might consider ideas such as basic-income or job-guarantee policies to facilitate participation, while providing a social safety net. Democratic deliberation could be supported by a basic income that is available to everyone, conditional only on participation. Such a policy could be combined with variants of direct democracy, sortition, or liquid democracy. As proposed by Chris Muller and Suresh Naidu in correspondence we had in October 2024, it could also be institutionalized in the form of “democracy Fridays,” where one day per week is dedicated to participation in collective decision-making, supported by a basic income. 
 Other systems are conceivable, beyond those sketched here. The larger point is that democratic governance can take many forms. We should not let our imagination be limited by the failings of present Western representative democracies. Examples from the sortition systems of ancient Athens to the online constitutional deliberations of modern Iceland to the experiments in liquid democracy of internet activists show that alternatives are possible. And we should not constrain ourselves to thinking of democracy as restricted to the state. As argued by authors such as the political philosopher Elizabeth Anderson and the sociologist Isabelle Ferreras, realizing the promise of democracy might require extending it to the sphere of corporate decision-making and the workplace. 
 In whichever ways democratic deliberation and democratic  decision-making will be organized in the future, real democracy will require a redistribution of power. “Democratic participation” that is reduced to a mere consulting role, say in the context of participatory design of AI tools, is bound to fail. Without real power, whenever democratic input conflicts with the interests of those in control, it will be overruled. And whenever it is to be expected that democratic input will be overruled, democratic participation will dwindle, since few people will be willing to engage in a spectacle of democracy without consequence. 
 The task, then, for the years to come is to create institutions and societies where power is shared by those impacted by AI, and where decisions about AI are made democratically and after public deliberation. Creating the intellectual, political, and legal foundations for this will not be easy. But it will be worth the effort.
