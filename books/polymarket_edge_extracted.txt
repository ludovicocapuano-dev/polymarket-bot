This makes the process faster and cheaper, as there’s no need for intermediaries to manage or validate the transaction. Smart contracts ensure that all parties involved follow the rules set out in the code. Once the contract is created, it can’t be changed, reducing the risk of fraud or manipulation. The result is a more secure and efficient way of conducting transactions, especially in environments like blockchain, where trust in a central authority is not required.

Each vote can be recorded as a transaction on the blockchain, ensuring that it cannot be tampered with while maintaining voter anonymity. Several pilot projects around the world have explored blockchain-based voting systems as a way to enhance electoral integrity. 
 Furthermore, blockchain is being explored in areas such as intellectual property rights, digital identity verification, and even charitable donations. By providing a transparent and verifiable record, blockchain can help artists and creators protect their work, streamline identity verification processes, and ensure that charitable contributions reach their intended recipients without interference from corrupt intermediaries.

These markets offer a unique way for people to make money by using their knowledge or predictions about future events. Because blockchain and crypto make everything quick, secure, and automatic, prediction markets are growing in popularity and offer new opportunities for traders and investors to profit from their insights. 
 As blockchain technology continues to evolve, its potential applications will expand even further.  Cryptocurrencies like Bitcoin and Ethereum operate on blockchain technology, which serves as the backbone of their decentralized structure. A blockchain functions as a transparent, tamper-resistant ledger where transactions are permanently  recorded. Because anyone can view and independently verify these records, trust is established without the need for a central governing body. For example, the Bitcoin blockchain maintains a comprehensive history of every transaction ever made, detailing each instance of bitcoin being sent or received. This decentralized approach eliminates the need for traditional intermediaries like banks or credit card companies, allowing value to be transferred directly between individuals over the internet. 
 By removing middlemen, blockchain technology empowers users with greater control, security, and freedom in managing their digital assets. 
 Imagine a global, open alternative to every financial service you use today, accessible with little more than a smartphone and internet connection. What is a Blockchain? At its core, a blockchain is a digital ledger, like an online notebook, used to record transactions or data in a way that is secure, transparent, and tamper-proof. Unlike traditional ledgers maintained by a central authority (like a bank), a blockchain is decentralized, meaning it is managed by a network of computers that work together to verify and maintain its records. 
 The term “blockchain” can be understood by breaking it into its two components: “block” and “chain.” A block is essentially a collection of data, typically containing information about transactions. Each block also has a unique identifier known as a hash, along with the hash of the previous block, which links it to the next one. These blocks are connected in chronological order, forming a chain. This design makes it extremely difficult  to alter a block, as doing so would require changing every subsequent block in the chain—a task that demands enormous computational power. 
 What makes blockchain special is how it addresses three significant issues found in traditional systems: trust, security, and transparency. First, it is decentralized, meaning no single person or organization controls it. Instead, the blockchain is maintained by a network of participants, known as nodes. Second, it is highly transparent, as every participant can view the transactions recorded on the blockchain, fostering trust because nothing is hidden. Third, blockchain ensures immutability; once a transaction is added, it cannot be altered, making the data permanent and tamper-proof. Finally, blockchain is secure, as transactions are verified using advanced cryptography, making it incredibly difficult for hackers to manipulate the system. 
 Here’s how blockchain works: When someone initiates a transaction, such as Alice wanting to send money to Bob, the details of that transaction are broadcast to the entire network. All participating computers, known as nodes, receive this information. These nodes then work together to validate the transaction using specific processes called consensus mechanisms, such as Proof of Work or Proof of Stake. Once the transaction is confirmed as legitimate, it is grouped with other transactions into a block. This block is then added to the chain, linking it to the previous block. At this point, the blockchain updates across all nodes, ensuring every participant has an identical and up-to-date record. 
 Proof of Work (PoW) is like a race to solve a really hard puzzle. Imagine a group of people trying to solve a complicated math problem. The first one to solve it gets a reward. This is how Bitcoin and some other blockchains work. Computers compete  to solve these problems, and the winner gets to add a new block to the blockchain and earn rewards. The system is very secure and hard to cheat, but it uses a lot of electricity and can be slow. Proof of Stake (PoS) is more like a lottery. Instead of solving hard puzzles, people lock up some of their coins, a process called staking. The system then randomly picks someone to add the next block. The more coins someone stakes, the higher their chances of being chosen. This method uses much less energy and allows for faster transactions, but it can favor people who already have a lot of coins. In summary, Proof of Work is based on solving difficult puzzles that require a lot of energy, while Proof of Stake selects validators based on how many coins they are willing to lock up. Both methods help keep blockchains secure, but Proof of Stake is becoming more popular because it is more efficient. Platforms like Polymarket, which use blockchain technology for decentralized prediction markets, rely on PoS to ensure fair and transparent transactions. 
 The blockchain has several key features that make it unique. It operates as a distributed ledger, meaning every participant has a copy of the entire system, ensuring transparency. Transactions are protected through cryptographic security, using advanced algorithms to safeguard the data. Additionally, the network relies on consensus mechanisms to agree on the validity of transactions without needing a central authority, making the system both decentralized and trustworthy. 
 In essence, blockchain is not just a technological innovation it’s a new way of building trust in a digital world. 
 Blockchain needs cryptocurrency to function effectively for a  few key reasons. First, cryptocurrency acts as an incentive mechanism. Since blockchain networks are decentralized and there is no central authority to oversee transactions, participants—known as miners or validators—are rewarded with cryptocurrency for verifying and adding new blocks of transactions to the blockchain. This system encourages people to keep the network secure and running smoothly. Second, cryptocurrencies provide a means of exchanging value within a blockchain network. For example, in a blockchain-based application or a prediction market, users need a way to buy and sell assets or place bets. Cryptocurrency allows for fast, secure, and borderless transactions without needing a third party like a bank. Finally, the use of cryptocurrency ensures the blockchain remains decentralized. Traditional currencies are controlled by governments and financial institutions, but cryptocurrency isn’t owned or regulated by anyone. This decentralization is one of the core strengths of blockchain, as it provides users with more control over their transactions and reduces the risk of manipulation or censorship.

 High gas fees on the Ethereum network have driven the rise in popularity of Layer 2 (L2) scaling solutions like Polygon. Gas fees are transaction costs required to process operations on Ethereum, and they fluctuate based on network congestion. As Ethereum gained widespread adoption for decentralized finance (DeFi), non-fungible tokens (NFTs), and smart contracts, the network became increasingly congested, causing transaction fees to spike dramatically.  Layer 2 solutions like Polygon address this issue by processing transactions off the main Ethereum blockchain while still leveraging its security and decentralization. Ethereum’s roadmap includes upgrades like sharding and the transition to Ethereum 2.0, but these improvements take time to implement. As  a result, Layer 2 adoption continues to grow, helping Ethereum remain a dominant force in the blockchain space.

 Win by Design 
 Statistics can sometimes feel intimidating—full of formulas, complex graphs, and technical jargon. But don’t worry, you don’t need to be a math expert to follow along. In this chapter on expected value, I’m going to break things down into simple, practical insights that anyone can understand. My goal is to make these concepts approachable, engaging, and—most importantly—useful for making smarter decisions on Polymarket. 
 Think of this as a guided journey rather than a technical deep dive. We’ll start with intuitive concepts, using relatable examples and clear explanations to show how expected value works in real-life scenarios. Whether you’re completely new to statistics or just need a fresh perspective, I’ll make sure the path is easy to navigate. By the end of this chapter, you’ll not only understand the concept of expected value but also feel confident  in applying it to your Polymarket strategies and everyday decision-making. 
 Numbers and calculations often feel certain and predictable—just like managing your salary and savings. But life is rarely that straightforward. Let’s start with a simple scenario. Imagine you work for a company that pays you a fixed salary of $7,500 every month. Your total monthly expenses amount to $2,500, and you put the remaining $5,000 into your personal bank account. Also, assume you start with exactly $0 in your account on January 1st. A quick calculation tells us that after 12 months, you will have saved: 5,000×12=60,0005,000 \times 12 = 60,0005,000×12=60,000. Simple, right? 
 This kind of certainty is comforting. But what if your salary wasn’t fixed? What if unexpected expenses arose? Suddenly, the final amount in your account wouldn’t be a straightforward calculation—it would be a matter of probability. That’s what we’re going to explore next. Probability helps us navigate uncertainty, make better decisions, and understand the world in a more realistic way. Don’t worry—we’ll take it step by step, keeping things clear and approachable. 
 Now, let’s add a twist to our example. Your salary and monthly expenses remain the same, but now you also receive a variable bonus each month. This bonus isn’t fixed—it depends on market factors beyond your control. Because of this uncertainty, the final amount in your account is no longer a simple, fixed number but instead follows a probabilistic pattern. (If you need a quick refresher on probabilities, check out my introductory post.) To keep things simple, let’s assume the following probabilities for  your monthly bonus, where each month’s bonus is independent of the others: 
 $1,000 with a probability of 0.6 $500 with a probability of 0.3 $0 with a probability of 0.1 
 Now, let’s revisit our original question: Given these conditions, how much do you expect to have in your account after 12 months of work? It’s easy to calculate the extremes. The absolute minimum is $60,000 (if you receive no bonuses at all), while the maximum is $72,000 (if you get the highest bonus every single month—nice!). However, both of these extremes are unlikely—most of the time, your actual savings will fall somewhere in between. But is there a specific value within this range that stands out? A number that you should expect more than the others? Yes, there is. And it’s called… the expected value. 
 The expected bonus value for 1 month is: 1000*0.6 + 500*0.3 + 0 = 750. Then, you multiply this by 12 to get the expected bonus amount for 12 months. By the end of the year, the expected value of your bonuses is $9,000. See, it wasn’t hard at all.

 Imagine now you have  100 USDC  and want to place a YES bet on Polymarket. Your strategy is straightforward: buy when the probability is  X% , hold until it reaches  100%  to secure a win, or sell at a loss if it falls to  X - Y% . The real challenge is figuring  out the best values for  X  and  Y  that maximize your returns over time. 
 Design your strategy to maximize average returns over multiple trades. 
 This isn’t about a single bet—it’s about playing the odds like a professional. Just as a casino thrives by consistently applying mathematical edge over thousands of hands, you want to structure your bets so that, over many trades, your average return is as high as possible. If your  X  is too high, you risk low payouts. If  Y  is too wide, you take bigger losses. Too tight, and you might sell prematurely before the price rebounds. 
 The key lies in  expected value (EV) — a statistical concept that helps traders determine if, on average, they should make money by taking a large number of bets. Much like a fair coin should land on heads  50% of the time , optimal bet sizing and risk management ensure that even with occasional losses, your gains compound over time. So, what’s the sweet spot? Let’s break it down. 
 
 The EV formula seems tough, but it’s just a simple math tool for better decision-making. 
 EV = (Probability of Winning × Amount Won) + (Probability of Losing × Amount Lost) 
 Let’s say X = 30% and Y = 10%. This means you buy shares when the probability is at 30%, and if it drops to 20%, you cut your losses. If the probability reaches 100%, you win the full payout. Since each share costs 0.30 USDC, you can buy 333 shares with 100 USDC. If you win, each share becomes worth 1 USDC, so you get 333 USDC, making a profit of 233 USDC. If you lose and the price drops to 0.20, you sell your 333 shares for 66.7 USDC, meaning you lost 33.3 USDC. 
 To calculate the expected return, we multiply each outcome by its probability. If the chance of winning is 30%, the formula is (profit if win × probability of winning) + (loss if lose × probability of losing).
